Abstract 

Comprehensive understanding of time series remains a significant challenge for Large Language Models (LLMs). Current research is hindered by fragmented task definitions and benchmarks with inherent ambiguities, precluding rigorous evaluation and the develop-ment of unified Time Series Reasoning Models (TSRMs). To bridge this gap, we formalize Time Series Reasoning (TSR) via a four-level taxonomy of increasing cognitive complexity. We introduce H ITSR, a hi erarchical time 

series reasoning dataset comprising 83k samples with diverse task combinations and verified Chain-of-Thought (CoT) trajectories. Leveraging H ITSR, we propose LL ATISA, a strong TSRM that integrates visualized patterns with precision-calibrated numerical tables to enhance the temporal perception of Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Through a multi-stage curriculum fine-tuning strategy, LL ATISA achieves superior performance and exhibits robust out-of-distribution gen-eralization across diverse TSR tasks and real-world scenarios. Our code is available at https://github.com/RainingNovember/LLaTiSA. 

1 Introduction 

Time series data serve as a fundamental modal-ity for capturing the temporal dynamics of com-plex systems, underpinning a wide range of critical applications, including medical diagnostics (Xie et al., 2025a), financial valuation (Xu et al., 2025), and industrial condition monitoring (Wang et al., 2025b). Interpreting real-world temporal dynamics necessitates Time Series Reasoning (TSR): deriv-ing a comprehensive understanding of time series by grounding analysis in numerical evidence, per-ceiving salient patterns, and incorporating contex-tual information to support challenging tasks such as decision-making or forecasting.

Recent studies have demonstrated that Large Language Models (LLMs) can be effective for time-series understanding (Xie et al., 2025b) and provide interpretable reasoning to further enrich traditional time series analysis (Zhang et al., 2025a). Despite these advancements, building unified TSRMs faces two critical bottlenecks: (1) The lack of a for-malized taxonomy for reasoning tasks. Exist-ing suites like TSQA (Kong et al., 2025) or BED-Time (Sen et al., 2025) either present a mapping of traditional analytical tasks (e.g., forecasting and classification) to text formats or focus on realiz-ing scenario-agnostic series patterns. In contrast, domain-specific datasets like Engine-QA (Wang et al., 2025b) and Heartcare-Bench (Xie et al., 2025a) tend to be numerically-insensitive, primar-ily relying on the semantic relationships between multiple series. (2) Reliability deficits in exist-ing benchmarks. Beyond insufficient contextual information, current benchmarks are often ham-pered by semantic ambiguities in label definitions and low-fidelity question-answer pairs (Guan et al., 2025). To facilitate the systematic development and rigorous assessment of TSRMs, it is essential to formalize unified definitions of TSR and con-struct reliable benchmarks that comprehensively model capabilities across diverse scenarios. Building on the aforementioned limitations, we posit that cultivating reliable TSR capabilities mir-rors a multi-stage cognitive process: transitioning from point-level numerical grounding to series-level perception, facilitating high-level semantic in-terpretation, and ultimately enabling context-aware generation. Guided by this philosophy, we for-malize a systematic taxonomy that decomposes TSR into four levels of increasing complexity (L1-L4), as shown in Figure 1(a). 2 Related Works 

2.1 Time Series Reasoning Datasets 

Recently released multimodal time series datasets adopt various types of TSR tasks. For general question-answering, early efforts like TSQA (Kong et al., 2025) and TRQA (Jing et al., 2025) ex-plore LLMs’ potential in question-answering and traditional time series analysis tasks. SciTS (Wu et al., 2025) introduces a benchmark specifically designed for scientific time series analysis across a wide range of scientific domains. RATs40k (Yang et al., 2025b) transforms classical time se-ries anomaly detection from discriminative into a reasoning-intensive task and annotates samples with explanatory reasoning. TimeMMD (Liu et al., 2024a)is tailored for the context-aware time series forecasting task. MMTS-Bench (Yin et al., 2026) provides a multi-dimensional task classification tax-onomy, yet it overlooks evaluating models’ basic numerical read-out and combining diverse seman-tics of real scenarios. In addition to insufficient con-text, current benchmarks are further hampered by semantic ambiguities in label definitions and low-fidelity question-answer pairs (Guan et al., 2025). Moreover, most of the datasets focus on either pat-tern description or complex reasoning scenarios such as forecasting, and overlook evaluating mod-els’ basic ability of read-out numerical values. Our findings suggest that current models even struggle with finding point-level numerical evidence, suffer-ing from overthinking or hallucinations with longer time horizons. 

2.2 Time Series Reasoning Models 

Understanding time series via language models generally mostly follows three encoding paradigms: (i) direct serialization into text tokens, (ii) visual rendering as plots for VLMs, and (iii) the integra-tion of dedicated time-series encoders to construct Time-Series Multimodal Large Language Models (TS-MLLMs). TimeOmni-1 (Guan et al., 2025) and Time-R1 (Luo et al., 2025) employ textual in-puts for context-aware forecasting. ChatTS (Xie et al., 2025b) and ITFormer (Wang et al., 2025b) incorporate an MLP-based and a Q-former (Li et al., 2023) style time series encoder to perform multivariate TSR tasks, respectively. TimeMaster (Zhang et al., 2025a) enables VLMs to perform in-terpretable classification based on visualized time series. While recent studies point out that TSRMs struggle with basic TSR tasks like description dif-ferentiation (Sen et al., 2025), VLMs have demon-strated superior robustness compared to their LLM and TS-MLLM counterparts. This observation mo-tivates us to further unlock the potential of VLMs for general-purpose TSR scenarios. 

3 Method 

3.1 Time Series Reasoning Taxonomy 

We argue that reliable TSR is inherently composi-tional, necessitating a hierarchy that can: (i) faith-fully ground observations to timestamps, (ii) ab-stract salient temporal patterns beyond point-level evidence, and (iii) integrate with contextual knowl-edge to support semantic judgment and prediction. In contrast, prior TSR benchmarks are typically organized around isolated tasks and adopt hetero-geneous definitions of what constitutes “reason-ing”, often over-emphasizing a narrow subset of problem types (e.g., coarse pattern discrimination). Such fragmentation leads to two limitations. First, cross-benchmark comparability is compromised because mismatched capability sets and inconsis-tent task difficulties prevent a unified assessment of progress. Second, current TSRMs often attempt to perform high-level reasoning (e.g., forecasting) on an unsteady foundation: models still struggle with elementary grounding and verification, which undermines the credibility and interpretability of more complex reasoning. To address these limitations, we introduce a difficulty-stratified taxonomy that organizes TSR into progressively increasing levels of complex-ity. This framework serves as a unified schema for categorizing diverse TSR tasks and provides a diagnostic lens to pinpoint the specific cognitive boundaries of current models. Concretely, we de-compose TSR into four hierarchical levels: • L1: Numerical Read-out . Establish time-aware indexing and point-level numerical retrieval. • L2: Pattern Perception . Identify and differentiate multi-scale temporal patterns using quantitative evidence. • L3: Semantic Reasoning . Integrate time series observations with contextual knowledge to per-form domain-specific reasoning. • L4: Predictive Inference . Generate high-fidelity time-series predictions. Based on such a taxonomy, we summarize current benchmarks into different levels in Section A.2, posing a general standard to task definition and evaluation of TSRMs’ capabilities. 

3.2 HITSR Dataset 

Building on our four-level TSR taxonomy, we in-troduce H ITSR, a unified dataset for both training and evaluation of TSR capabilities across levels L1-L3. Data Sources. In total, the HITSR dataset comprises three hierarchical levels: H ITSR-L1 (30 ,000 samples), H ITSR-L2 ( 50 ,703 samples), and H ITSR-L3 ( 3,121 samples). Each subset in-cludes a high-fidelity test set rigorously verified by human annotators. For H ITSR-L1 and H ITSR-L2, our primary objective is to provide large-scale supervision that strengthens fundamental reason-ing abilities in a controlled yet diverse manner. We therefore generate H ITSR-L1 and H ITSR-L2 in-stances using synthetic time series sourced from (Xie et al., 2025b). This approach allows us to sys-tematically manipulate temporal structures and un-derlying patterns, while scaling the data to accom-modate various training-budget requirements with-out being restricted by the scarcity of real-world annotations. For H ITSR-L3, we curate publicly available real-world time series from diverse do-mains and construct question-answer pairs written by the LLM analyzer. The complete data synthe-sis pipeline and the summary of real-world data information are detailed in Section B. 

Task Formulation. Following the proposed tax-onomy, we instantiate a set of subtasks within each level to cover complementary facets of the TSR as shown in Section C.1. L1 tasks are formulated as short-answer questions, given that the ground truth can be generated via rule-based methods. Tasks within L2-L3 are structured as multiple-choice (MC) questions to ensure objective and repro-ducible evaluation. To maintain a strict instruction-following orientation and minimize biases from for-matting artifacts, each supervision target is crafted as a complete natural-language statement rather than a concise label. Detailed task definitions and additional examples are provided in Section C. Ad-ditionally, we adopt different answer-construction protocols tailored to the degree of output determi-nacy. For L1 and the Numerical Perception subtask in L2, the correct option can be derived by explicit rules from the underlying series; we therefore gen-erate options programmatically under fixed tem-plates. For the two Pattern Differentiation subtasks in L2 and the L3 Semantic Understanding task, the outputs are descriptive and require higher-level interpretation. We construct these items with an LLM-assisted pipeline and subsequently verify by humans to ensure correctness and remove ambigu-ous cases. 

Multi-Stage Verified CoT Annotation. We adopt distinct annotation and verification pipelines for L2 and L3 tasks. For L2 Pattern Differentia-tion , we first prompt GPT-5 with each time series’ visual plot and key statistics (e.g., extreme value-index pairs, spike locations with precise values, and notable local events) to produce numerically aware descriptions of local and global patterns. We then construct multiple-choice questions by sam-pling distractor options from descriptions of other series. To ensure reliability, we employ GPT-5 to cross-validate each question, removing ambigu-ous descriptions and flawed items (e.g., those with non-unique correct options). Finally, we hold out 10% of the validated pool for evaluation. This subset undergoes a comprehensive human audit-ing, and only items that pass this rigorous review are retained, yielding a unambiguous test set. For L3 Semantic Understanding , we leverage LLMs to generate scenario-aware multiple-choice ques-tions by prompting them with real-world time se-ries and their associated metadata. Each scenario description integrates pattern recognition and nu-merical grounding, effectively encapsulating the TSRMs’ capabilities across L1-L3. Each item is subsequently dual-checked by GPT-5 and then re-viewed by humans to comprehensively verify that the correct answer is supported by consistent nu-merical, pattern, and semantic evidence. 

3.3 LL ATISA 

Time series can be presented to language models in various formats (Sen et al., 2025). While purely visual plots effectively convey global shapes and local morphological cues, they often lack the fine-grained numerical precision required for grounded reasoning. Consequently, many TSR methods typi-cally represent time series as textualized numerical sequences (Guan et al., 2025; Kong et al., 2025) or integrate LLMs with dedicated time series encoders to develop TS-MLLMs (Xie et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2025b). Recent studies, however, indicate that TS-MLLMs frequently underperform relative to general-purpose LLMs or VLMs on L2 TSR tasks, while VLMs are stronger on pattern-centric tasks (e.g., local pattern differentiation) (Sen et al., 2025), underscoring the advantages of visual repre-sentations for L2 reasoning. To empower VLMs with precise numerical grounding, we propose LL ATISA (Figure 2.b), a dual-view input framework that pairs standard time series visualizations with a secondary image ren-dering the data as a structured index-value table. This design facilitates a progressive interpretation paradigm: the first plot enables macroscopic in-spection for overall perception, the image of the structured table enables point-accurate verification of specific indices or intervals. By synthesizing these dual modalities alongside textual context, the model produces semantically grounded reasoning. Empirically, compared to processing series as tex-tual arrays, LL ATISA provides extra index infor-mation, thereby significantly mitigating numeri-cal hallucinations and improving performance on numerical-sensitive tasks. Leveraging H ITSR, LL ATISA is trained via a three-stage curriculum designed to progressively acquire the prerequisite capabilities defined in our taxonomy. During the initial two stages, we per-form sequential Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on H ITSR-L1 and H ITSR-L2 to consolidate the model’s numerical read-out precision and pat-tern perception capabilities. In the third stage, LL ATISA could be either trained on H ITSR-L3 or general-purpose domain-specific TSR benchmarks. Our experiments further show that existing TSRMs often fail even on basic L1-L2 TSR, and struggle to combine LLM priors with series-level evidence for reliable L3 semantic inference. In contrast, curriculum-trained LL ATISA across L1-L2 could be easily transferred to domain-specific L3 tasks (e.g., ECG interpretation).

4.5 Ablation Study Effectiveness of CoT. In the H ITSR dataset, we incorporate LLM-annotated CoT data, verified by humans, to enhance LL ATISA’s performance and generalization, particularly on the more complex tasks at levels L2-L3. To isolate the impact of CoT, we perform SFT on the same dataset using only the correct options as supervision (“w/o CoT”). As shown in Table 4, while ID performance does not degrade significantly, the OOD performance de-teriorates markedly, with accuracy decreasing by 4.31% and 17.91% on L2 and L3, respectively. Fur-thermore, training solely on correct options signifi-cantly undermines the model’s original instruction-following capabilities. We observe that the model tuned without CoT data fails to generate reason-ing chains, even when explicitly prompted to do so. This phenomenon highlights the critical role of H ITSR as a thinking-aware TSR training re-source. By integrating high-quality CoT trajecto-ries, our benchmark not only incentivizes robust cross-task reasoning but also synergistically pre-serves the foundational generative alignment of the backbone model.

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose a difficulty-stratified view of TSR and formalize a four-level taxonomy (L1-L4) that characterizes progressive TSR capabilities. We introduce H ITSR, a large-scale hierarchical dataset with over 83k high-fidelity TSR questions spanning L1-L3, constructed with unambiguous labels and verifiable reasoning chains to support both reliable training and evaluation. Building on HITSR, we present LL ATISA, a VLM-based TSR model that pairs time series plots with an auxiliary index-value table to bridge qualitative perception and precise numerical evidence, and is trained via a three-stage curriculum aligned with L1-L3. Ex-tensive experiments on diverse OOD benchmarks and real-world scenarios show that LL ATISA con-sistently outperforms strong baselines and different modeling strategies, and transfers effectively to domain-specific semantic reasoning, suggesting a practical path toward more robust unified TSRMs. Limitations 

Our study primarily focuses on systematic dataset construction and supervised curriculum fine-tuning, leaving the exploration of Reinforcement Learning Fine-Tuning (RFT) on H ITSR as a future direc-tion. Effectively implementing RFT in this domain remains challenging due to the complexity of re-ward design, which must simultaneously supervise low-level numerical precision and high-level se-mantic logic across our L1-L4 taxonomy. Future work includes: (i) integrating RL into the hierarchi-cal curriculum to further refine the model’s policy; (ii) investigating robust initialization strategies to mitigate cold-start issues.

All datasets and language models used in this work are publicly available. The H ITSR dataset was constructed from well-established, publicly acces-sible time series benchmarks and synthetic data generation pipelines, and LL ATISA is built on the open-sourced model.

Appendix A Findings 

A.1 Model Analysis 

Our research is primarily motivated by a critical appraisal of existing TSR benchmarks. Preliminary evaluations of GPT-4o on local pattern differentia-tion tasks reveal significant deficiencies in handling foundational TSR operations, echoing the obser-vations in (Sen et al., 2025). As illustrated in Ta-ble 18, GPT-4o fails to perform accurate numerical grounding when interpreting a visual plot; it strug-gles to discern the precise relationship between start and end points or characterize the specific rate of change (e.g., confusing abrupt drops with "smooth" decreases). This diagnostic case under-scores a pivotal prerequisite: establishing robust numerical read-out capabilities for TSRMs is es-sential before tackling the higher-order reasoning required in L3 and L4 tasks. Furthermore, our em-pirical results across both ID (Table 15) and OOD (Table 1) scenarios demonstrate that current LLMs, VLMs, and even specialized TSRMs fall short in foundational L1-L2 tasks, which suggests a funda-mental perception-reasoning gap. 

A.2 Dataset Analysis 

We systematically categorize existing TSR datasets according to our proposed four-level taxonomy in Table 16, through which we identify several critical deficiencies in current benchmarks. For instance, certain questions exhibit label ambiguity, contain-ing multiple valid options (Tables 20 and 21), while others lack sufficient contextual information for logical reasoning (Table 22). Furthermore, most TSR datasets are designed exclusively for evalua-tion rather than providing comprehensive training-test splits. Among the limited training data avail-able, the absence of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) an-notations is a major drawback, while solely relying on correct labels for training tends to degrade the generative reasoning capabilities of TSRMs.

C.2 Data Verification L1: Numerical Read-out. The question-answer (QA) pairs utilized in this stage are completely constructed by strict rule-based annotations based on each time series’ attribution pool. 

L2: Pattern Perception. For numerical percep-tion, it is similar to L1, which generated the complete QA pairs through an explicit rule-based pipeline. The distractors are created by slightly perturbing the numerical evidence. For local pattern differentiation, distractors are synthesized using rule-based perturbations, such as numerical manipulation or random attribute sam-pling from heterogeneous series. To ensure la-bel uniqueness, we perform cross-verification by benchmarking each candidate option against the ground-truth attributes of the series. Subsequently, we use GPT-5 with both the metadata and visual plots of the given time series to annotate the correct answers with CoT reasoning. For quality assurance, the training set only includes samples correctly re-solved by GPT-5, while the test set undergoes rig-orous human verification to ensure high fidelity. Regarding global pattern differentiation, we first prompt GPT-5 to generate comprehensive descrip-tions of the series. Distractors are then constructed by incorporating annotations from disparate se-quences. Similarly, the original dataset is processed by GPT-5 to produce reasoning-enhanced ground truth, ensuring the depth and accuracy of the labels. 

L3: Semantic Understanding. The raw QA pairs are initially synthesized by GPT-5 in a sin-gle pass. To ensure data integrity, we implement a cross-validation stage where GPT-5 evaluates the generated pairs against a set of rigorous criteria. This evaluation focuses on two critical dimensions: (i) verifying the ground truth, ensuring that nu-merical evidence, pattern characterization, and se-mantic logic are technically sound; and (ii) audit-ing distractors, where each incorrect option is re-examined to eliminate potential overlaps or coun-terexamples that might inadvertently align with the time series. Finally, a manual dual-check is per-formed on the validated candidates to finalize a high-fidelity, real-world TSR dataset. C.3 Prompt Templates

C.5 Comparison to Existed TSR Datesets 

The differences among H ITSR and existing TSR datasets are summarized as follows: • The Enrichment of Verified Chain-of-Thought. 

Different from evaluation-only benchmarks (e.g., BEDTime (Sen et al., 2025), TimeMCQ2 (Mer-rill et al., 2024), and MMTS-Bench (Yin et al., 2026)), H ITSR provides 83k samples enriched with verified Chain-of-Thought (CoT) trajecto-ries. By transforming raw data into high-quality instruction-tuning pairs, we enable TSRMs to learn the "process of reasoning" rather than solely mapping correct answers. Our results (Section 4.5) show that this approach significantly en-hances the LL ATISA’s performance in complex reasoning tasks while preserving the model’s gen-eral instruction-following capabilities. • Unambiguous and Logic-Grounded CoT. In contrast to the datasets with noisy (see Table 13-14 in the manuscript) or purely semantic an-notations, our CoT follows a strict “Perception-to-Reasoning” logic (Numerical -> Pattern -> Semantic). Each step is cross-verified against ground-truth data, ensuring that the reasoning path is grounded in numerical reality rather than textual hallucination. • Emphasis on Foundational TSR Capabilities. 

Most existing studies (e.g., Time-MMD (Liu et al., 2024a), TSR-Suite (Guan et al., 2025)) focus on L2-L4 high-level TSR tasks but over-look the verification of a model’s numerical read-out ability, especially in long-horizon settings. HITSR uniquely introduces L1 tasks (precise grounding and localization) as a diagnostic an-chor. This ensures that complex TSR abilities are evaluated only after verifying the model’s foundational perceptual accuracy. • A clear difficulty-Stratified taxonomy for TSR. 

Guided by our 4-level taxonomy (grounded in Bloom’s Taxonomy (Bloom et al., 1956) and Bertin’s Levels of Reading (Monmonier, 1985)), HITSR acts as a systematic diagnostic frame-work. Unlike fragmented benchmarks, H ITSR’s stratified design allows for curriculum learning (L1 → L3) and enables researchers to pinpoint exactly where a TSRM’s reasoning chain breaks down. 

D Evaluation on the H ITSR 

We evaluate representative baseline models on the HITSR test set, comprising 100 randomly sampled questions, with results summarized in Table 15. Key observations are as follows: First, incorpo-rating temporal indices into textualized numerical arrays significantly enhances the reasoning perfor-mance of LLMs; for instance, GPT-4o’s L1 accu-racy improves from 6.0% to 23.0% with indices, particularly aiding numerical grounding tasks. Sec-ond, ChatTS demonstrates a strong capability to es-timate approximate values while being poor at map-ping the correct index for the target value, achiev-ing a competitive 70.0% in semantic understanding (L3), which is merely a 1.0% performance gap com-pared to the best-performing multimodal GPT-4o. A consistent performance disparity exists be-tween local and global pattern differentiation, re-vealing the limitations of single-modality reason-ing. While pure vision or text-only models excel in global perception (e.g., GPT-4o reaching 93.0% and 94.0% in L2-Global, respectively), they both struggle with local granularity, as evidenced by their stagnant performance in L2-Local (hovering around 55.0%). This bottleneck is exemplified in Table 18, where pure vision fails to discern pre-cise temporal transitions, such as confusing a sharp step-drop with a “smooth” decrease. Crucially, our results demonstrate a significant performance leap when Vision and Text modal-ities are integrated, with GPT-4o’s L2-Local ac-curacy rising to 69.0%. This synergistic gain highlights the effectiveness of a dual-stream in-put paradigm—utilizing visual plots for overall pattern recognition and textual sequences for nu-merical precision. These findings suggest that fu-ture TSRMs should prioritize the fusion of visual global context and numerical fine-grained features to overcome the inherent perceptual gaps of iso-lated modalities. For the “Numerical Perception task” in L2, we introduce subtle numerical perturbations into lo-cal pattern differentiation to synthesize challenging distractors. Experimental results in Table 15 show that Qwen3-VL-8B achieves the state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance of 78.0% when equipped with both vision and indexed textual series inputs. Be-sides, we observe a significant performance degra-dation for Qwen3-8B in the "Text (w/o index)" setting (36.0%); this is primarily due to its inef-ficient regressive value retrieval patterns, which often exceed the maximum output length, thus fail to answer the question. Furthermore, the Vision-only modality consistently yields the lower scores across models (e.g., 58.0% for GPT-4o and 47.0% for Qwen3-VL). Such a failure of visual heuristics underscores the success of our dataset construction: the “Numerical Perception” task necessitates a re-liance on precise numerical evidence rather than coarse visual patterns, thereby establishing a more rigorous benchmark for fine-grained TSR.

I Theoretical Underpinning of the Difficulty-Stratified Taxonomy 

Our four-level decomposition of TSR is not arbi-trary but is grounded in two well-established the-oretical frameworks: Bloom’s Taxonomy (Bloom et al., 1956) (cognitive psychology) and Bertin’s Levels of Reading (Monmonier, 1985) (visual ana-lytic). • Cognitive Reasoning (Bloom’s Taxonomy): 

Our hierarchy mirrors the progression from Low-Order Thinking Skills (LOTS) to High-Order Thinking Skills (HOTS). Specifically: (i) L1 (Numerical Read-out) maps to Remembering/Re-trieving (discrete fact extraction); (ii) L2 (Pattern Perception) maps to Understanding (identifying structural relationships and trends); (iii) L3 (Se-mantic Reasoning) maps to Applying/Analyzing (contextualizing patterns within domain-specific knowledge); and (iv) L4 (Predictive Inference) maps to Creating (generating future states or log-ical relationships based on historical temporal data). Recent studies (Huber and Niklaus, 2025) have formally advocated for using Bloom’s Tax-onomy to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. • Visual Perception (Bertin’s Levels of Reading): 

From a data visualization perspective, Jacques Bertin’s classic framework justifies our structural necessity: (i) Elementary Level (L1) focuses on a single data element; (ii) Intermediate/Global Level (L2) focuses on relationships and macro-scopic trends across multiple elements; and (iii) Extrapolative Level (L3/L4) involves deducing hidden semantics or future trends beyond the given data. By aligning with these frameworks, H ITSR pro-vides a principled road-map to evaluate whether a TSRM’s failure stems from a perceptual bottleneck (L1-L2) or a reasoning deficit (L3-L4). This struc-tured decomposition is necessary for diagnosing the precise cognitive boundaries of TSRMs. 

J Position of Our Work 

We emphasize that our paper intentionally focuses on TSR-Understanding (L1-L3) rather than TSR-Generation (L4). We argue that these are two fun-damentally different paradigms. • Paradigm Distinction. In the broader multi-modal field, understanding models (e.g., Qwen3-VL) and generative model (e.g., the diffusion model) are typically treated as distinct research directions. Integrating L4 (especially numerical forecasting) would necessitate a transition from a Reasoning Model to a Unified Understanding-and-Generation Model, which presents distinct technical challenges (e.g., specialized generative losses) and constitutes a separate research fron-tier. • Diagnostic Purity. Our primary goal is to pro-vide a diagnostic lens to pinpoint the cognitive boundaries of current TSRMs. By focusing on L1-L3, we ensure a rigorous evaluation of the model’s analytical capabilities without the con-founding factors of generative precision.

K.1 Task Introduction 

Given a time series segment x, the model is tasked with a Multiple Choice (MC) question to identify the unique chronological successor from a pool of four candidate patches y1, y 2, y 3, y 4. While the cor-rect successor follows x immediately in the orig-inal series, the negative candidates are sampled from distinct time series with diverse characteris-tics. This setup evaluates the model’s ability to extrapolate underlying temporal dynamics and dis-tinguish the true evolution of a series from statisti-cally distinct alternatives. 

K.2 Dataset Construction 

We extended our synthetic generation pipeline to ensure that L4 tasks are both challenging and unam-biguous. To prevent “shared ground truth” (where multiple options could be plausible), we imple-mented a multi-stage filtering protocol for negative candidates: • Statistical Uniqueness. We filter out candidates with high Pearson Correlation ( ≥ 0.8) or small Euclidean Distance to the ground truth to ensure distinctiveness. • Strategic Truncation. To avoid introducing excessive ambiguity, we ensure the split point between x and y does not occur during high-variance events, such as mid-spike or at sharp turning points. Splitting at such critical junctures may cause multiple plausible answers; by select-ing structurally stable partition points, we ensure the ground truth is the only logically consistent successor. • Trend Smoothness. We use a weighted continu-ation score, which incorporates gradient consis-tency and tail-to-head difference to verify that the positive sample is the most semantically and numerically plausible successor. • Mutual Exclusion. Negative candidates are cross-checked to ensure low mutual correlation, preventing redundant options within a single question. 

K.3 Transition to Vision Modalities 

To align with the dual-view strategy of LL ATISA, we transform the L4 predictive task into a dual-image input format. Specifically, the visualized patterns are rendered as a 2 × 2 grid of subplots, each representing one of the four options. Within each subplot, the lookback context and the candi-date prediction patch are visualized in blue and red, respectively, allowing the model to perform visual comparative analysis of temporal continuity. Com-plementing this, a precision-calibrated numerical table provides exact values for the lookback win-dow. This dual-view setup ensures the model can leverage global trend intuition from the plots while maintaining the fine-grained numerical grounding necessary for rigorous successor identification.
