# ABSTRACT 16 

Medical time-series data captures the dynamic progression of patient conditions, playing a vital 17 

role in modern clinical decision support systems. However, real-world clinical data is highly 18 

heterogeneous and inconsistently formatted. Furthermore, existing machine learning tools often 19 

have steep learning curves and fragmented workflows. Consequently, a significant gap remains 20 

between cutting-edge AI technologies and clinical application. To address this, we introduce 21 

mtslearn, an end-to-end integrated toolkit specifically designed for medical time-series data. 22 

First, the framework provides a unified data interface that automates the parsing and alignment 23 

of wide, long, and flat data formats. This design significantly reduces data cleaning overhead. 24 

Building on this, mtslearn provides a complete pipeline from data reading and feature engineering 25 

to model training and result visualization. Furthermore, it offers flexible interfaces for custom 26 

algorithms. Through a modular design, mtslearn simplifies complex data engineering tasks 27 

into a few lines of code. This significantly lowers the barrier to entry for clinicians with limited 28 

programming experience, empowering them to focus more on exploring medical hypotheses and 29 

accelerating the translation of advanced algorithms into real-world clinical practice. mtslearn is 30 

publicly available at https://github.com/PKUDigitalHealth/mtslearn . 31

# INTRODUCTION 34 

With the widespread adoption of electronic health records 1 and modern medical monitoring 35 

devices 2, medical data is experiencing significant expansion. Within this massive volume of data, 36 

the vast majority, such as vital signs and medication records, are fundamentally time-series 3. 37 

These time-series data capture the dynamic evolution of patient states. By leveraging effective 38 

machine learning techniques, they can systematically support clinical decision-making 4. 39

Despite the proven efficacy of machine learning in healthcare and the proliferation of machine 40 

learning tools, a significant divide persists between artificial intelligence technologies and clinical 41 

medicine. First, existing tools often require advanced programming skills, creating a barrier for 42 

clinical researchers 5. Second, data processing workflows lack standardization. Real-world clinical 43 

data is highly heterogeneous, often distributed in tables with inconsistent formats. Consequently, 44 

researchers must spend considerable time on tedious data cleaning and alignment prior to model 45 

training, which significantly slows down the research process. Finally, model implementation 46 

remains cumbersome. Advanced algorithms are often difficult to invoke directly, impeding the 47 

deployment of new technologies in real patient analysis 6. 48 

To overcome these challenges and bridge the gap between algorithmic development and clini- 49 

cal application, we propose mtslearn, a highly integrated analysis framework explicitly designed 50 

for time-series data. The core contributions of mtslearn are twofold: 51 

• Standardization of medical time-series data formats: We establish a unified data inter- 52 

face specification that addresses the inconsistencies in raw data formats, so that data from 53 

multiple sources in different formats can be parsed and aligned automatically. 54 

• Construction of an accessible, full-pipeline framework: The framework integrates a 55 

complete pipeline from raw data cleaning and feature engineering to model training and 56 

automated evaluation. With our highly encapsulated interfaces, clinicians can set up a 57 

complete analysis workflow in fewer than 20 lines of code. This allows them to focus on 58 

medical research rather than software implementation. 59 

# DESIGN AND DEVELOPMENT 60 

Problem Formulation 61 

Clinical prediction models have evolved significantly recently driven by the growing availability of 62 

longitudinal data and the need for timely decisions. This evolutionary path from simple statistical 63 

mapping to complex time series modeling is shown in Figure 1. 64 

Early models relied on structured tabular data, mapping a fixed set of patient variables to 65 

a single outcome. Logistic regression is a classic example and remains widely used due to 66 

its interpretability and robustness 7–10 . Survival analysis extended this approach by making the 67 

prediction target time-dependent. For instance, the Cox proportional hazards model estimates 68 

risk over time while keeping the input features static, allowing researchers to address a broader 69 

range of clinical questions 11–14 . 70 

The widespread use of electronic health records now provides a richer, longitudinal view 71 

of patient health. Initially, researchers handled repeated measurements by aggregating them 72 

into summary statistics, which forced temporal data into conventional tabular formats 15,16 . More 73 

recently, deep learning sequence models have allowed researchers to model patient trajectories 74 

directly, capturing true temporal dependencies 17,18 . In clinical settings, these methods are further 75 

adapted. For example, T-LSTM 19 can handle irregular sampling and varying time intervals, 76 

allowing models to better reflect real-world data collection processes. 77 

Nowadays, researchers have multiple complementary ways to model patient data, but these 78 

methods are typically confined to isolated workflows. The wide variance in medical data formats, 79 

coupled with the steep engineering complexity of deep learning pipelines, severely limits the 80 

bedside adoption of advanced models. To overcome these barriers, rather than introducing 81 

increasingly complex deep learning algorithms, this study capitalizes on mature, established AI 82 

medical tools and traditional statistical methods. By integrating these well-validated tools into 83

Data Specifications 88 

To address the inconsistencies in clinical time series data and lower the technical barrier for 89 

clinicians, we first formalize the data input structures. As illustrated in Figure 2, we standardize 90 

three common tabular data schemas: wide, long, and flattened formats 20 . 91 

• Wide format : This structure represents temporal snapshots, where each row corresponds 92 

to a specific time point for an individual subject, and feature measurements are organized 93 

into distinct columns. 94 

• Long format : Highly suitable for sparse clinical data, this schema records each measure- 95 

ment as a separate row containing the identifier, timestamp, variable name, and correspond- 96 

ing value. 97 

• Flattened format : This layout expands the data horizontally, using the subject ID as the 98 

unique row index. Measurement dimensions including time or specific features serve as 99 

column headers, encapsulating a patient’s entire trajectory within a single row. 100 

Based on the above format definition, mtslearn can easily integrate various datasets without 101 

requiring extensive manual preprocessing. Taking the common wide format as an example, clini- 102 

cians simply specify basic metadata, including the patient ID id_col , event timestamp time_col , 103 

and outcome label label_col . The framework then automatically parses and standardizes the 104 

underlying data structures, substantially reducing preprocessing overhead. 105

4End-to-End Pipeline Architecture 116 

mtslearn adopts a modular architecture to ensure maintainability and scalability. As illustrated in 117 

Figure 3, the core workflow begins with raw data reading, which then branches into static and 118 

time-series processing pipelines. Finally, the processed data are fed into the classifier module for 119 

model training, cross-validation, and comprehensive performance evaluation. 120 

Core Functional Modules 121 

The framework contains four core modules. Together, they cover the entire analytical pipeline 122 

from data ingestion to model evaluation. The specific processing logic and capabilities of each 123 

module are detailed below. 124 

Data Ingestion and Standardization: Processor 125 

The Processor module manages data ingestion and standardizes heterogeneous tabular data. 126 

It parses both individual files and multi-entity directories. The module transforms diverse input 127 

structures (long, wide, or flattened) into a unified wide representation, indexed by entity identifier 128 

and timestamp. It subsequently handles string parsing, time formatting, and the removal of 129 

redundant columns. Finally, it separates predictors from ground-truth labels before exporting the 130 

data. 131 

Static Feature Engineering: StaticProcessor 132 

The StaticProcessor prepares temporal records for static machine learning algorithms. It 133 

achieves this by aggregating time-series sequences into static features. Specifically, it computes 134 

statistics like mean and variance, and extracts a duration metric to measure the observation 135 

window length. The module also handles standard data preparation tasks. These include dataset 136 

splitting, outlier removal, missing value imputation, and feature scaling. 137 

Time-Series Data Processing: TSProcessor 138 

Deep learning models take fixed-size 3D arrays as input. TSProcessor converts variable-length 139 

multivariate sequences into this format by padding with trailing zeros and interpolating irregular 140 

time steps. For missing values, it first tries to fill from other observations of the same subject; if 141 

none are available, it falls back to global statistics. For missing data, the module uses a localized 142 

fallback imputation method. Missing values are first imputed using other observations from the 143 

same subject; if none exist, global statistics are used instead. During feature scaling, masking 144 

mechanisms are applied so that padded zeros do not distort the calculations. 145 

Modeling and Evaluation Paradigms: Static_Classifier and Temporal_Classifier 146 

The modeling pipeline is encapsulated by two foundational classes, Static_Classifier and 147 

Temporal_Classifier , which handle static and continuous outputs, respectively. Together, they 148 

offer a unified interface for training optimization and tracking loss. Depending on the input and 149 

output data structures, the framework supports four different algorithmic paradigms. 150 

First, for tasks predicting single-point outcomes from static baselines, the toolkit integrates 151 

models like XGBoost 24 . Second, for sequential inputs with static outcomes, it supports standard 152 

LSTMs 25 and Time-Aware LSTMs 19 . The latter is specifically designed for irregular recording 153

# DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 212 

To help close the technical gap between clinical medicine and machine learning, we developed 213 

mtslearn, a toolkit specifically for medical time-series analysis. mtslearn allows clinicians with 214 

limited programming experience to manage data processing and modeling tasks independently. 215 

This makes building clinical decision-support models from real-world data faster and easier. 216 

The main advantage of mtslearn is its simplicity and ease of use. It follows the scikit-learn API, 217 

making it easy to learn. The toolkit is comprehensive, covering data loading and preprocessing 218 

while providing suitable modeling approaches for four common input-output types in clinical tasks. 219 

Essentially, mtslearn takes a step further than traditional statistical methods. 220 

Despite these advantages, the current work has a few limitations. First, while mtslearn already 221 

includes some basic deep learning capabilities, it has not yet expanded to more advanced deep 222 

learning architectures. This limits its full potential when dealing with highly complex tasks. Second, 223 

the current system lacks built-in explainability. Without explicit clinical reasoning, it is difficult to 224 

show how the models make decisions, which is a common barrier to earning doctors’ trust. 225 

In future work, we plan to introduce explainable AI modules to provide clinical justifications 226 

for model decisions, thereby increasing physician trust in these systems. Concurrently, we will 227 

continuously integrate more advanced deep learning models. This will ensure the toolkit remains 228 

capable of addressing increasingly complex clinical analysis requirements. 229
