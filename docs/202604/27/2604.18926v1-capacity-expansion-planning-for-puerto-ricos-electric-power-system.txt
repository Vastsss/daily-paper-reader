# ABSTRACT 

This study presents a mathematical optimization framework and preliminary analysis to inform long-term investment planning for Puerto Rico’s electric power system. In this study, we consider a high-resolution capacity expansion planning (CEP) model to identify least-cost generation and storage investments that improve the reliability of Puerto Rico’s power system. The model co-optimizes new investments along with thermal generator retirements, while considering a detailed operational model of generator dispatch, thermal generator commitments, fuel selection, and storage operation, subject to equipment engineering limits, system constraints, fuel supply limitations, and load satisfaction. Key methodological advances relative to prior long-term planning studies on Puerto Rico’s power system include: (i) a nodal representation of the transmission network at 38 kV and above, (ii) hourly chronological simulation of system operations for a set of representative days, (iii) an explicit unit commitment formulation for all existing and new thermal units, with realistic ramping, minimum up-and down-times, and startup costs, (iv) explicit modeling of system-wide fuel supply constraints, and (v) stochastic operational scenarios that capture load variability, renewable availability, and the high forced outage rates of legacy units. Using data supplied by LUMA, PREPA, and the DOE along with publicly available data, we construct present day (2024) and future (2030) Puerto Rico test systems, where the future system includes planned generation and storage projects. We then analyze several planning scenarios with varying future load levels, fuel supply assumptions, materializations of currently planned additions to the generation and storage fleets, and allowable new technologies. Our results from these studies indicate that given the recent relaxation of interim renewable goals for the near future in Puerto Rico, an optimal portfolio for Puerto Rico’s future power system includes at least 1.5 GW of new H-class combined cycle (CC) capacity in addition to planned generation projects. This addition is needed primarily to replace unreliable legacy thermal units rather than to serve new load. The new CC investments eliminate modeled load shedding in the bulk system and restore a robust reserve margin, even under stressed load and outage conditions.

# 1 Introduction 

The Puerto Rico power grid is an isolated system serving approximately 1.5 million customers [1]. Its island geography, in an area prone to hurricanes, coupled with decades of operational and financial challenges have led to a rapidly deteriorating situation over recent years. The frequency and duration of outages in Puerto Rico have increased since 2021, even in the absence of major weather events, with customers experiencing on average up to 13.7 interruptions annually, adding up to approximately 30 hours of interruptions per year [2], over twice the national average and far above other island systems like Hawaii or hurricane-prone areas like the Florida keys [3]. Among the challenges contributing most to Puerto Rico’s reliability deficiencies are a debilitated transmission grid and an aging generation fleet with frequent planned and unplanned outages. As a result, operating with insufficient system reserves has become nearly quotidian in Puerto Rico [4]. Several efforts have been undertaken by local and federal authorities to bring the quality of the electricity service in Puerto Rico up to national standards, while simultaneously pursuing other objectives like the goal, enacted into law by the Puerto Rico legislature in 2019, of meeting 100% of its electricity needs with renewable sources by 2050. Some of the actions taken during this time included the arrival of LUMA, a new agent tasked with operating the power system and coordinating the Integrated Resource Planning process [5]. Under this framework, a number of storage and generation projects, mostly solar photovoltaic and wind power plants, have been announced and find themselves in various stages of development. These projects are organized into so-called “tranches,” a term we sometimes use in this text to refer to these projects. After some years with limited success and a situation that has continued to deteriorate, recent measures taken include a short-term (12-36 months) stabilization plan [6] to identify projects that must be prioritized, notably: postponing the retirement of existing coal-fired generation, extending and adding short-term emergency generation units and confirming and adding battery storage capacity to the bulk power system. Recent developments also include relaxing the interim renewable target goals in the interest of improving reliability for the customers [7] and moving to change the current system operator [8]. The Department of Energy (DOE) has led various efforts to mobilize the National Laboratories and provide modeling support to Puerto Rico. Most relevant for the work presented in this report was the PR100 study [9], in which different pathways are investigated to reach the 100% renewable target while increasing the quality of service for the customers as soon as possible. This was an extensive study spanning data gathering and generation, modeling, and a significant social component. The modeling included studies on production cost modeling, capacity expansion planning, and resource adequacy. The work performed by LLNL and presented here is much less extensive than the PR100 study and focuses only on capacity expansion planning (CEP). The CEP modeling performed here differs from the mentioned previous study in a few important aspects. Regarding the assumptions, we do not treat the 100% renewable target as a fixed directive but consider a renewables-only scenario as only one possible planning scenario in the study. On a methodological front, a few key aspects differentiate this study from previous modeling efforts. Most importantly, having identified that the operational limitations and frequent outages of the legacy thermal units in the system have a very high impact on system reliability, significant effort has gone into accurately capturing the operational details of these units, operational details that are often simplified away in these types of studies. This includes a detailed modeling of the unit commitment problem as part of the CEP model. Other differences include: • Fuel supply constraints are explicitly modeled • Stochastic operational scenarios including generator outages are included in the CEP model • The temporal resolution is hourly across all the operational scenarios considered. • The system is modeled with nodal resolution at voltage levels of 38kV and above, as opposed to only 115kV and above. • Generation units are forced to be whole multiples of real turbine sizes, as opposed to allowing fractional numbers that may underestimate investment costs. 

1.1 Scope of the Study 

This study serves as an exploratory tool to determine a least-cost investment plan in generation and storage infrastructure for the island of Puerto Rico for a series of exogenous scenarios regarding fuel availability, technology portfolios and target years. The different planning scenarios are meant to provide a broad view of the investment space and the differences in cost and buildout implied by different realities or policies. The selected scenarios are used only for illustrative purposes, rather than due to any expectations or preferences. 5Capacity Expansion Planning for Puerto Rico LLNL-JRNL-2017618 

The high-fidelity modeling of the operational limitations of thermal power plants and the high cost attributed to the involuntary disconnection of customers (i.e., load shedding) are aimed at finding a least-cost investment plan for increasing the reliability of the electricity service experienced by Puerto Rico customers as soon as possible. Setting the objective for our modeling tool as total-cost minimization with a high penalty for unserved energy in this way is expected to result in an investment plan that is in the best interest of the electricity users, both in terms of reliability of service and service cost. Nevertheless, a resource adequacy (RA) study and a detailed analysis of the implications of any proposed investment plan for the ratepayer’s electricity bill were out of the scope of this study. They should be performed as part of the modeling work used to quantify decisions made in Puerto Rico infrastructure rebuild effort. The CEP model used here makes significant simplifications regarding the constraints imposed by the transmission grid. A selection of the investment plans obtained was reviewed by a team at Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL). They performed power flow, contingency, and dynamic stability analyses on the Puerto Rico system that would result from the implementation of these investment plans. The feasibility of the proposed system was assessed, and in particular, the need for additional generation and/or battery storage reserves for frequency and voltage support. While preliminary results show that the investment plans obtained from the CEP tool presented in this study are feasible and reasonable, exhaustive analysis in this direction was out of the scope of this study. The remainder of this report is organized as follows. In Section 2, we introduce our Capacity Expansion Planning model and describe in detail the mathematical formulation of this problem as an optimization model as well as modeling assumptions and limitations. In Section 3, we describe the data sources used as inputs for the CEP model and assumptions made about missing or incomplete data to enable this analysis. The different planning scenarios and corresponding results are presented in Section 4. The conclusions of the study are presented in Section 5. The Appendix contains details about the mathematical model and data that were relegated from the main part of this text. 

# 2 Capacity Expansion Planning 

To determine a cost-optimal long-term investment plan for the Puerto Rico power system, we consider the problem of Capacity Expansion Planning (CEP), a structured mathematical optimization problem that can be formulated as a stochastic mixed-integer linear program (MILP). The model aims to find the best portfolio of new generators and storage, and the size and location of these units, subject to constraints on land-use, fuel availability, thermal limits of transmission lines, thermal generator commitments, generator availability, and demand projections. The objective of the model is to minimize overall system investment and operational costs, including a high penalty on load shedding to encourage system reliability. There are three features of our model worth highlighting: 1) the model is a nodal model with high spatial and temporal resolution 2) it includes high-resolution modeling of the operational details of thermal generators, including constraints on their fuel supply, and 3) it is a stochastic model. While CEP is a well-studied problem in the power systems literature, most existing models are zonal models with low spatial resolution, e.g., using 34 buses to model the entire Western Interconnection of the United States [10]. In our recent work [11], we found that lower spatial resolution models led to 36% under-investment in new generation and corresponded to modeling errors of around 40%, equivalent to billions of dollars a year for a realistic test system. Recent work has also demonstrated how higher-resolution modeling of thermal generator commitments can impact CEP results, e.g., by encouraging investments in more flexible and dispatchable resources [12, 13]. High temporal resolution allows for higher fidelity modeling of renewable power sources and thermal generator commitments [14, 15]. Lastly, because we are planning for many years into the future, there are large uncertainties associated with future inputs to any model, e.g., projections for electric power demand and possible future weather scenarios. Thus, it is critical to consider stochastic planning models that incorporate uncertainty via a set of future scenarios. There is significant evidence that stochastic models should be used instead of deterministic ones for CEP [16, 17]. Our model is a high-resolution, nodal model that co-optimizes new generation and grid-scale storage investments in a power system. It is a stochastic model in that we consider several representative scenarios (modeled via representative days) of electric power demand, wind and solar availability, and forced outages of thermal generators. This model is based on the formulation introduced in [18] and further developed in [19, 20]. Unlike these models, we also consider constraints from the commitment of thermal generators, including ramping constraints and startup and shutdown costs and constraints, as well as more advanced fuel supply modeling, including dual fuel switching and area-specific fuel supply constraints. Our formulation includes both investment-stage and operational-stage costs and constraints, where the operational-stage problems are effectively modified Unit Commitment (UC) / Economic Dispatch (ED) problems [21]. The model includes constraints that link the second-stage decisions to the investment-stage decisions, e.g., the power generation at a bus must be bounded by the amount of installed generation capacity at that bus. 2.7 Assumptions 

The formulation described above includes the following assumptions: • The sets of buses and transmission lines are assumed to be fixed. We are not considering bus additions or transmission expansion in this work. • The distribution system is not considered in the modeling, so possible costs associated to distribution system improvements are not included. • The model is technology-agnostic, i.e., policy concerns like renewable portfolio standards (RPS) are not included. Sensitivities with renewables-only future expansion are included and explicitly noted; however, only some of these correspond to having no existing thermal units in the future system (a surrogate for a 100% RPS requirement). • All new generators are assumed to be utility-scale and connected to the transmission system. We do not consider DERs as distinct from behind-the-meter load. • Storage and renewable generator investments are treated as continuous decisions in our model. • We only consider instantaneous generation and storage expansion decisions, i.e., investments selected by the model are available immediately for operation. • The model considers all operational hours of the high-stress 3-month time horizon from May to July, unless otherwise noted. • All costs are scaled to be in units of $/year. Capital costs are amortized over the lifetime of the plant during data processing, and operational costs are calculated over a representative 3-month time horizon and scaled to a year. • We neglect real-time or day-ahead forecast error in the commitment and operation of the generation dispatch; however, we choose a large reserve margin that helps compensate for this. • Generator capacities and costs are taken to be after generation losses. • Operations and maintenance costs for transmission lines and transformers are negligible. • The transmission system is modeled with a nodal “pipe-and-bubble” approach, which treats power flow like a transportation network with branches subject to thermal limits. This approach neglects the physics of power flow and overestimates the transfer capacity in the system. This assumption was made for computational reasons, rather than modeling needs, and could be improved in a future iteration of this work. • We do not model transmission outages or an N − 1 criterion. Outages are captured via the reserve margin constraint. • UC decisions are made over representative days. Consecutive commitment and operational decisions are represented within a day but not across different days. The ending on/off status of a thermal generator at the last time period is taken to be the initial status of the generator at the start of the same day. However, minimum up/down time and ramping are not enforced for this end-of-day/start-of-day linking. • Similarly for storage modeling, we assume that the ending storage level at the last time period is the same as the initial storage level at the start of the same day. This models charge/discharge cycles. • We assume that retiring a generator eliminates FOM costs for that generator. Thermal units eligible for retirement will be retired only if doing so saves on overall systems costs, as determined by the retirement costs, FOM cost savings, generator under-utilization penalty, and system operation. • The under-utilization of existing thermal generators is computed for each day (summing over 24 hours) in the 3-month horizon, with soft enforcement via a penalty term. This formulation allows for model decomposition via the progressive hedging algorithm. • Forced and planned outages are modeled for new thermal generators via derating to meet an assumed capacity factor, rather than as a binary time-series input as we do for existing thermal generators. 13 Capacity Expansion Planning for Puerto Rico LLNL-JRNL-2017618 

2.8 Limitations of the Approach 

There are several limitations of our approach, namely that we are not capturing power flow (mentioned in the section above) but also that we are not capturing sub-hourly variability and system dynamics/stability. Because of this, there could be better investments for an objective based on system stability, e.g., BESS may have more value in that context. Post-processing can be used to validate power flow and reserve margins on a set of possible contingencies, i.e., line or generator outages, and our colleagues at PNNL have been validating our results using their PSS/E software. Because our model is only a two-stage model, we do not capture multi-stage investment approaches that may be preferred for system reliability, i.e., gradual or sequential buildout approaches. Certain resources such as BESS may be more costly long-term but quicker to deploy due to supply chain constraints, regulatory approvals, and construction time. These facts are not within the scope of our model. Finally, the presented study is a “snapshot” run for the season of highest stress in the system and should not be viewed as a full-scale resource adequacy study. For a complete resource adequacy study, we would need to stress test the system with a variety of different weather, demand, forced outage, and fuel price scenarios, while considering an N-k contingency criterion. Due to data limitations, this is not within scope for this work.

3.4 Forced Outages 

The high rate of forced outages for existing generation is the main culprit for the low quality of service currently faced by Puerto Rico customers. To accurately capture this reality in our study, forced outages were incorporated in the operational scenarios generated for the analysis. Forced outages for each existing plant were randomly generated so that the fraction of hours out of service matches the outage rate specified in [23], and each outage was assumed to last 40 hours, consistent with [23]. Planned outages included in Table 12 of that same source were also incorporated into the generated outage scenarios. For new generation units, no individual outages are generated. We instead represent any required maintenance or forced outage via an 85% capacity factor, which we model by derating the nameplate capacity of all new units by this amount at all periods. No transmission outages were explicitly modeled.

The fuels considered in the PR test system are natural gas (NG), bunker (also called residual fuel oil – RFO), diesel, and coal. We used the EIA-923 form to obtain data on Puerto Rico’s fuel consumption for electricity production from May to July 2024 [32]. This plant-specific data was used to obtain the fuel consumption at the locations of all the existing thermal plants. Because this is historical consumption data, it likely underestimates the possible available supply of fuels. For example, historical consumption of fuel may have been reduced if some generators were out-of-service, but that does not necessarily imply a lack of available imports. After doing several fuel supply sensitivity studies and discussing with DOE staff, we settled on the following three fuel supply scenarios: 1. Existing (2024) fuel supply • San Juan: fix NG and bunker to the 2024 consumption levels from EIA-923 • Palo Seco: allow 20% of the 2024 NG consumption at San Juan (can be trucked), fix bunker to the 2024 consumption level • Aguirre: fix bunker to the 2024 consumption level • Costa Sur: allow unlimited NG and bunker • EcoElectrica: allow unlimited NG • AES: fix coal to the 2024 consumption level • Allow unlimited diesel at all locations except AES; since it is the most expensive fuel, it will only be used when other fuels are not available 2. Future (2030) fuel supply • Allow unlimited NG at San Juan, Palo Seco, Cambalache, and Mayaguez • Same as the existing case for the rest 3. Future+ expanded fuel supply • Allow unlimited NG at Aguirre and AES • Same as the future case for the rest Note that the fuel supply for each representative day in Equation (35) is taken as the daily average consumption (in MMBtu/day) from the May to July 2024 EIA data. This may be overly conservative as it does not allow for shifting fuel from high to low load days. The location-specific fuel costs for San Juan, Palo Seco, Costa Sur, EcoElectrica, and the new Energiza project were based on DOE input for NG prices, which are relative to Henry Hub (HH) prices. We use historical HH prices from May to July 2024 for the existing cases and projected HH prices for 2030 (based on the reference case in EIA’s 2025 AEO) for the future cases [34, 35]. We scale the 2030 price by a seasonality factor for the May to July period, based on the 2024 historical HH data. The location-specific bunker fuel prices for San Juan, Palo Seco, Aguirre, and Costa Sur are obtained from the PREPA PI dashboard, from a snapshot of prices taken in October 2025. The diesel fuel prices were provided by DOE based on current day oil prices and are not location-specific. For the remaining fuel costs, we used the LUMA December 2024 report, which has prices for NG, bunker, and coal but does not split these out by location within Puerto Rico [4]. See Section 6.3 in the Appendix for more details on the actual fuel costs used in the model.

# 4 Simulations 

We now present computational results for solving the CEP problems on the PR test system. The model is formulated in python using the open-source algebraic modeling language pyomo [36, 37] and the open-source UC/ED modeling package EGRET [21]. To decompose these large-scale problems, we use a parallel implementation of Progressive Hedging (PH) with a hub-and-spoke architecture implemented in mpi-sppy [38]. This PH configuration includes incumbent finders and lower bounding methods, allowing us to compute optimality gaps that provide a certificate on the quality of our solutions (the lower the gap, the closer to optimal). We decompose the 3-month time horizon by representative day, and the single-day subproblems are solved with the commercial optimization software Gurobi. We run these simulations on the high performance computing (HPC) cluster dane at LLNL. Each compute node has 112 2.0GHz cores and 256GB of RAM, and we fit 5 single-day scenarios per node.

# 5 Conclusions 

The work performed for this report created a CEP model and data workflow for Puerto Rico. This tool was used for some initial analysis and can be continued to be used as a modeling support tool for decision makers looking to improve Puerto Rico’s power grid infrastructure. This tool differs from previous work in three important features: 1) the power system model is a nodal model with high spatial and temporal resolution 2) it includes high-resolution modeling of the operational details of thermal generators, including system-wide constraints on fuel supply, and 3) it is a stochastic model. Based on analysis over a range of planning scenarios, we find that an optimal investment portfolio will include at least 1.5 GW of new CC (H-class) units, in addition to already planned new thermal units. These new thermal units are needed to replace unreliable existing thermal units. Preliminary results suggest that this CC investment greatly reduces the involuntary disconnection of consumers, i.e., load shedding, to near zero, excluding other details like distribution and transmission outages that are not captured in this analysis. A direct assessment of the impact of an RPS policy was not part of the study. However, it was found that only allowing the construction of new renewable generation investments increases investment and operational costs up to 14% 1. In the case where the future fleet is composed of 100% renewable sources, the cost increase was much higher at 71% for the projected future load scenario. These numbers may increase if additional BESS capacity is found to be necessary to provide system stability support upon further analysis. Results also suggest that if construction of thermal units is unconstrained, the scale of currently planned BESS deployment may be unnecessary. It is important however to recall that this study may not capture all the value provided by BESS since stability and contingency analysis were not conducted here. As implied above, further studies are needed for a more definitive assessment. We discuss recommended next steps next. 

5.1 Suggestions for Future Work 

Although it is expected that the inclusion of stochastic outage scenarios and a more accurate representation of the operational characteristics of thermal power plants will lead to an investment plan much more aligned with the reliability needs of the island, this work does not substitute a resource adequacy study. An RA study should be performed to refine the results of this analysis and assess the need for complementary investments. Our work demonstrates how different stochastic operational scenarios can be handled by this CEP tool. As a step that could be subsequent or parallel to the RA study, a richer set of operational scenarios could be identified to better represent the most relevant operational conditions of the system. This may include a broader time horizon, including contingencies, extreme weather conditions, more forced outages, etc. Increasing modeling detail must be undertaken with care to not compromise the computational tractability of the model. We believe some improvement of the representation of spinning reserves and ancillary services could be made to enhance the operational modeling capabilities of the tool without significantly complicating the model. Finally, this model did not consider the uncertainty around the ability to deploy the recommended resources. Different technologies or sizes of power plants may face different social acceptance and supply chain difficulties. Introducing a multi-stage planning model that captures such uncertainties would be of interest to decision makers. This would likely necessitate simplifying other parts of the model to make computational tractability possible but would be a path worth pursuing.

6.3 Cost Assumptions for Fuel Costs 

As discussed in Section 3.5, the location-specific fuel costs for San Juan, Palo Seco, Costa Sur, EcoElectrica, and the new Energiza project are relative to Henry Hub (HH) prices. We use the following HH prices for the existing (2024) and future (2030) cases: • (Existing) $2.24/MMBTu • (Future) $3.15/MMBTu where the existing price is the historical HH average price from May-July 2024, and the future price is taken as the projected HH price in 2030 from EIA’s most recent AEO, scaled by a seasonality factor of 1.023 for the May to July season based on the 2024 HH data. Note that these are all in 2024 dollars. Then we use the following equations, as provided by DOE, to determine the location-specific NG prices in Puerto Rico: • San Juan: $6.5 + 1.15*HH • Palo Seco (existing case - trucking): $7.60 + 1.15*HH + $250/truck • Palo Seco (future cases - pipe): $7.60 + 1.15*HH • EcoElectrica, Costa Sur: $5.5 + 1.15*HH • Energiza: $7.95 + 1.15*HH where we consider the trucks to hold 860 MMBTu/truck, yielding an additional cost of $0.29/MMBTu for trucked fuel. We also obtained location-specific bunker fuel prices for San Juan, Palo Seco, Aguirre, and Costa Sur from the PREPA PI dashboard, from a snapshot of prices taken in October 2025. Those are given as: • San Juan: $12.67/MMBTu • Palo Seco: $12.65/MMBTu • Aguirre: $13.63/MMBTu • Costa Sur: $12.98/MMBTu The diesel price of $17/MMBTu was obtained from DOE and corresponds to December 2025 oil prices. For the remaining fuel costs, including filling in NG prices for new units at Cambalache, Aguirre, etc., we used the LUMA December 2024 report [4].
