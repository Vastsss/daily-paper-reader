Abstract 

Subsample-based estimation is a standard tool for achieving robustness to outliers in econo-metric models. This paper shows that, in dynamic time series settings, such procedures are fundamentally invalid under contamination, even under oracle knowledge of contamination locations. The key issue is that contamination propagates through the model’s residual filter and distorts the estimation criterion itself. As a result, removing contaminated observations does not, in general, restore the uncontaminated objective or ensure consistency. We charac-terise this failure as a structural incompatibility between pointwise subsampling and residual propagation. To address it, we propose a propagation-compatible transformation of index sets, formalised through a patch removal operator that removes the residual footprint of contami-nation. Under suitable conditions, the proposed operator leaves the estimator asymptotically unchanged under the uncontaminated model, while restoring consistency for the clean-data parameter under contamination. The results apply to a broad class of residual-based estimators and show that valid subsample-based estimation in dynamic models requires explicit control of residual propagation. 

Keywords : Outliers; Residual propagation; Robustness; Consistency; Asymptotic theory. 

# 1 Introduction 

Outliers are a pervasive feature of economic and financial time series. Their main statistical effect is not merely to create a few anomalous observations, but to obstruct accurate estimation of the underlying clean process. From an econometric perspective, the object of interest is the latent data generating mechanism rather than the contaminated observations themselves. If the clean process does not exhibit a given feature, but the observed data appear to do so because of outliers, inference based on the contaminated series is misleading.

In cross-sectional models, contamination is local in the sense that its effect is tied to the contaminated observation, and robust procedures operate by down-weighting or removing individual observations. In dynamic time series models, however, this abstraction breaks down. Contamination propagates through the temporal structure and, in particular, through the residual filter that defines the estimation criterion, so that local contamination in the observed series induces non-local distortions in the residual sequence. In dynamic models, robustness cannot be achieved by selecting subsets of observations alone. Be-cause estimation is based on a residual-based criterion, procedures that operate only at the level of individual observations do not remove the induced distortions, even when contaminated obser-vations are correctly identified. Residual propagation through the model filter induces non-local distortions in the residual sequence and, consequently, in the estimation criterion itself. Thus, the classical subsampling paradigm is structurally incompatible with dynamic propagation, and this failure is not due to imperfect detection but arises at the level of the estimation criterion. As a result, standard subsample-based procedures do not, in general, restore consistency in dy-namic models, even under oracle knowledge of contamination locations. The failure is structural rather than algorithmic, since approaches based on improved detection, residual down-weighting, or ad hoc trimming operate at the level of individual observations or residual magnitudes rather than on the mechanism by which contamination enters the criterion. The relevant object for es-timation is therefore not contamination at the level of the observed series, but contamination at the level of the residual-based criterion, where local contamination induces non-local distortions. Consequently, the objective function is not centred at the true parameter, and consistency cannot be restored by pointwise robustification alone. The economic relevance of this issue is immediate. In linear models, outliers may induce spuri-ous statistical significance, so that parameters that are insignificant under the clean model appear significant in contaminated samples, or vice versa. In nonlinear settings, they may create false ev-idence of volatility clustering, structural breaks, regime switching, or other forms of nonlinearity, even when the clean process exhibits no such features, or distort or mask such features when they are present. This broader view is consistent with the classical time-series outlier literature, which has long noted that outliers may mimic or obscure level shifts and variance changes; see, for ex-ample, Tsay (1988). Such features reflect properties of the underlying stochastic mechanism and are compatible with stationarity, whereas outliers represent external contamination arising from atypical or transient events. The econometric objective is therefore to recover the structure of the clean process rather than the distortions induced by contamination. These considerations raise a fundamental requirement. Estimation procedures must remain con-sistent for the clean model even when the observed data are contaminated. Without this property, hypothesis testing, model interpretation, and specification analysis become unreliable, because the estimated model reflects contamination rather than the underlying data generating mechanism. The existing literature recognises that outliers in time series may have dynamic effects. Early work, surveyed for example by Martin (1979) and Martin and Yohai (1986), studies additive and innovative outliers and their effects on residuals, diagnostics, and influence functions in dependent data. Classical model-based approaches include Chang et al. (1988) and Chen and Liu (1993), 2Yang and Sandberg Subsample-based Estimation under Dynamic Contamination who analyse outlier effects and develop procedures for parameter estimation and joint estima-tion of model and outlier effects in ARMA-type settings. More recent contributions, particularly Johansen and Nielsen (2016), develop outlier detection procedures and connect them to robust estimation through residual-based screening and trimming of observed residuals, including Huber-type estimators, least trimmed squares (LTS), and forward search methods (Huber, 1964; Welsh and Ronchetti, 2002; Rousseeuw, 1984; Hadi and Simonoff, 1993; Atkinson and Riani, 2000). Such approaches rely on diagnostic identification of aberrant observations, a problem long recog-nised as subtle in time series settings; see, for example, Ljung (1993). The asymptotic properties of these estimators are well understood under clean-model conditions, including for trimming-based procedures such as LTS (Huber, 1964; Chen and Wu, 1988; Jureˇ cková and Sen, 1996; Rousseeuw, 1984; Víšek, 2006). However, this literature focuses on detection, robustification, or estimator redesign, rather than on the structural effect of dynamic contamination on the estimation criterion itself. Even when contaminated observations are correctly identified, the resulting procedures do not address how contamination enters the residual-based criterion. This includes robust estimation for multivariate dynamic models, such as VARMA settings (Ben et al., 1999), and approaches based on modified residual constructions that limit the effect of individual outliers (Muler et al., 2009). These con-tributions do not recognise that, as a consequence, subsample-based estimation may fail to remain consistent even under oracle knowledge of contamination locations. By contrast, the present paper identifies this incompatibility and proposes a propagation-compatible correction that can be com-bined with a broad class of residual-based procedures. Importantly, the analysis does not rely on modelling the contamination mechanism itself, but instead operates through high-level conditions on its magnitude, rate, and propagation through the residual filter. The contribution of this paper is threefold. First, we establish that subsample-based estimation is generically inconsistent in dynamic time series models under contamination, even under oracle knowledge of contamination locations. In particular, pointwise subsampling is structurally incom-patible with dynamic contamination. Second, we characterise the mechanism underlying this failure. We derive an exact representa-tion of additive and innovative contamination after passage through the residual filter and use it to show how propagation induces non-local distortions in the residual-based criterion. This allows us to distinguish between propagation in the observed series and propagation in the residual sequence, and to identify the latter as the relevant object for estimation. The analysis yields a precise descrip-tion of aberrant residual patches and shows that the nature of propagation depends fundamentally on the dynamic structure of the model. For pure autoregressive (AR) models, the residual effect of contamination has finite memory, whereas for models with moving average (MA) components it may have infinite memory, although it decays under causality and invertibility. Stationarity is therefore the structural condition that makes consistency restoration possible. Without such decay, for example in nonstationary settings with unit root behaviour, contamination cannot be controlled by finite corrections. Third, we provide a correction that restores validity. We show that subsample-based estimation must be compatible with the propagation structure induced by the model, thereby providing a nec-essary condition for consistency under dynamic contamination. In particular, the retained subset must exclude not only the directly contaminated observations, but also the indices affected by their 3Yang and Sandberg Subsample-based Estimation under Dynamic Contamination residual propagation. This leads to a propagation-compatible transformation of index sets governed by a propagation length parameter κ. We formalise this correction through a patch removal opera-tor, which removes the residual footprint of contamination and can be combined with a broad class of residual-based procedures without modifying their internal structure. Within this framework, we establish asymptotic invariance under the transformation in the uncontaminated case and asymp-totic equivalence between contaminated and uncontaminated estimators once residual propagation is properly controlled, yielding consistency for the clean-data parameter under contamination. In the purely AR case, the correction is especially sharp, as taking κ = p is sufficient. In particular, the key mechanism operates at the level of the mapping from observations to resid-uals, rather than on the specific parametric form of the model. This indicates that the proposed framework is not tied to a particular time series specification, but applies more generally to set-tings in which estimation is based on observations processed through stable filtering mechanisms. The proposed patch removal operator may appear similar to the common practice of discarding observations in the vicinity of detected outliers. However, this interpretation is misleading. Such practices do not arise from an explicit model of residual propagation and therefore do not target the mechanism by which contamination distorts the estimation criterion. In contrast, the present approach is derived from a structural analysis of residual propagation. The transformation of index sets is governed by the model-implied filter, which determines how contamination enters the criterion. This yields a propagation-compatible correction that is model-based rather than ad hoc, and for which we establish formal results on asymptotic invariance and consistency. The contribution is therefore not an additional trimming step, but the identification and resolution of a structural failure of subsample-based estimation in dynamic models. These results matter because consistent estimation of the clean model is a prerequisite for reliable inference. Residual-based tests for heteroskedasticity, serial dependence, and nonlinearity, as well as specification searches, are informative only if the fitted model consistently approximates the latent clean process. Otherwise, contamination may be misinterpreted as structural features of the data generating mechanism. In both linear and nonlinear settings, this leads to distorted statisti-cal evidence and unreliable conclusions about model structure. The same issue arises in model selection, where contamination may distort specification searches and favour incongruent models (Johansen and Nielsen, 2009; Doornik and Hendry, 2016). The paper is also related to the agenda highlighted by Johansen and Nielsen (2016), who emphasise the importance of clustered contamination and ε-contamination in time series. We address a fun-damental aspect of this problem by showing that even sparse contamination, when combined with temporal dependence, can undermine consistency through residual propagation, and by providing a general, propagation-compatible correction that restores it under suitable conditions. This propa-gation mechanism is distinct from the more commonly studied propagation in the observed series, which we refer to as level propagation . Level propagation refers to how contamination appears in the observed series itself, while residual propagation refers to how the same contamination appears after the residual filter is applied. The latter is the directly relevant object for any estimator that can be expressed as a function of the residual sequence. Consequently, the observed series alone does not reveal the full effect of contamination on estimation. The propagation patterns described above depend critically on the dynamic structure of the model. Under the causality and invertibility conditions imposed earlier, the filters ψ(L) and π(L) are absolutely summable. As a consequence, the effect of a single contamination decays over time in magnitude, even when the associated patch is of infinite length. 6Yang and Sandberg Subsample-based Estimation under Dynamic Contamination This decay property is essential for the subsequent analysis. It implies that, although contamination may induce non-local distortions in the residual sequence, these distortions become asymptotically negligible beyond a sufficiently large horizon. In the absence of such decay, the effect of contami-nation would persist indefinitely without attenuation, and no trimming or patch removal procedure could restore consistency. The role of stationarity (causality and invertibility) is therefore not merely technical. It ensures that contamination propagation through the residual filter remains controllable, and provides the foundation for consistency restoration under the procedures developed in the following sections. We introduce the notion of patches to formalise the propagation effects described above. An outlier patch corresponds to the consecutive set of time indices affected by the propagation of a single outlier through the time series dynamics. If the propagation affects the observed series itself, the resulting set of indices is called an aberrant level patch , or simply a level patch , corresponding to level propagation. If the propagation affects the residual sequence, we obtain an aberrant residual patch , or residual patch , corresponding to residual propagation, defined as a consecutive set of indices for which the residuals do not coincide with the structural errors et (ϕ0) and therefore do not conform to the underlying data-generating mechanism. In both cases, the patch is generated by dynamic propagation from a single outlier, even though no further outliers are present within the patch itself. Under AO contamination, the observed series differs from the uncontaminated series only at the outlying observation, so the level effect is local. However, the residual filter produces a residual patch in the residual sequence, that is, a set of indices over which the residuals do not coincide with the structural errors et (ϕ0). In an AR( p) model this residual patch has finite length determined by the AR order p. In contrast, when a moving-average component is present, the inverse moving-average filter generates a residual patch of infinite length. Under IO contamination the propagation pattern differs. In AR models the level effect propagates through the AR dynamics and produces a level patch of infinite length, whereas the residual effect remains local. In MA models the level effect is local, whereas the residual filter produces a residual patch of infinite length. # 3 Subsample-Based Estimation under Dynamic Contamina-tion 

In this section, we develop a general framework for subsample-based estimation under dynamic contamination. The framework applies to a broad class of residual-based subsample estimators and operates at the level of the propagation mechanism. The analysis is conducted within a causal and invertible VARMA representation, used as a broadly applicable weakly stationary operator framework in which residual propagation can be characterised explicitly. As shown in the previous section, contamination propagates through the residual filter and induces residual patches, so that its effect is not local in time. Consequently, robustness in dynamic models cannot be characterised solely at the level of individual observations. It must instead be analysed at the level of the residual-based criterion that defines the estimator. This observation leads to a structural requirement. For a subsample-based estimator to remain valid under dynamic contamination, the retained index set must be compatible with the propaga-tion structure induced by the model. In particular, it is not sufficient to exclude only the directly contaminated observations, since the associated residual distortions may persist beyond those time points. We formalise this requirement through a criterion-based framework defined on retained subsets of indices. Within this framework, we introduce a transformation of index sets that removes the resid-ual footprint of contamination and study the conditions under which it preserves the asymptotic behaviour of the estimator under clean data while restoring consistency under contamination.

In this subsection, we clarify the roles of the assumptions and indicate which of them require veri-fication in applications. The key objects are Assumptions 1 and 2, which govern the optimisation problem under clean data and can be verified under standard regularity conditions. Notably, the analysis does not require structural assumptions on the contamination process, such as indepen-dence, distributional forms, or temporal dependence. Instead, contamination enters only through its magnitude, its rate, and its propagation through the residual filter. Assumption 1 ensures that the sample criterion admits a well-defined local optimum, capturing identification and curvature in a neighbourhood of the estimator. Assumption 6 imposes regularity conditions on the criterion function that ensure that residual perturbations induce controlled perturbations of the sample criterion. It is a structural condition satisfied by standard residual-based criteria and is not the primary object of verification in the propositions below. This distinction explains why the verification step focuses on Assumptions 1 and 2. The opti-misation related assumptions determine the behaviour of the estimator under clean data, while the contamination-related assumptions determine whether the effective sample approximates this clean setting. Rather, they illustrate that the assumptions can be verified under familiar regularity conditions and connect the present framework to standard extremum estimation theory and existing subsample-based robust estimators. The assumptions are formulated at a high level to capture the structural features of the problem and to cover a broad class of settings, rather than to provide minimal conditions. For each specification, we report three estimators: the full sample estimator, the Huber-skip esti-mator without patch removal, and the corresponding estimator with patch removal. These corre-spond to different treatments of contamination. The full sample estimator serves as a contaminated benchmark. The Huber-skip estimator represents a standard robust approach based on pointwise trimming of outliers. The patch removal estimator augments this approach by accounting for resid-ual propagation. The comparison between Huber-skip and patch removal therefore isolates the effect of residual propagation, as any remaining discrepancy after trimming reflects contamination effects that are not removed by standard robust procedures. The trimming proportion α = 0.1 is chosen to allow for a moderate level of contamination, so that the retained subset is expected to be dominated by observations consistent with the underlying data-generating mechanism. For each (p, q), we report two versions of the information criterion: one based on the retained subset after patch removal, and one based on the full sample. The use of AIC avg is primarily heuristic, serving as a per-observation measure for comparing candidate models rather than as a formal robust model selection procedure. Several features emerge from the results. First, the patch removal procedure generally yields lower values of AIC avg , reflecting an improved Gaussian fit once extreme observations and their propa-gation effects are removed. Second, for certain specifications, such as (p, q) = ( 1, 2) and (2, 3), the information criterion becomes extremely large under both approaches, indicating numerical insta-bility in the estimated residual covariance matrix. This behaviour is not driven by subsampling or patch removal, but reflects the well-known sensitivity of VARMA models with MA components, which require nonlinear optimisation and may yield ill-conditioned estimates in finite samples. Such configurations are consistent with instability in the optimisation step rather than structural features of the data. Taken together, these patterns suggest that irregular values for higher-order specifications are driven primarily by numerical difficulties associated with the MA component, rather than by the subsampling or patch removal procedure. This distinction highlights that the proposed method primarily affects the statistical properties of the estimation criterion, whereas numerical stability is governed by the underlying VARMA structure. This indicates that standard robust procedures can be structurally misleading in dy-namic settings, whereas the proposed method directly corrects this deficiency. The empirical dif-ferences are consistent with the theoretical results, which show that pointwise subsampling does not, in general, eliminate contamination effects from the estimation criterion in dynamic models. # 7 Conclusion 

This paper establishes that subsample-based estimation, as commonly used in robust time series analysis, is generically inconsistent in dynamic settings when contamination propagates through the residual filter. Because estimation is based on residual criteria, such propagation distorts the objective function itself, so that subsample-based procedures fail to restore consistency in general, even under oracle knowledge of contamination locations. This failure arises at the level of the estimation criterion rather than from imperfect identification of contaminated observations. We identify a structural requirement for valid subsample-based estimation in dynamic models, namely compatibility with the propagation structure induced by the model. This requirement leads to a propagation-compatible transformation of index sets, formalised through the patch removal operator. The role of this operator is not to introduce a new estimator but to provide a propagation-compatible correction that restores the validity of subsample-based estimators under dynamic contamination. Under suitable conditions, it leaves the uncontaminated estimator asymptotically unchanged and ensures consistency for the clean-data parameter under contamination for a broad class of residual-based subsample estimators. The analysis also clarifies a key distinction across model classes: for 26 Yang and Sandberg Subsample-based Estimation under Dynamic Contamination pure VAR models the required correction is finite, whereas moving-average components generate propagation effects that may have infinite memory. These results have direct implications for empirical work. When outliers are suspected, inference should not be based on standard estimators applied to the contaminated sample, or on subsam-ple procedures that remove only the visibly aberrant observations. Instead, inference should be based on an estimator that remains consistent for the underlying clean process in the presence of contamination. This is particularly important when the aim is to assess structural features of the clean data generating mechanism, such as heteroskedasticity, nonlinearity, or model adequacy more generally. The paper also points to several natural extensions, which fall into four directions. First, at the level of estimation, an important extension is the estimation of the contamination mag-nitude ζt . This problem is inherently conditional on the one studied here, as meaningful estimation of contamination effects requires consistent estimation of the clean-model parameters. Further extensions concern the patch removal mechanism, including the use of heterogeneous trimming lengths across contamination events or across observations. While the present analysis shows that a uniform bound on κ suffices for consistency, relaxing this restriction may improve flexibility in practice. Second, forecasting is a natural extension. In many applications the primary objective is to predict the latent clean process xt , rather than the contaminated observations. Since contamination is typically sparse and does not reflect the underlying dynamics, forecasts should be based on a consistent estimate of the clean model, so as to capture the behaviour of the system in the absence of contamination. Third, an important direction concerns inference. Outliers and residual propagation induce a struc-tural distortion in estimation, under which standard hypothesis testing is conducted on estimators that are not consistent under contamination. Consequently, hypothesis tests may produce spurious statistical evidence, including misleading conclusions on the significance of model parameters and on structural features such as serial correlation, heteroskedasticity, regime switching, or nonlinear-ity, thereby distorting the interpretation of fitted models. By restoring consistency at the estimation stage, the present framework provides a basis for developing inference procedures that remain valid under contamination, including tests that account for residual propagation. Finally, there are extensions related to model evaluation and selection. In particular, the develop-ment of subsample-based information criteria tailored to this framework remains an open problem. The average AIC used in the present paper serves only as a practical device, and a principled criterion for contaminated dynamic models with patch removal requires separate analysis. These directions are important but raise distinct issues, and are therefore best studied separately. The present paper provides a foundation for such developments. There are also clear limitations. First, the analysis is developed under settings in which contam-ination is sufficiently sparse. This is not merely a technical restriction. When contamination is frequent or persistent, it becomes difficult to distinguish external outliers from features that belong to the underlying structural mechanism. Second, patch removal necessarily reduces the effective sample size. Consequently, finite-sample convergence cannot be faster than in uncontaminated full 27 Yang and Sandberg Subsample-based Estimation under Dynamic Contamination sample estimation, and is slower when contamination is substantial or when the required patch length is large. This cost is unavoidable if consistency for the clean parameter is to be preserved. For these reasons, the main practical recommendation of the paper is simple. If there are good reasons to suspect outliers in a dynamic time series, empirical conclusions should be based on an estimation procedure that is consistent for the clean model under contamination. The framework developed here provides a principled way to achieve this by correcting for residual propagation, thereby restoring the validity of subsample-based estimation in dynamic models.
