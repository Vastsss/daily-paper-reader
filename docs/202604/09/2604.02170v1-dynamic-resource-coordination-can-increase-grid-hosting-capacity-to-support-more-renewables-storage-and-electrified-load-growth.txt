# SUMMARY 11 

We show that dynamic coordination of distributed energy resources (DERs) can increase the 12 

capacity of low- and medium-voltage grids, improve reliability and power quality, and reduce so- 13 

lar curtailment. We develop three approaches to compute hosting capacity on a representative 14 

distribution grid with realistic scenarios. A deterministic iterative method provides insight into 15 

how dynamic operation and DER interactions enhance capacity and affect power flows, demon- 16 

strating clear gains over static methods even with low-to-moderate levels of storage and flexible 17 

demand. A stochastic programming approach jointly optimizes DER siting and sizing, show- 18 

ing that nodal colocation and complementary effects expand the feasible region of solar, heat 19 

pump, and battery penetrations by over 22 ×. This enables up to 200% solar, 100% battery, 20 

and 90% heat pump penetration. Batteries emerge as the most critical technology, followed by 21 

heat pumps and electric vehicles. A Monte Carlo–based extension shows that uncertainty sig- 22 

nificantly impacts hosting capacity and grid metrics, with 46% higher volatility under dynamic 23 

operation. 24

# INTRODUCTION 28 

In order to decarbonize the grid, we need to rapidly integrate renewables like wind and solar, in 29 

addition to battery storage. Furthermore, the electrification of other residential sectors like heat- 30 

ing and transportation is causing massive growth in electricity demand. We expect to see an 31 

additional 262 GW of distributed energy resources (DERs) in the distribution grid between 2023 32 

and 2027, which is comparable to the growth in utility-scale resources (+272 GW) at the transmis- 33 

sion grid level over the same period 1, and corresponds to billions of dollars in new investments. 34 

The current conversation tends to be dominated by concerns around transmission grid capacity 35 

and utility-scale interconnection, particularly with data center expansion. However, it is crucial to 36 

ensure that the distribution grid also has enough capacity to accommodate the growth in instal- 37 

lations of such small-scale resources. This paper explores how a distributed coordination and 38 

optimization approach can help the distribution grid reliably and affordably accommodate more 39 

DERs. We assume that all the DERs are autonomous and independently owned. Coordination 40

Hosting capacity (HC) refers to the maximum amount of DERs such as solar PV, battery 48 

storage, electric vehicles (EVs), and heat pumps (HPs) that can be integrated into the electric 49 

grid at a given location without causing reliability, power quality, or safety violations and without 50 

requiring major system retrofits or upgrades. It is typically expressed in either absolute or relative 51 

terms as a percentage of the peak load at that location. This is a critical metric for utilities and grid 52 

operators to assess the feasibility of integrating renewable energy sources and other distributed 53 

generation technologies, as well as new loads into the existing distribution grid. In order to 54 

determine the HC for additional DERs, we aggregate constraints related to voltage, thermal, or 55 

protection limits, over the whole distribution feeder and use it for high-level planning 2. 56 

HC may be limited by a variety of voltage, power quality, reliability, thermal, or operational 57 

constraints. Here, we will mainly be focusing on voltage and line thermal constraints. Overvolt- 58 

age and undervoltage limits are the most common voltage concerns for hosting capacity, since 59 

violating these upper or lower bounds can threaten grid stability. In particular, excess solar PV 60 

output during the middle of the day can lead to significant overvoltage issues. In addition to 61 

operational limits, utilities also prefer to maintain voltage profiles over the network as uniform 62 

as possible. Thus, undervoltage issues at the end of the feeder should also be avoided. This 63 

can make it more challenging to meet the massive demand growth that we’re seeing today due 64 

to increasing electrification, as well as emerging issues like data centers. In addition to voltage 65 

bounds, voltage imbalances (across phases) can also be a concern for unbalanced three-phase 66 

systems. However, for our initial case studies, this does not apply since we assume that the 67 

systems are either already balanced or we convert them to this form. Apart from voltages, the 68 

other major limiting factors are the thermal limits on maximum line currents, or equivalently, their 69 

maximum allowable apparent power flows. Finally, the HC can also be limited by the substation 70 

transformer’s rated capacity. Many utilities across the US are already severely HC-constrained, 71 

which delays or prevents the integration of new DERs in the distribution grid. The issue of hosting 72 

capacity is also closely related to the problem of renewables curtailment, wherein excess solar 73 

PV output in the middle of the day is increasingly being diverted or wasted simply because the 74 

grid does not have sufficient capacity to transport that power. This leads to overbuilding of capac- 75 

ity, increased capital costs, and higher electricity rates. Ideally, we would like to avoid curtailment 76 

of clean power and instead utilize PV output productively (e.g., to charge batteries/EVs). 77 

Related work 78 

Hosting capacity analysis (HCA) methods have evolved considerably over the past decade, pro- 79 

gressing from simple worst-case estimates to sophisticated scenario-based frameworks. Ex- 80 

isting methodologies can be broadly categorized into deterministic, stochastic/probabilistic, and 81 

time-series approaches 3,4 . In static or deterministic HCA, the distribution system is evaluated un- 82 

der fixed extreme conditions such as maximum PV output coinciding with minimum load, yielding 83 

conservative HC values that are computationally tractable but may not reflect operational reality 5. 84 

While these approaches provide a useful baseline, they fail to capture the variability inherent in 85 

renewable generation and demand, which can lead to either underestimation or overestimation 86 

of true hosting limits and potentially misguide investment and interconnection decisions. Recent 87 

2reviews emphasize that both steady-state and transient constraints should be included in HC 88 

studies, and that reliance on a single worst-case snapshot is increasingly untenable as DER 89 

penetration grows 3,6 . 90 

Time-series (quasi-static) methods extend static analyses by simulating the feeder over hours, 91 

days, or seasons at high temporal resolution, capturing diurnal and seasonal variability in load 92 

and generation 7,8 . For example, 7 and 9 evaluate hosting capacity at critical hours, i.e., midday 93 

solar peaks and evening load peaks, and generally find higher capacity than static worst-case 94 

analyses, since constraints such as overvoltage bind only intermittently. To simplify the anal- 95 

ysis, some studies adopt a worst-case time-period approach and consider only specific hours 96 

with peak PV output or peak load 3. Tavares de Oliveira et al. 8 further demonstrate that incor- 97 

porating time-varying medium-voltage background voltages with increasing penetration reduces 98 

the estimated hosting capacity by 32% on average, underscoring the importance of multi-day or 99 

year-long horizons to fully capture diverse operating scenarios. Moreover, these methods can 100 

be computationally intensive and, on their own, do not incorporate uncertainty in a principled 101 

stochastic framework. 102 

Stochastic and probabilistic methods address these limitations by explicitly modeling uncer- 103 

tainties in DER output, load profiles, and network parameters. Approaches include Monte Carlo 104 

simulation, probability distribution functions, and chance-constrained optimization, which yield a 105 

distribution of hosting capacity outcomes and can quantify the probability of constraint violations 106 

that enable risk-informed planning 4,10 . The authors in 4 conduct a systematic benchmark of de- 107 

terministic and stochastic hosting capacity definitions on a real semi-urban network, proposing a 108 

reference stochastic method that considers all sources of uncertainty as well as operational and 109 

probabilistic limits. Moreover, the authors in 10 cast probabilistic HCA as a chance-constrained 110 

optimization solved via Bayesian optimization, demonstrating a 25% improvement in hosting 111 

capacity with substantially less computation than traditional solvers. Mulenga et al. 9 further 112 

distinguish between aleatory (inherent randomness) and epistemic (knowledge-based) uncer- 113 

tainties in solar PV hosting capacity, using superposition and reduced time-domain sampling to 114 

efficiently capture both. The authors in 11 leverage multiparametric programming to dramatically 115 

accelerate probabilistic HCA, solving over 500,000 optimal power flow instances by computing 116 

only a small fraction of them. More recently, Lee et al. 12 introduce active learning into HCA 117 

to efficiently explore the vast space of DER integration scenarios, showing that hosting capac- 118 

ity and its interpretations change significantly depending on DER adoption patterns and control 119 

strategies. In general, probabilistic HCA provides more nuanced insight such as the probability 120 

of voltage violations, and higher capacity estimates by relaxing worst-case assumptions, but at 121 

the cost of much larger scenario sets. Critically, most probabilistic studies focus on assessment 122 

rather than optimization , and few integrate coordination mechanisms or market-based incentives 123 

into the analysis. 124 

A complementary line of work embeds control and planning decisions directly into the HCA, 125 

actively enhancing capacity rather than merely evaluating it. For instance, 13 proposes a frame- 126 

work based on optimization to determine PV hosting capacity considering autonomous voltage 127 

control strategies including active power curtailment, reactive power control, and on-load tap 128 

changer management, and demonstrates their effectiveness across 128 low-voltage UK feeders. 129 

Moreover, the authors in 14 propose a constructive geometric model for HC determination that 130 

preserves global optimality even with non-convex formulations, achieving larger hosting capacity 131 

values with less computation time than prior approaches. Wu et al. 15 develop a robust compre- 132 

hensive PV hosting capacity assessment model that considers both spatial and temporal correla- 133 

tions of PV output using ellipsoidal uncertainty sets, showing that considering these correlations 134 

significantly increases the estimated hosting capacity. A hierarchical bi-level model in which the 135 

lower level optimizes customer demand via demand response and dynamic tariffs, while the 136 

upper level simultaneously maximizes DG and EV capacity, achieving dramatic increases in al- 137 

3lowable DER penetration on both the IEEE 33-bus test system and a real 59-bus Cairo network, 138 

is proposed in 16 . The authors in 17 develop a bi-level framework that optimizes both capital and 139 

operational costs of grid expansion plans considering smart grid technologies and conventional 140 

upgrades, demonstrating that leveraging load-shifting flexibilities can defer costly asset replace- 141 

ments. These works collectively demonstrate that incorporating active network management 142 

such as reactive power support, topology control, and flexible load into HCA can substantially 143 

expand capacity relative to passive scenarios. However, most optimization-based HC studies 144 

consider only a single DER type (typically solar PV) and do not jointly optimize multiple DER 145 

categories within a unified framework. 146 

Coordination of DERs represents a further frontier. Whereas most prior HCA assumes au- 147 

tonomous DER operation, emerging studies quantify the benefits of coordinated scheduling. For 148 

example, 18 uses a power-flow-driven simulation and optimization framework to compare default 149 

local DER control against a perfect-foresight centralized controller and finds that central coordi- 150 

nation sharply reduces grid violations; for example, the fraction of overloaded transformers falls 151 

from approximately 81% under local control to 28% under centralized control by 2050, while peak 152 

load is reduced by 17%. Moreover, a hybrid volt-var control architecture leveraging distributed 153 

optimization for DER coordination in active distribution grids is demonstrated in 19 , showing that 154 

even in networks with existing voltage control devices, explicit DER coordination is necessary 155 

to maintain acceptable voltage profiles. Hwang et al. 20 present hosting capacity enhancement 156 

methods based on optimal price-based demand response of heating, ventilation and cooling 157 

(HVAC) systems in commercial buildings, using a bi-level decision model that considers both 158 

distribution system operator (DSO) profits and end-user costs. Recent advances also leverage 159 

data-driven approaches including machine learning, deep reinforcement learning, and physics- 160 

informed learning for real-time, adaptive DER control and decision-making 12,21 . However, most 161 

coordination studies focus on operational control rather than joint planning and design, and few 162 

explicitly quantify the resulting improvement in hosting capacity within an optimization framework. 163 

Finally, a growing body of work recognizes that hosting capacity is not only a technical limit 164 

but also a socio-economic and regulatory challenge. For instance, 22 highlights that existing grid 165 

constraints lead to inequitable DER access, with low-income and disadvantaged communities 166 

often facing much lower hosting capacity on their circuits. Gorman et al. 23 document severe grid 167 

interconnection bottlenecks at the bulk level, U.S. queues now exceed system capacity, with new 168 

requests facing long delays and high withdrawal rates. Xie et al. 6 warn that distribution equip- 169 

ment and protection schemes, designed for one-way power flows, will increasingly be violated 170 

under high DER penetration, even with flat demand profiles. Meanwhile, it is estimated that 171 

accommodating full electrification of housing and personal vehicles across the U.S. could re- 172 

quire up to 312 GW of distribution grid reinforcement at a cost of $183–$415 billion in 24 , though 173 

demand-side management can reduce these costs by up to 92%. Moreover, the authors in 25 174 

further demonstrate that fairness-aware dynamic hosting capacity approaches with strategic so- 175 

lar PV curtailment of no more than 5% can increase hosting capacity by at least 50% with net 176 

positive economic impact. These findings underscore that maximizing hosting capacity also re- 177 

quires addressing regulatory, planning, and equity dimensions — a context in which technically 178 

rigorous, optimization-driven HCA methods become all the more essential. 179 

Our contributions 180 

The majority of prior HC works have focused on maximizing only solar PV HC 2. Some works 181 

have assessed the HC of either EV, HVAC, or HPs 26 , but generally in isolation. However, to 182 

our knowledge, no prior work has studied in detail the HC of multiple DERs simultaneously on 183 

a given network. Our contributions, therefore, are the following. We conduct a comprehensive 184 

4HC analysis while considering all major types of DERs together, i.e., solar PV, electric vehicles, 185 

batteries, and heat pumps. Unlike most previous works which rely on a single approach, we 186 

propose, validate, and compare three distinct methods to estimate HC, both with and without 187 

uncertainty. We develop a novel, flexible framework to simultaneously co-optimize various DER 188 

types together, based on a two-stage mixed integer second order cone stochastic program. In 189 

each of the three approaches, we also accurately embed the device-level dynamics of DERs to 190 

capture their true flexibility and controllability. Finally, we improve computational performance of 191 

stochastic approaches using a combination of k-means clustering, Monte Carlo sampling, and 192 

warm starting the optimization process. Our multi-DER approach reveals critical insights into 193 

the relationships among different types of energy resources as well as their impacts on grid 194 

operation, in terms of voltages and currents. 195 

# RESULTS 196 

Input data 197 

The key inputs into our simulation are data related to DER power injections for both generation 198 

and load. For the demand side, this includes load profiles and temperature data, which influ- 199 

ences HP use. On the supply side, solar irradiation data affects the PV output. In addition to the 200 

baseline injection profiles, there are also distributions of parameters that influence the amount 201 

of flexibility (along with its temporal signature) that customers (or prosumers) are willing to pro- 202 

vide. These include both downward and upward flexibilities in generation and load. For the 203 

static cases without flexibility, we derived several representative scenarios and profiles for DER 204 

power injections from real historical data and smart meter measurements. These specify how 205 

prosumers operate their DERs in the nominal case 27 . It is especially important to have accu- 206 

rate estimates of net injection profiles for BS, EV, and HP operation, while PV output will follow 207 

irradiance profiles. We use real-time locational marginal price (LMP) data from the California 208 

Independent System Operator (CAISO) 28 , which influences how the DSO and agents balance 209 

importing power from the main transmission grid versus relying on the dispatch of local assets 210 

like batteries and EVs. The remaining parameters are generated randomly for each feeder node 211 

and assumed to be the same for all scenarios of inputs. For example, distributions of the key 212 

EV parameters can be obtained from actual load profiles of EV charging data. These include 213 

the unavailable time windows and state of charge targets for each EV. Our simulations are con- 214 

ducted using a version of the IEEE 123-node test feeder 29 . This synthetic network was modified 215 

to be representative of realistic distribution systems with high penetrations of renewables, stor- 216 

age, and flexible loads. Our approach can also be applied to other radial distribution networks, 217 

which represent the majority of utility feeders in the United States 18 . 218

For the static case, we use inflexible baseline profiles for fixed loads, heat pumps, batteries, 229 

and solar radiation profiles for PV. We derive each of these profiles from historical data and 230 

measurements. Considering many scenarios also allows us to move towards non-deterministic 231 

or probabilistic results as well. We assume that these injections are not flexible and that there’s 232 

no coordination or optimization among the DERs. We simply run the power flow model and 233 

check if the DER power injections are AC-feasible. The heat pump and electric vehicle baseline 234 

profiles are based on real-world data, while solar radiation profiles correspond to a location near 235 

San Francisco, CA. For the static battery charging or discharging profiles, we assume that the 236 

batteries are uncoordinated across nodes — batteries at each node are operated to maximize 237 

self-consumption while respecting state of charge (SOC) limits, but in a localized and myopic 238 

manner without considering the rest of the network or planning for future time periods. 239 

For the dynamic case, we leverage network-wide global coordination over the whole feeder. 240 

The dynamic approach consists of the following steps: 241 

1. Coordination of different types of DERs at each node. This determines the flexibility in 242 

power injections available, while respecting all device-level operational constraints and user 243 

preferences. 244 

2. Solve the network-wide multiperiod optimization problem. 245 

3. Use optimization solutions for market-clearing . 246 

4. Schedule or operate the DERs based on their updated dispatch setpoints (e.g., power 247 

output, temperature). 248 

This allows collaboration among different nodes rather than each agent just optimizing its own 249 

DERs locally. This is enabled by solving the global AC optimal power flow problem. This global 250 

optimization can be solved either in a centralized manner or by using distributed algorithms which 251 

are privacy-preserving and more computationally efficient 30 . A distribution-level retail market 252 

structure could be one approach to achieve this coordination, which would also allow us to 253 

compensate resources for their flexibility through pricing. However, we can also do this without 254 

markets, as long as there is infrastructure in place for communication and coordination among 255 

the different operators and agents. 256

with both BS and HP. This makes sense because excess PV output during the day can be utilized 377 

for both BS charging and to meet heating or cooling demand. The correlation between PV and 378 

EV is weaker since EV charging mainly dominates during time periods when solar PV generation 379 

isn’t available. BS is also strongly correlated with HPs since BS discharging can help support HP 380 

load during peak demand periods. However, BS and EVs are less strongly correlated since they 381 

both essentially function as energy storage devices and their charging and discharging profiles 382 

may not always align in a complementary fashion. Finally, we see that the weakest correlations 383 

are between HP and EV since these are both loads and may compete for power consumption 384 

during some periods, especially overnight. However, it’s important to underscore that their corre- 385 

lation is still positive, indicating that they can support each other, e.g., the HP can shift or curtail 386 

its demand to make more room for EV demand, or vice versa. 387

# DISCUSSION 525 

The current conversation largely takes the perspective that load growth and renewable growth 526 

pose a challenge for grid planning and operation due to the additional stress placed on infras- 527 

tructure and insufficient existing grid capacity. Expanding distribution grid capacity often requires 528 

interventions like upgrading substation transformers, increasing ampacity of lines, installing ad- 529 

ditional voltage regulators and capacitor banks, etc. It is also challenging to upgrade, retrofit, 530 

or build new infrastructure in many regions (especially the United States) due to the massive 531 

capital investment required, regulatory issues related to permitting and environmental laws, as 532 

well as opposition from local communities etc. 32 . These also cause significant delays and in- 533 

crease the cost of grid projects. On the other hand, dynamic coordination provides an appealing 534 

alternative by intelligently managing and optimizing resources and energy assets using virtual 535 

software-based solutions, to either replace or augment investments in new physical infrastruc- 536 

ture. A key takeaway from our results is that with dynamic coordination and colocation, as we 537 

increase penetrations of the different DER types, their complementarity benefits can become 538 

stronger and help reduce grid stress in terms of voltage and current constraints. In addition to 539 

boosting hosting capacity, this allows DERs to capture more economic value from participating 540 

in current and future market structures. 541 

However, implementing such real-time coordination mechanisms will require installing new 542 

sensing, monitoring, communication, and DER orchestration systems 33 . Although we didn’t con- 543 

duct a detailed cost analysis in this study, we expect that the potential benefits offered by coor- 544 

dination will likely outweigh such additional costs incurred. These monetary savings would arise 545 

from factors like reduced transmission grid dependence, lower losses and solar curtailment, and 546 

deferred or avoided grid upgrades. This places dynamic coordination of DERs alongside other 547 

data-based grid modernization strategies to optimize both transmission and distribution grids. 548 

There is also an increasing push from regulators and policymakers to encourage utilities and 549 

grid operators to adopt such technologies, primarily to reduce prices for ratepayers and move 550 

towards new electricity rate schemes like performance- or service-based regulation. This would 551 

further incentivize dynamic approaches for managing DERs. More broadly, rather than see- 552 

ing this electricity demand and generation growth as a challenge for the grid, we can reframe 553 

this as an opportunity. By intelligently incentivizing, managing, and coordinating DER flexibil- 554 

ity, and leveraging their complementary relationships, we can better accommodate this growth 555 

while also improving other outcomes like lower costs, congestion, curtailment, and losses. Such 556 

an approach that simultaneously co-optimizes for generation, load, and storage can also help 557 

accelerate the planning process and DER integration, significantly cutting down the time from 558 

19 commissioning to operation. 559 

Further developments and potential areas for future work 560 

In this study, we demonstrated the proposed framework on a medium-scale distribution grid, 561 

which allowed us to solve the stochastic programming problem by combining both stages into 562 

one large single-level formulation. This approach is reasonably fast for small to medium-scale 563 

networks, particularly when accelerated with scenario reduction and warm start. A natural and 564 

promising extension would be to apply this framework to larger networks with more scenarios, 565 

which would benefit from methods like Benders’ decomposition or SDDP. Adapting these de- 566 

composition methods to nonlinear problems with integer decision variables in the 2nd stage is 567 

an active research frontier — most studies rely on linear formulations, and stochastic MINLPs 568 

have only been tackled by a few recent works, generally considering integer variables in the 1st 569 

stage (e.g., unit commitment). Future extensions could leverage such algorithms to study larger 570 

test cases such as the IEEE 8500-node system across many more scenarios. 571 

A further avenue for enriching the framework is to incorporate greater realism by increasing 572 

the complexity of DER models, especially for EVs and HPs. More accurate thermal dynamics 573 

models for HPs could be considered as an alternative to our linear discretized model, while EVs 574 

could use more granular availability windows. Building on the heterogeneity in device parameters 575 

across nodes already included in this work, increasing the diversity in DER characteristics would 576 

further strengthen the generalizability of the resulting HC estimates. 577 

Another promising direction is to sample more accurate scenarios for the stochastic ap- 578 

proaches by considering probability distributions specific to each DER type, complementing our 579 

approach of deriving profiles from historical data under Gaussian noise. For instance, past 580 

works have explored beta distributions for solar irradiance, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck processes for 581 

electricity prices or load profiles, and exponential inter-arrival distributions for EV charging ses- 582 

sions. Finally, an exciting emerging opportunity is to explore the hosting capacity implications 583 

and coordination benefits of integrating small, modular data centers into distribution grids. Such 584 

edge-computing sites are becoming increasingly popular for latency-sensitive and localized ap- 585 

plications such as 5G, autonomous driving, and other Internet-of-Things (IoT) use cases — these 586 

sites also open new avenues for demand flexibility. 587

Defining DER penetration 589 

DER penetration is defined slightly differently depending on the type of DER. PV and BS pen- 590 

etrations are defined relative to the peak feeder load, with no upper limit or cap. HP and EV 591 

penetrations are defined in terms of the proportion (%) of homes/buildings in the feeder that 592 

have been electrified, and the upper limit is 100%. All air conditioners for cooling are already 593 

electric. But heating (both space and water) is largely still gas-based and will transition to elec- 594 

tric heat pumps. These heat pumps (either retrofitted or newly installed) can also act as cooling 595 

devices, and the average heat pump (HP) load for a home is about 5-6 kW. The average US 596 

house load is 1.2 kW with a peak load of 3-6 kW 34 . The base load (fixed) is 0.5-0.8 kW (from ap- 597 

pliances like refrigerators, standby electronics, etc.), while the peak load is from thermostatically 598 

controlled loads (TCLs) like ACs, heat pumps, water heaters, electric dryers, etc. In our IEEE 599 

123-node feeder configuration, there are about 8 houses (on average) connected to each node. 600

Deterministic iterative (DI) approach for HCA 687 

We first consider a deterministic case where we don’t account for uncertainty and only consider 688 

a single scenario of inputs. One simplification is to only restrict ourselves to a specific time of 689 

day, which corresponds to the worst possible inputs. For example, if we’re mainly focused on 690 

PV hosting capacity, we can consider just mid-day hours (e.g., 12-1pm) during peak PV output 691 

since the main concern is overvoltage due to excess generation 41 . However, while estimating 692 

HC for other DERs like EVs, BS, or HPs, we may also need to include other time periods since 693 

there are also risks of undervoltage (due to excess load) or line overloading. As summarized in 694 

Fig. 22, we implement an iterative approach where we gradually increase the DER penetration in 695 

small increments and run both the static and dynamic models until we hit power flow infeasibility. 696 

This allows us to keep track of maximum bus voltages and line loadings at each intermediate 697 

penetration level. Note that most papers based on iterative approaches focus on estimating the 698 

HC of only one type of DER at a time, e.g., maximizing PV only or HP only 26 . However, it is still 699 

relatively more computationally expensive and may not give as granular HC estimates due to the 700 

fixed step size increments at each iteration. 701

Two-stage stochastic program (2-SSP) for HCA 763 

A two-stage stochastic program (2-SSP) is a bilevel problem with the master problem being 764 

solved at the first decision stage and several sub-problems solved at the second operational (or 765 

recourse) stage, one for each scenario or realization. We combine both the 1st and 2nd stages 766 

into a single large-scale deterministic equivalent program. We then solve for separate sets of 767 

27 2nd-stage decision variables for each scenario, and the goal is to obtain solutions for the 1st 768 

stage decisions that are also feasible for the 2nd stage. This requires coupling between the two 769 

stages. Although such problems are infinite dimensional due to continuous probabilities, we use 770 

a discretized version of the 2-SSP to get a finite optimization problem, also known as sample 771 

average approximation (SAA). Sampling a large number of diverse scenarios (with Monte Carlo 772 

simulations) helps ensure that the finite case is still a good approximation for the infinite case. 773 

The HCA can be written as a 2-stage stochastic optimization problem with fixed recourse. 774 

In the 1st stage (upper level master problem), we choose the overall DER penetrations before 775 

scenario realization, and in the 2nd stage (lower level sub-problems), we run power flow simula- 776 

tions for all realized scenarios. The 1st stage decisions are the DER penetrations along with their 777 

locations and capacities at nodes. The 2nd stage problem is an ACOPF model that checks feasi- 778 

bility for each scenario ω ∈ Ω. Thus, the upper problem is concerned with design, planning, and 779 

investment — the grid planner has to make these decisions before seeing the input uncertainty. 780 

The lower problem is concerned with actual grid operation to set the power flow solutions and 781 

DER dispatch. These can only be decided after the uncertain input scenarios ω are realized. 782 

This results in a convex mixed-integer optimization problem. The advantages of such an ap- 783 

proach include (i) better computational efficiency, especially when considering non-deterministic 784 

problems over longer time horizons, and (ii) more flexibility for the decision-maker. 785

Solving 2-stage stochastic problems 881 

There are several approaches to solving 2-SSPs. Benders decomposition is one of the most 882 

commonly used approaches to solve two-stage stochastic programming problems 44,49 . It is 883 

a general decomposition approach for large-scale mixed-integer and continuous optimization 884 

problems. It decomposes the problem into a master problem and subproblems, iteratively solv- 885 

ing them to find the optimal solution. The algorithm iterates between solving the master problem 886 

and the subproblems, adding Benders feasibility cuts to the master problem (based on the dual 887 

solutions of the subproblems) to refine the solutions, until we converge to both feasibility and 888 

optimality. 889 

The L-shaped method is a specific application of Benders for 2-stage stochastic linear pro- 890 

grams 50 . The subproblems in the L-shaped method are normally linear programs, which allow 891 

the derivation of Benders cuts. If the second-stage problem contains integer variables, the dual 892 

function is no longer convex, which breaks the standard Benders decomposition and L-shaped 893 

approach. However, extensions have been proposed, such as the integer L-shaped method 894 

to handle integer programming subproblems by generating valid inequalities (also called inte- 895 

ger optimality cuts) instead of standard optimality cuts 51 . Generalized Benders decomposition 896 

extends this to nonlinear and integer-constrained problems by solving a relaxed master prob- 897 

lem and generating outer approximations instead of simple linear cuts 52 . There are also hybrid 898 

decomposition methods like combining branch-and-bound with Benders decomposition (Branch- 899 

and-Benders cut) 53 and scenario decomposition, where scenarios are solved separately using 900 

31 heuristics. The latter decomposes large-scale stochastic optimization problems into scenario- 901 

based subproblems, facilitating more efficient solutions 54 . The choice of method used depends 902 

on whether integer variables appear in the first stage, the second stage, or both. 903 

Stochastic dual dynamic programming (SDDP) is an extension of Benders Decomposition 904 

tailored for multistage convex stochastic optimization problems. It is particularly effective for high- 905 

dimensional problems with a large number of stages and scenarios. It operates by approximating 906 

the cost-to-go (or value) functions using cutting planes, enabling the handling of uncertainties 907 

over multiple stages. It involves a (i) forward pass that simulates or samples paths (forward in 908 

time) of the stochastic process variables to generate some trial (or candidate) solutions and (ii) a 909 

backward pass that uses the trial solutions to solve each of the subproblems backwards in time 910 

by generating Benders cuts that approximate the value functions 55 . 911 

In terms of computational complexity, SDDP’s sampling-based approach can be more effi- 912 

cient for problems with many stages, as it avoids explicit enumeration of all scenarios, which is 913 

often required in traditional Benders Decomposition. However, even so, the worst-case complex- 914 

ity of SDDP scales exponentially in the number of decision variables 56 . Extensions of SDDP to 915 

nonlinear 57 , mixed integer 55 , and mixed integer nonlinear programs (MINLP) 58 have also been 916 

proposed. However, it is quite challenging to adapt either Benders decomposition or SDDP for 917 

our specific problem since it is an MINLP with a complex structure that’s not readily amenable to 918 

either algorithm. The presence of integer variables in the 2nd stage (instead of the 1st) makes 919 

it difficult to apply such cutting plane methods since they rely on the convexity of the 2nd stage 920 

value function (which is nonconvex in our case). 921 

As a result, we decided to implement the 2-SSP ourselves from scratch natively in Julia using 922 

the JuMP package. Gurobi is able to efficiently solve such MISOCPs to guarantee global opti- 923 

mality as long as the problem is feasible, even though the optimal solution may not be unique 924 

(due to the presence of binary variables). While this approach has worked reasonably well for 925 

our moderately-sized problems, it may not be computationally practical for larger-scale problems, 926 

with many more decision variables and constraints. In future work while considering larger net- 927 

works with more scenarios, we will explore specialized solution algorithms that can exploit the 928 

special multi-stage and bilevel temporal structure of the problem. 929 

Accelerating 2-SSP for HCA 930 

One approach to improve the computational efficiency of this manual sweep approach is to re- 931 

duce the number of scenarios that need to be run. We can perform unsupervised machine 932 

learning methods to identify the most representative/characteristic scenarios and only run the 933 

simulation for these. It is crucial that this reduced scenario set must still include the most se- 934 

vere or extreme cases that will place more stress on the grid and make it challenging to satisfy 935 

constraints. 936 

As an alternative to using decomposition algorithms, we continued to use the deterministic 937 

equivalent approach, but instead used scenario reduction to accelerate the simulation. We ap- 938 

plied k-means clustering to first cluster all the profiles in the full scenario set. This clustering 939 

allows us to identify the most representative scenarios that capture most of the trends in the 940 

original timeseries data. The left plot in Fig. 24 shows how we applied the elbow method to iden- 941 

tify the optimal number of clusters for k-means. We see that the within-cluster variation stops 942

# CONCLUSION 955 

We showed that market-based dynamic coordination and real-time optimization of DERs can 956 

aid in both distribution grid planning and real-time operation, by utilizing the inherent flexibility 957 

available in distributed generation, energy storage, and loads at the grid edge. Leveraging com- 958 

plementary effects among different types of DERs can significantly boost grid hosting capacity 959 

over static, inflexible approaches, and such coordination can also improve network operation, 960 

power quality, and efficiency by reducing current flows and mitigating voltage violations. This 961 

supports growth in both demand and renewable generation, while keeping solar curtailment low. 962 

All DER types play a crucial role in regulating voltages and currents, and thus expand the space 963 

of power flow feasible solutions. We find that BS is the most important enabling technology due 964 

33 to its strong complementarity with both PV and HPs, followed by HPs with a slightly weaker ef- 965 

fect. Using a dynamic approach can integrate more DERs while minimizing grid stress and the 966 

need for new physical infrastructure or upgrades. This can also help circumvent long develop- 967 

ment timelines, clear DER interconnection queues, and accelerate grid decarbonization while 968 

also improving reliability and lowering costs for all stakeholders. Rapid DER integration would 969 

thus allow us to sustainably meet growing electricity demand from transportation, heating, indus- 970 

try, and computing. Finally, while dynamic approaches help improve overall outcomes, they can 971 

also magnify the effects of uncertainty, and this increased volatility would need to be carefully 972 

managed by regulators and grid operators. 973
