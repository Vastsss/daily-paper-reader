# From Net Load Modifiers to Firm Capacity: The Role of Distributed Energy Resources in Resource Adequacy 

Li, Y. ∗, Moreira, A., Heleno, M.

Abstract 

Distributed energy resources (DERs) such as rooftop solar, battery storage, and demand response offer substantial potential for power system reliability, yet integrating them into resource adequacy (RA) frameworks as firm capac-ity contributors remains difficult across jurisdictions. Existing analyses often treat these barriers as isolated technical problems at individual stages of the RA participation process, overlooking the cross-stage dependencies that prevent reforms at one stage from producing scalable participation. This paper introduces a four-gate compliance pathway (entry and classification, metering and verification, accreditation, and enforcement), preceded by an upstream forecasting layer, as a unified lens for tracing where DER capac-ity value is lost at the institutional interfaces between these stages. Using a document-grounded comparative synthesis of tariff provisions, compliance protocols, and regulatory documents across five jurisdictions spanning U.S. capacity markets and European capacity remuneration mechanisms, we show that these barriers persist despite substantial variation in market design and

regulatory structure, indicating that the problem is structural rather than jurisdiction-specific. We identify three cross-stage coupling mechanisms that explain why gate-level reforms have repeatedly failed to scale DER participa-tion, and derive coordination principles for end-to-end compliance redesign. The central finding is that compliance architecture, rather than DER tech-nology itself, is the binding constraint on translating DER capability into firm RA contributions.

1. Introduction 

1.1. Context and Motivation 

RA is formally defined as the ability of the electric system to supply the aggregate electrical demand and energy requirements of end-use customers at all times, taking into account scheduled and reasonably expected unsched-uled outages of system elements [1]. Historically, this objective was met in a paradigm dominated by large, centralized, dispatchable generators and 4relatively passive loads. Thus, adequacy assessments focused on ensuring sufficient firm generation capacity to meet aggregate demand under stochas-tic generator and transmission outages, typically within vertically integrated utility structures or CRM-based frameworks. However, this foundation is now under pressure from multiple direc-tions. On the supply side, accelerated retirements of aging thermal plants are shrinking the pool of conventional firm capacity, while increasing VRE penetration shifts reliability risk across more hours and seasons [2]. On the demand side, widespread electrification of transportation and heating sectors is projected to increase peak demand and alter load shapes, often stressing local distribution equipment [3]. At the same time, the rapid expansion of energy-intensive data centers raises both overall demand and the economic and societal cost of interruptions, effectively tightening reliability expecta-tions [4]. These trends are further compounded by more frequent and severe extreme events [2], which challenge historical planning assumptions and in-crease the frequency of hours where available capacity margins fall below reliability thresholds. Against this backdrop, DERs are reshaping both sides of the adequacy equation. DERs comprise a broad and heterogeneous class of smaller-scale resources located on distribution networks or behind the customer meter, including rooftop PV, battery storage, DR, controllable loads, and grid-interactive EVs [5]. When effectively coordinated, DERs can function as "load modifiers" that reduce system peaks and defer infrastructure invest-ments, for example when aggregated into VPPs to provide dispatchable ca-pacity and flexibility services [6]. Regulatory reforms such as FERC Order 5No. 2222 are opening formal pathways for DERs to participate in wholesale energy, ancillary service, and capacity constructs [7]. In principle, this cre-ates a new portfolio of resources that can support adequacy in systems with declining centralized firm capacity. However, realizing this potential requires overcoming fundamental mis-matches between DER characteristics and the structures of existing RA frameworks. Unlike conventional generators, DERs present challenges across the entire adequacy lifecycle from forecasting and planning, through inter-connection and accreditation, to market procurement and operational en-forcement. In forecasting, the adoption and operation of millions of behind-the-meter devices are driven by decentralized customer decisions rather than utility planning, creating substantial uncertainty in long-term net load tra-jectories [8]. For capacity valuation, DER performance is heterogeneous and state-dependent: battery contributions depend on distributed charge profiles, flexible loads exhibit rebound effects and participation fatigue, and demand response baselines are inherently counterfactual [9]. In deliverability assess-ment, distribution network constraints can prevent DER aggregations from responding during critical events even when they appear adequate in bulk-system models [10]. Finally, institutional barriers around registration eligi-bility, telemetry standards, and performance obligations determine whether potential DER capacity can be translated into enforceable commitments [11]. Traditional RA frameworks, designed around bulk generators, do not address these challenges well natively: forecasting uncertainty, state-dependent op-eration, distribution-level constraints, or fragmented registration, telemetry, and enforcement rules. 61.2. Research Gaps 

Existing literature has begun to address elements of this transition through three primary research strands. Research on planning and accreditation has developed foundational probabilistic methods for adequacy assessment [12, 13] and capacity accreditation techniques that move beyond static UCAP derating to ELCC-based valuation for intermittent generation, storage, and DR [14, 15, 16]. However, many of these analyses assume “copper plate” deliv-erability and abstract away from the institutional processes, such as market registration, telemetry verification, and performance obligations, that deter-mine whether analytically derived capacity values translate into contractually enforceable commitments. In parallel, control and optimization research has established the technical feasibility of DER aggregation and VPP operation, examining forecasting, dispatch algorithms, and performance verification at the distribution level [6, 17]. Yet these studies generally devote limited attention to the whole-sale market compliance layer, leaving a gap between demonstrated technical capability and actual market participation. A related strand of market design and policy analyses examines CRMs and reforms to enable DER participation [18]. While these reviews document regulatory barriers to entry, they typically treat resources as generic capacity blocks, overlooking how heterogeneous distributed portfolios face systematic filtering through rigid classification, verification, and accreditation rules de-signed for conventional generators. These disciplinary silos leave three critical gaps. First, few studies trace the full compliance pathway from forecasting assumptions to enforceable pro-7curement obligations, making it difficult to identify where technical DER capability is filtered out at institutional interfaces between planning, inter-connection, measurement, accreditation, and enforcement. Second, while regulatory intent such as FERC Order No. 2222 is well documented, system-atic cross-jurisdictional comparisons of operational requirements remain lim-ited, leaving many de facto participation barriers insufficiently characterized. Third, many analyses still treat implementation barriers as separable tech-nical problems, rather than as coordination problems in which constraints at one stage shape what is feasible at another. For example, optimization models maximize theoretical VPP flexibility without accounting for upstream entry rules that force assets into rigid categories, which makes that flexibility contractually invisible. This siloed perspective obscures the need for end-to-end design consistency across RA definitions, DER control and aggregation techniques, and regulatory frameworks. 

1.3. Contributions and Review Methodology 

To address these gaps, this paper makes three contributions: 

• First, we introduce a four-gate compliance pathway (entry and classifi-cation, metering and verification, accreditation and firmness, and obli-gations and enforcement), preceded by an upstream planning layer, as a unified analytical lens for DER integration in RA frameworks. Unlike prior analyses that treat DER barriers as isolated technical problems at individual stages, this framework shifts the focus to institutional com-pliance failures at the interfaces between stages, systematically tracing where and why capacity value is lost to institutional friction rather than physical limitation. 8• Second, through a document-grounded comparative synthesis across five jurisdictions spanning decentralized obligation frameworks, forward capacity markets, and reliability option mechanisms, this review sug-gests that many DER integration barriers are structural properties of CRM-based compliance architectures rather than artifacts of any par-ticular institutional arrangement. This finding is difficult to establish through single-jurisdiction analyses alone, and has direct implications for reform strategy: it indicates that jurisdiction-specific fixes will not resolve challenges whose causes are architectural. 

• Third, this review identifies three structural coupling mechanisms ex-plaining why isolated gate-level reforms have failed to scale DER par-ticipation, and derives three coordination principles, namely specify-ing inter-institutional information flows, establishing graded compli-ance options, and implementing adaptive accreditation updates, offer-ing design guidance toward a more coherent end-to-end compliance architecture. These contributions are sequentially structured: the first establishes an analytical lens, the second applies it to produce a cross-jurisdictional empir-ical finding, and the third explains that finding through structural mecha-nisms and derives actionable design principles. The analysis is grounded in a comparative regulatory synthesis across jurisdictions satisfying three criteria: (i) the presence of explicit resource adequacy constructs such as capacity markets or binding obligations; (ii) es-tablished wholesale market structures allowing third-party aggregation; and (iii) active regulatory proceedings regarding DER integration. 9Based on these criteria, the primary data corpus comprises technical docu-mentation from three representative U.S. capacity markets (California, PJM, and ISO-NE) and two European CRMs (Great Britain and Ireland). This review prioritizes operational documents, including tariff provisions, business practice manuals, and interconnection handbooks, cross-referenced with re-cent academic literature to contrast regulatory intent with implementation outcomes. A structured gate-by-gate comparison of practices and barriers across all five jurisdictions is compiled in Table S1 in the supplementary material (appended at the end of this document). The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews foundational RA principles. Section 3 establishes the DER taxonomy and introduces the four-gate compliance pathway. Section 4 assesses current practices and barriers at each gate. Section 5 identifies structural coupling mechanisms across compliance stages and derives coordination principles for scalable DER integration. Section 6 concludes the paper.

2.2. Reliability Metrics and Targets 

System-level RA assessment relies on quantitative metrics that describe the grid’s ability to meet aggregate demand under uncertainty of generation and grid failures. Methodologies have evolved from deterministic rules to probabilistic methods that capture the stochastic and chronological nature of modern power systems [14]. As systems began to include high penetrations of weather-dependent and energy-limited resources, probabilistic methods increasingly supplemented or replaced deterministic criteria such as fixed PRM to capture common-mode failures and chronological constraints [14, 20, 21]. Currently, system operators use these probabilistic metrics to translate chosen risk tolerance into explicit capacity requirements for both long-term planning and near-term RA procurement. While often expressed as a system-wide PRM for administrative simplicity, in many jurisdictions, PRM is increasingly treated as a derived output calibrated to achieve a target LOLE rather than as a fixed input based solely on engineering judgment. Furthermore, many jurisdictions disaggregate these requirements into multiple RA products. For instance, CAISO defines separate system, local, and flexible capacity obligations to ensure not only that total capacity is sufficient, but also that it is deliverable to load pockets and capable of following ramps and intra-day variability [22, 23]. These targets form the baseline against which resource contributions 12 are measured in subsequent accreditation and procurement stages. 

2.3. Resource Capacity Accreditation 

System-level adequacy analysis and resource capacity accreditation serve two distinct but complementary purposes. System-level studies based on metrics like LOLE answer the question, “How much capacity does the sys-tem need in total?” Capacity accreditation, in turn, answers, “How many megawatts from a specific resource can be counted as firm capacity toward that requirement?” In this way, accreditation provides the link between an abstract reliability target and resource-specific procurement and planning actions. Accreditation of a conventional thermal generator’s contribution, often termed UCAP , is calculated by de-rating its nameplate capacity with an 

EFOR to reflect availability probability [14]. While effective for dispatchable units, this approach is not well suited to VRE, because it treats resource availability as effectively uncorrelated and time-invariant. Hence, many ju-risdictions have moved toward resource-specific accreditation methods that combine chronological simulation with probabilistic system analysis. For VRE sources like wind and solar, system operators have increasingly moved from simple average or percentile statistics in peak hours [14] to ELCC-style studies that use stochastic, multi-period simulations of load, outages and weather to quantify each technology’s average or marginal contribution to meeting a LOLE-based reliability target [24, 25, 26]. For energy-limited resources like batteries, accreditation frameworks cap-ture duration constraints and state-of-charge constraints, so that 2-hour, 4-hour and 8-hour devices receive different ELCC or capacity accreditation 13 factors [2, 27, 28, 29]. For DR, accreditation often relies on baseline method-ologies and load impact studies combined with performance and availability factors intended to reflect limited call frequency and related persistence con-straints [30, 31]. Several jurisdictions are beginning to embed DR in prob-abilistic models used for variable generation and storage, so that accredited capacity more fully reflects both response variability and call constraints [15]. However, these probabilistic methods are computationally and data intensive, and often perceived as “black boxes” by stakeholders [2]. 

2.4. Market Frameworks and Regulatory Context for RA 

Reliability targets and accreditation methods operate within institutional structures that determine how capacity is procured and obligations enforced. Market restructuring beginning in the 1990s unbundled competitive genera-tion from regulated transmission and distribution, transforming RA from an internal utility planning exercise into a market coordination problem requir-ing explicit CRMs. Two broad institutional approaches have emerged, differ-ing in how procurement authority is allocated but sharing a common com-pliance architecture. In centralized capacity markets (e.g., PJM, ISO-NE, NYISO, MISO), system operators conduct forward auctions where resources compete to meet aggregate reliability requirements, with clearing prices and obligations determined through centralized optimization [32, 33, 25, 27]. In decentralized obligation frameworks (e.g., California), planning agencies forecast system needs and regulatory commissions assign capacity obliga-tions to LSEs, who must then bilaterally contract or self-supply resources while system operators verify deliverability and provide backstop procure-ment [23, 34, 35, 36]. 14 Despite these institutional differences, both models operationalize the five-component framework in Figure 1 through formalized compliance path-ways: resources must satisfy classification rules to establish eligibility, meet verification requirements to demonstrate capability, undergo accreditation to quantify capacity value, and accept performance obligations enforceable through financial or operational penalties. This formalized structure reflects design assumptions inherited from the vertically integrated era. In partic-ular, it presumes resources that are relatively large, centrally coordinated, and dispatchable; performance that is observable through transmission-level metering; and a planning-to-procurement workflow that can be treated as largely sequential [14]. These assumptions enabled clean separation between reliability assessment, capacity valuation, and market operations, but cre-ate systemic challenges for DERs whose characteristics diverge from this paradigm. 

3. The Role of DERs and Their Participation Pathways in RA Frameworks 

Traditional RA frameworks were designed for large, centralized, dispatch-able generators. While DERs offer opportunities for enhanced flexibility, reliability, and deferred infrastructure costs, realizing this potential requires overcoming fundamental mismatches between DER characteristics and legacy adequacy frameworks. This section defines DERs’ dual role as passive load modifiers and active capacity resources, then introduces a four-gate compli-ance pathway structuring their progression from eligibility to enforceable RA obligations. Figure 2 illustrates this framework and provides a roadmap for 15 the gate-by-gate discussion in Section 4. 

3.1. DER Participation Pathways and Their Dual Role in Adequacy 

DERs occupy two distinct positions within adequacy analysis. In their 

passive role, they alter the net load target that planners must cover. In their 

active role, they are explicitly accredited, settled and scheduled as capacity that satisfies that target. The same physical device can appear in either role, depending on contractual arrangements, telemetry, and regulatory au-thorizations. 

Passive DERs: Shaping the net load baseline. Long before active DERs par-ticipated explicitly in RA, a large share of adequacy assessments treated them as passive net-load modifiers . The canonical example is BTM genera-tion, such as rooftop PV or small CHP units, that reduces demand without explicit registration as a capacity resource or any direct control by the system operator. In the same family, energy efficiency programs such as HVAC and appliance replacement can reduce peak demand and overall energy use over extended periods. We use passive DERs as an umbrella term that includes energy efficiency programs, BTM generation used for self-supply, and other non-dispatchable measures that reduce net load in the background. In practice, many RA frameworks treat these resources primarily on the forecasting side: for example, ISO-NE’s FCM and PJM’s Reliability Pricing Model include demand-side resources whose contribution to adequacy is rep-resented primarily through load reduction rather than direct ISO dispatch [33, 32], while California’s CEC/CPUC framework models “load modifiers” such as additional achievable efficiency and BTM generation directly in the 16 net load forecast used for RA studies [35, 30]. Their contributions are thus re-flected in the net load trajectories and stress-hour definitions that determine adequacy targets, while explicit RA accreditation and performance obliga-tions are reserved for resources that can be actively committed and verified. 

Active DERs: Enrolled capacity resources with RA obligations. Building on the passive-active distinction above, we use active DER to refer to DERs that are explicitly enrolled, assigned an accredited capacity value, and subject to RA performance and compliance obligations. Existing RA frameworks use a variety of tariff-specific categories, but these are not consistent across juris-dictions and regions and often reflect legacy program history. For analytical clarity, we adopt a simplified functional grouping that aligns with how DERs interact with adequacy assessment and verification, rather than mirroring every jurisdiction’s tariff labels. First, we use supply-side DERs as an umbrella term for distribution-level resources that are able to inject power and are explicitly registered and metered as capacity resources. This family includes distribution-connected generators such as small CHP and community solar, front-of-the-meter bat-teries on distribution feeders, and aggregated BTM generation and storage that qualify as supply-like capacity. Within RA frameworks, these resources are typically treated as generator-like or storage-like assets: they contribute positive injections at a node and are typically subject to deliverability tests and generator-style telemetry and testing requirements [22, 24, 26, 28]. Second, we refer to load-modifying capacity resources as demand-side re-sources that primarily change the net load profile rather than injecting power. These resources reduce or shift load relative to a baseline when called upon 17 in scarcity conditions. They include demand response programs such as curtailable loads, as well as managed EV charging and other flexible loads whose consumption can be adjusted in response to system needs. In ex-isting RA frameworks, such resources are typically credited through base-line methodologies, load impact studies, and performance adjustments rather than through direct measurement of injected output [15, 30, 31]. Third, we define hybrid portfolios as systems and aggregations that com-bine supply-side and load-modifying capabilities within a single operational entity. Examples include co-located hybrid plants such as PV paired with storage, and multi-asset aggregations that coordinate BTM PV, batteries, thermostats, and managed EV charging across many customers [37, 38, 39, 40]. Many RA frameworks still require these portfolios to register under legacy categories such as generation, storage, or demand response, even where hybrid participation models are beginning to emerge [7, 41].

4.1. Gate 0: Net Load Forecasting and Planning 

As described in Section 3, Gate 0 is the upstream layer of the RA workflow where planners construct DER-aware net load futures and identify the hours of highest loss-of-load risk that anchor downstream capacity assessments. 

4.1.1. Current practices 

Jurisdictions with explicit RA constructs typically represent passive DER through exogenous adjustments to baseline demand. BTM solar, energy efficiency, EV charging, and distributed storage are represented as scenario-based load modifiers that reduce gross load to produce net load inputs for adequacy assessments [42]. The resulting hourly profiles inform capacity procurement targets and define reliability-critical hours. California’s IEPR exemplifies this approach by publishing multiple net load scenarios under varying DER adoption assumptions, including analy-23 ses of net-peak conditions, duck curve dynamics, and evening ramp stress under high electrification and BTM solar penetration [35, 43]. NERC’s LTRA similarly incorporates passive DER as load reductions while stress-testing adequacy under extreme weather and accelerated electrification [2]. European adequacy outlooks follow comparable approaches, incorporating country-specific passive DER trajectories [44]. In practice, these approaches often compress net-load uncertainty into a manageable set of discrete scenarios. Many planning processes define a reference forecast supplemented by additional scenarios, such as high elec-trification with accelerated EV adoption or low DER reflecting slower de-ployment. The number of scenarios typically ranges from three to six cases, each producing hourly net load profiles for multi-year horizons [42]. Relia-bility simulations then evaluate whether capacity requirements or risk-hour definitions differ materially across scenarios. 

4.1.2. Key challenges and design directions 

Passive DER deployment can outpace planning cycles. In high-adoption jurisdictions, BTM solar deployment has grown rapidly, shifting net load peak timing within single planning horizons [35]. Yet capacity procurement typically anchors to forecasts finalized one to three years before delivery [42]. When net load conditions evolve faster than forecast updates, downstream assessments may test capacity under outdated stress-hour definitions. For example, continued passive solar growth can shift the timing of net-load stress into later evening hours, such that capacity procured under earlier forecasts may be less well aligned with realized scarcity conditions [35]. One implication is that Gate 0 outputs may need to be structured as ver-24 sioned packages that document passive DER assumptions, stress-hour defini-tions, and when updates are warranted. Similarly, explicit refresh protocols, whether synchronized with planning cycles or triggered by observed devia-tions from forecasts, could help formalize when scenario updates propagate to downstream planning and procurement processes [42]. As passive DER deployment continues outpacing historical trends, these mechanisms become essential for preventing disconnects between forecasted and realized net load conditions, which can propagate into downstream gates. 

4.2. Gate 1: Entry and Classification 

Gate 1 establishes eligibility for RA participation through market/regulation qualification and resource classification. 

4.2.1. Current practices Market qualification and aggregation requirements. Resources must satisfy eligibility criteria defined by system operators, market administrators, or regulatory commissions. Key requirements include (i) minimum size thresh-olds, (ii) telemetry and control capabilities verified through testing, and (iii) coordination protocols among aggregators, distribution utilities, and system operators. For individual DERs below participation thresholds, typically 100 kW in U.S. markets, aggregation into portfolios is mandatory [7]. In U.S. organized markets, FERC Order 2222 mandates that ISOs and RTOs establish DER aggregation participation models with coordination re-quirements for distribution utilities on local reliability [45], while the EU’s Di-rective 2019/944 establishes access rights for independent aggregators while delegating DSO coordination to Member States [46, 47]. 25 Resource classification and participation pathways. Qualified resources are assigned to participation categories that determine applicable accreditation methods, performance obligations, and settlement rules. In practice, these qualification pathways often map onto three broad functional categories. 

Supply-side pathways apply to distribution-connected generators and front-of-meter storage. Capacity is demonstrated through measured injection and resources undergo deliverability screening similar to bulk generators. Demand-side pathways apply to load-modifying resources including demand response, managed EV charging, and controllable loads. Capacity is determined through baseline methodologies and performance factors rather than nameplate rat-ings. Storage or hybrid pathways recognize state-dependent operation or co-located configurations. Treatment varies by jurisdictions, with some frame-works providing specialized rules while others require qualification under supply-side or demand-side designations (details are summarized in Table S1 in the supplementary material, appended at the end of this document). 

4.2.2. Key challenges Fragmented approval processes. Coordination requirements across ISOs/RTOs, distribution utilities, and regulatory bodies create sequential approval bot-tlenecks. Distribution practices vary substantially across utilities, with dif-ferent interconnection agreements, screening criteria, and automation levels, producing wide variation in approval timelines. For instance, some distri-bution utilities apply automated fast-track screens for small inverter-based resources, while others require full power-flow impact studies regardless of asset size, with reported processing times ranging from weeks to well over a year for otherwise similar resources [11, 48]. For aggregators building port-26 folios across multiple service territories within the same ISO footprint, this heterogeneity creates a risk of partial qualification, where assets in slower-approving territories remain pending while others are already registered, pre-venting the portfolio from meeting the minimum capacity threshold required for a compliant RA filing. 

Category mismatch for hybrid and aggregated portfolios. Beyond fragmented onboarding, category-based frameworks create systematic barriers for hybrid and aggregated portfolios. Verification and testing regimes often assess per-formance at the asset or category level. In practice, however, response assur-ance in hybrid or aggregated portfolios is frequently delivered through portfo-lio controllers and energy management systems [37, 38]. This mismatch man-ifests in two ways. First, forced decomposition into supply-side, demand-side, or storage registrations can prevent accreditation of coordinated capabilities. A co-located solar-plus-storage system operates as a unified dispatchable re-source, but separate registration may not capture coordinated charging and discharging strategies. Second, best-fit classification can impose obligations that are poorly matched to actual portfolio behavior. A VPP aggregating batteries, thermostats, and managed EV charging may register as demand response to access baseline-based crediting but then face performance re-quirements designed for curtailable industrial loads rather than distributed state-dependent flexibility [39, 40]. 

4.2.3. Design directions 

Addressing these challenges requires streamlining fragmented processes and enabling flexible aggregation boundaries where distribution coordination 27 permits. 

Integrated workflows with standardized data exchange. Entry processes can be structured around shared digital infrastructure rather than sequential bi-lateral approvals. A unified DER asset registry could provide a common reference point for device identifiers, locations, and technical specifications, enabling reuse across distribution interconnection, retail enrollment, and re-source adequacy registration [49]. Standardized evidence packages define fixed templates for telemetry, controllability, and verification metadata that multiple parties can reference without duplicative submission. The U.S. DOE’s Energy Service Interface work demonstrates how digital protocols can operationalize these concepts by specifying standardized interfaces for DER registration and operational data exchange [50]. International best practices similarly emphasize digital interconnection platforms and harmonized data templates as enablers for non-discriminatory and efficient DER entry [51]. 

Portfolio-compatible classification frameworks. To address category mismatch, frameworks should enable portfolio-level registration where resources demon-strate integrated control and verification capability. Rather than forcing de-composition, classification rules can recognize a hybrid or aggregated port-folio as distinct participation models with tailored accreditation and obliga-tion structures. A portfolio-compatible approach would likely require at least three elements: (i) explicit portfolio registration options that treat the ag-gregation as the primary compliance unit with internal composition disclosed but not separately accredited; (ii) portfolio-level qualification tests that verify coordinated response capability rather than summing component de-rates; and (iii) flexible obligation structures that accommodate state-dependent 28 availability without defaulting to worst-case constraints [39, 40]. Emerging implementations in several jurisdictions demonstrate feasibility of portfolio-first approaches, though most remain pilot-scale rather than fully integrated into mainstream RA frameworks [37]. 

4.3. Gate 2: Metering and Verification 4.3.1. Current practices 

Gate 2 establishes what must be measured, at what resolution, and under what data governance constraints to verify DER capacity contributions. For RA purposes, measurement frameworks balance three objectives: sufficient granularity to detect non-performance, manageable implementation costs, and protection of customer privacy. 

Tiered measurement architectures. Because many DER categories cannot meet the continuous telemetry standards designed for centralized genera-tors, many frameworks rely on tiered measurement architectures to bridge the gap between legacy classifications and distributed physical realities. This approach typically operationalizes observability through three distinct mech-anisms: (i) telemetry exemptions for capacity-only resources, where opera-tors waive real-time SCADA requirements for smaller aggregations in favor of settlement-quality interval metering to reduce entry barriers [52, 33]; (ii) ex-post verification regimes that assess compliance retrospectively against scarcity events rather than through real-time monitoring [32]; and (iii) in-ferential visibility for load-modifying resources, where capacity contributions are estimated via counterfactual baselines or static peak contributions rather than direct physical measurement [32, 30]. Taken together, these arrange-29 ments suggest a practical tradeoff between broader participation and granular observability for resources not providing ancillary services. 

4.3.2. Key challenges The data-to-credit feedback loop. Tiered measurement creates an inference gap between observed aggregation-level performance and attributed device-level contributions. Resource adequacy assumes accredited capacity is firm and verifiable, but DER visibility is inherently inferential: compliance is statistically derived from baselines or sample-based metering rather than direct observation [30]. When visibility is coarse or baselines uncertain, the resulting accreditation often becomes more conservative, and participation may be limited to more restrictive product designs [53]. This feedback loop can suppress participation through a structural cost-value mismatch. High-fidelity telemetry capable of deterministic verification can impose per-site costs that, for many mass-market portfolios, may exceed the incremental capacity value they unlock [54]. Cost assessments confirm that device-level instrumentation scales poorly for mass-market portfolios, which can leave such resources confined to lower-value product categories [40]. The result is a tradeoff where RA frameworks must choose between broad participation with statistical uncertainty or narrow participation with granular observability. 

Cross-entity data fragmentation under privacy constraints. Adequacy verifi-cation requires combining data across aggregators, DSOs, and system oper-ators, but data governance creates a barrier distinct from the coordination issues at Gate 1. Privacy rules such as GDPR and CCPA can limit ISO access 30 to granular behind-the-meter data needed to validate baselines or audit com-pliance, even where technical visibility is otherwise feasible [46, 55, 56, 57]. This creates a practical black-box verification problem: aggregators hold raw performance data that procuring entities cannot audit, and current trust-but-verify audits lack scalability for high-penetration futures. European re-views consistently identify fragmented data governance as an important bar-rier to scalable DER integration, with Directive 2019/944 mandating non-discriminatory access while requiring data protection, creating operational tension between observability and privacy [58, 59, 60]. The core challenge is constructing verification protocols that prove capacity contributions without exposing individual device behavior. 

4.3.3. Design directions Formalized statistical verification with explicit uncertainty bounds. To ad-dress the observability-cost tension, one implication is that RA frameworks may need to treat statistical inference as a valid, audit-ready verification method rather than as a secondary exemption. Rather than demanding de-terministic telemetry from every device, frameworks can establish standard-ized protocols for sampling-based verification where compliance is proven via representative device subsets with quantified error bounds [61, 62]. Implementation requires making inference uncertainty explicit and au-ditable across tiered observability levels. For example, lower tiers could rely on using aggregation-level metering with statistical baselines should report capacity credits with disclosed confidence bounds or probabilistic de-rates [61]. Intermediate tiers could use employ sample-based telemetry from rep-resentative device subsets with population inference and quality flags [62]. 31 Higher tiers, particularly for resources providing ancillary services or exceed-ing specified size thresholds, would still be more likely to require real-time telemetry [22]. 

Privacy-preserving verification with role-based data access. To resolve the black-box verification problem, frameworks may increasingly need to shift from raw data extraction toward privacy-preserving computation. Emerging cryptographic techniques such as zero-knowledge proofs allow aggregators to mathematically prove reliability obligations were met without revealing gran-ular consumption data to system operators [63]. Recent legal frameworks are enabling this transition: the EU Data Act mandates that connected devices provide machine-readable data interfaces for third parties, creating digital portability rights that support decentralized verification without compro-mising privacy [60]. Operationalizing these principles would likely involve standardized inter-faces with role-based access: aggregators retain customer data, DSOs provide distribution-level delivery evidence, and system operators receive aggregated performance attestations [50]. Federated verification architectures enable cross-entity compliance assessment without centralizing raw data [64]. Cryp-tographic aggregation schemes enable regulators to verify compliance at the system level without accessing individual device data [65]. These mechanisms implement the principle that system operators need assurance of aggregate performance, not visibility into individual device states. The quality and granularity of verification data from Gate 2 directly conditions what accred-itation methodologies can achieve at Gate 3. 32 4.4. Gate 3: Accreditation and Firmness 4.4.1. Current practices 

Gate 3 accreditation largely inherits the technology-based registration and obligation logic established in Gate 1 entry and classification. Once resources enter the RA framework through class-specific rules, accreditation typically converts heterogeneous physical capability into standardized capac-ity credits using qualification tests and performance adjustments. 

Supply-side accreditation through reliability metrics and deliverability screen-ing. For supply-side resources in U.S. capacity markets, accreditation is in-creasingly tied to reliability contribution metrics such as ELCC, implemented through combinations of technology-specific de-rating curves, seasonal fac-tors, and test-based mappings from nameplate capacity to qualified capacity [22, 24, 26, 32]. Several jurisdictions have begun refining accreditation gran-ularity to reflect hybrid and co-located configurations and to incorporate true-up mechanisms based on realized performance [32, 66]. Because accredited capacity must be deliverable to load under system stress, Deliverability testing remains a parallel qualification requirement. It is assessed mainly through ISO and RTO power flow studies on transmission networks [67, 68]. Distribution constraints are typically addressed through utility interconnection and planning processes that run alongside RA, rather than through RA deliverability assessments directly. 

Demand-side accreditation through baseline methodologies. For load-modifying resources, accreditation remains anchored in baseline and performance method-ologies. Qualifying capacity is inferred from measured load reductions rela-33 tive to counterfactual baselines, with program-level adjustments based on his-torical performance and audit requirements [25, 30]. ISO-NE and MISO im-plement similar measurement and verification pathways with seasonal map-ping and adjustments tied to observed performance in tests and events [69, 70]. ELCC-style methods are starting to appear for demand-side resources in some jurisdictions, but, where they are used, they more often appear as class-level calibration layered on top of baseline-based nominations rather than as a full replacement for measurement-based accreditation [32, 71]. 

Portfolio accreditation through component aggregation. For mixed portfolios, participation is increasingly organized through VPP-like aggregations under one operational entity [40]. However, most RA frameworks still form portfo-lio credits by summing class-level accredited values rather than accrediting the portfolio as the primary unit [32, 72]. NYISO’s DER Aggregation model introduces performance-based capability testing at the aggregation level, re-quiring aggregators to demonstrate deliverable capacity in qualification tests [73]. However, underlying accreditation of individual technologies remains class-based, and no probabilistic or ELCC-style adjustments are yet applied. In IRP settings including California, chronological reliability simulations are increasingly used to evaluate composite portfolios, including portfolio-level ELCC representations for common resource mixes [71]. 

4.4.2. Key challenges Distribution-level deliverability gaps for supply-side DER. Deliverability as-sessments determine whether accredited capacity can physically reach load during system stress, yet current practice focuses primarily on transmission 34 constraints while omitting distribution-level limits [67, 68]. Feeder and sub-station constraints are handled through separate interconnection and hosting-capacity processes rarely integrated into RA deliverability frameworks [74]. Distribution studies show that bulk-only approximations can can materially overestimate export capability when time-series voltage and thermal limits are enforced, with export headroom varying widely across feeders with similar DER penetration [75, 76]. This separation creates two accreditation problems. First, capacity may be accredited for nameplate or test-based ratings but curtailed behind con-strained feeders during risk hours, overstating deliverable resources [77]. Sec-ond, static deliverability caps miss state-dependent coincidence between local congestion and system scarcity. Distribution limits that bind primarily dur-ing high solar output or moderate load may not constrain capacity during the extreme conditions that drive LOLE, yet uniform de-rates treat all hours equally [75]. 

Baseline-driven accreditation under behavioral stochasticity. For load-modifying resources, Gate 3 accreditation is based on baseline-based accreditation raises two main problems. First, baseline contamination: methods developed for infrequent, independent events, infer counterfactuals from recent non-event consumption, but as dispatch frequency increases, prior events, pre-event load shifting, and tariff-driven behavioral changes contaminate recent his-tory [78, 79]. Baseline algorithms can begin to internalize customer response patterns, causing estimated baselines to track event-day patterns rather than true no-event consumption. California LIP evaluations document this failure mode under frequent calls and dynamic pricing [30]. 35 Second, behavioral stochasticity correlated with RA risk conditions: de-mand response availability degrades across consecutive stress days due to participation fatigue and post-event rebound [2, 80, 81]. Managed EV charg-ing weakens when events cluster due to mobility constraints [82]. Yet frame-works typically accredit these resources using static capacity values averaged over historical events, rarely propagating fatigue, rebound, or clustering into LOLE and EUE calculations [16, 83]. The result can be systematic over-accreditation, with credited capacity exceeding actual availability during the multi-day stress periods that most threaten adequacy. 

Class-based accreditation of mixed portfolios. Applying class-based accredita-tion to mixed portfolios without modeling internal interactions can misstate deliverable capacity. Summing independent technology-level de-rates may fail to capture joint operating constraints. When storage, demand response, and flexible loads serve the same customers or operate on the same feeders, their availability during stress hours is correlated rather than independent. For instance, a battery may be depleted precisely when demand response is also fatigued after consecutive dispatch events, meaning the portfolio delivers less than the sum of individually accredited components [84, 85]. A related problem is double counting: PV-driven net load reductions depress demand response baselines, yet both receive separate capacity credits for effects that partially overlap [86]. 

4.4.3. Design directions Feeder-aware deliverability for distribution-connected resources. To address distribution-level deliverability gaps, Gate 3 accreditation may need to inter-36 nalize feeder and substation constraints rather than treating them solely as interconnection outcomes [74]. This does not require embedding full feeder physics in RA models. Instead, the aim is to define an auditable interface between distribution planning and RA by translating local constraints into reduced-form deliverability limits that are stable, updateable, and usable in LOLE and ELCC studies [87]. Probabilistic hosting-capacity studies quantify export limits under un-certainty in load, DER output, and contingencies, producing feeder-specific bounds or stress-hour de-rates applicable to accreditation [74, 75]. Loca-tional capacity-value frameworks developed for non-wires alternatives can map these constraints into zone- or node-specific adjustment factors, dis-tinguishing deliverable capacity from capacity stranded behind local bottle-necks [77]. Where detailed coupling is necessary, transmission-distribution co-simulation can evaluate upgrades and export implications as reduced-form constraints for reliability models [88, 89, 90]. 

State-dependent availability models for demand-side accreditation. For base-line construction, a natural direction is to move from single determinis-tic counterfactuals toward segment-specific baseline distributions informed by AMI and device telemetry, recognizing participant heterogeneity and program design heterogeneity [91, 92]. Published data-quality flags and aggregation-level error statistics make uncertainty auditable without expos-ing customer-level traces. For reliability modeling, chronological simulations can instead treat de-mand response as a stochastic process with state-dependent parameters driven by weather, event frequency, and dispatch history, propagating persistence 37 and rebound directly into LOLE and EUE calculations [31]. For accredi-tation, one option is marginal, risk-hour-weighted capacity crediting, which would calibrate program- and season-specific adjustments so that credits bet-ter reflect time-varying availability rather than static values [93]. 

Portfolio-level accreditation. To address joint operating constraints and dou-ble counting, mixed portfolios may be better accredited as primary units where feasible. Chronological reliability simulations with Monte Carlo sam-pling track joint state trajectories (energy limits, fatigue, and rebound) rather than summing independent technology de-rates, capturing the correlated availability that component-level accreditation misses [94, 95]. Emerging VPP evidence supports the possibility of calibrating portfolio response dis-tributions using AMI and device telemetry, enabling auditable updates to persistence assumptions across clustered events [40]. For valuation, portfolio-level marginal ELCC provides a single capacity value for net portfolio effects, which helps avoid ambiguous attribution when multiple components con-tribute jointly to overlapping net-load reductions [41]. Accredited capacity values from Gate 3 become the basis for binding obligations and enforcement at the final stage. 

4.5. Gate 4: Obligations and Enforcement 4.5.1. Current practices Technology-neutral obligation structures. Gate 4 translates accredited capac-ity into binding performance obligations assessed and settled during system stress. Many jurisdictions implement broadly technology-neutral enforce-ment frameworks. U.S. organized markets such as PJM and ISO-NE use an-38 nual capacity products with pay-for-performance settlement during scarcity intervals, while NYISO structures obligations around summer and winter capability periods [25, 32, 96]. California’s Slice-of-Day framework applies finer temporal granularity, translating adequacy needs into hourly showing requirements better aligned with storage and load-modifying characteris-tics [72]. European implementations follow broadly similar patterns: Great Britain and Ireland combine certification with reliability-option settlements triggered during system stress [97, 98, 99]. However, DER portfolios may participate across multiple programs simul-taneously and may also face distribution-level operating constraints during bulk-system scarcity hours, creating enforcement ambiguities that existing frameworks address through ad-hoc coordination rather than systematic de-sign. 

4.5.2. Key challenges Multi-program participation without unified accounting. DER portfolios pro-vide value across multiple markets simultaneously: wholesale capacity, retail demand response, ancillary services, and distribution-level non-wires alterna-tives [77, 100, 101]. However, existing frameworks lack mechanisms to track and reconcile overlapping commitments. Each program maintains separate enrollment, dispatch protocols, and performance assessments with limited visibility into concurrent obligations held by the same physical assets [102]. This fragmentation can undermine enforcement in at least two ways. First, the same controllable capability may be credited toward multiple tar-gets without de-rating for mutual exclusivity, creating risk that total credited capacity exceeds deliverable output when programs call simultaneously [100]. 39 Second, resources may be legitimately unavailable for one obligation because dispatched under another, yet system operators lack auditable records to verify priority or resolve conflicts, and standardized post-event processes for attributing delivery shortfalls across overlapping obligations remain limited [102]. 

Shortfall attribution under distribution network curtailment. While Gate 3 deliverability assessments establish prospective deliverability under typical network conditions, real-time distribution constraints during scarcity hours may prevent delivery for reasons independent of resource availability. Many distribution operators now offer non-firm or flexible connection arrangements permitting interconnection but requiring curtailment when local limits bind. Implementation models include Active Network Management in Great Britain, Einspeisemanagement in Germany, flexible export limits in Australia, and Non-Firm Access in Ireland [103, 104]. When distribution-directed curtailment coincides with bulk-system scarcity, prevailing pay-for-performance structures can expose aggregators to penalties for measured shortfalls without clearly distinguishing resource unavailability from network-directed curtailment [103, 105]. This creates dual problems: aggregators face penalties for outcomes outside their control, suppressing enrollment in high-curtailment jurisdictions, while system operators may re-ceive performance signals that confound resource reliability with network constraints. Both problems stem from the absence of auditable curtailment records and clear liability allocation between aggregators and network oper-ators. 40 4.5.3. Design directions Ex-ante obligation registries with priority hierarchies. Rather than prohibit-ing multi-use or allowing unrestricted stacking, a unified obligation registry would track all capacity commitments for each DER portfolio across bulk-system RA, distribution services, ancillary markets, and retail programs [7, 102]. Each commitment is tagged with temporal scope, priority level, and mutual-exclusivity constraints, enabling operators to identify potential over-commitment and aggregators to price participation risk appropriately. Implementation can be phased: (i) initial reporting maps stacking pat-terns; (ii) mandatory disclosure of primary reliability obligations during risk hours; and (iii) binding priority hierarchies determining which commitment prevails when calls overlap, such as bulk reliability over energy arbitrage or distribution safety over wholesale dispatch [99, 106]. Order 2222 re-quires RTO-distribution coordination, while Ireland and Great Britain spec-ify reliability-option hierarchies and force majeure provisions, though none fully integrate cross-program tracking with binding priority rules [7, 98, 99]. 

Real-time curtailment coordination with conditional excuse protocols. Curtailment-aware settlement would create a basis for real-time coordination between distribution operations and bulk-system performance assessment [103, 105]. Such an approach would likely require three elements: (i) DSOs log curtail-ment instructions in time-synchronized auditable format recording affected assets, magnitude, and triggering conditions; (ii) settlement rules condition-ally excuse verified curtailment up to specified thresholds while preserving aggregator incentives to maintain redundancy; and (iii) participation agree-ments clarify liability allocation, with aggregators responsible for understand-41 ing feeder-level limits and DSOs committed to transparent protocols [107]. 

Ex-post reconciliation with aggregation-level verification. Post-event settle-ment for multi-program participation would require reconciliation to at-tribute performance and resolve conflicts. While ex-ante registries define commitment hierarchies, actual dispatch during scarcity may deviate, requir-ing settlement without device-level traceability. An aggregation-level recon-ciliation ledger ties each scarcity hour to the binding obligation, metered portfolio-level quantity, and curtailment or program-call flags that adjust enforcement [108]. Reconciliation enables two enforcement improvements: distinguishing resource unavailability from competing commitments, and ver-ifying distribution-directed curtailment versus portfolio non-delivery during scarcity-hour shortfalls. 5. From Gate-Level Solutions to Cross-Gate Coordination 

Gate-level analysis reveals where DER capacity value is lost, but by it-self it does not fully explain why targeted reforms have often failed to pro-duce scalable outcomes. Addressing that question requires examining the structural dependencies that link compliance stages: rigidities at one gate propagate as binding constraints at others, such that solutions designed for individual stages remain incomplete without coordinated reform across the 42 pathway. This section identifies three such dependencies and derives the coordination principles they imply. 

5.1. Interdependencies Across the Compliance Stages 

These dependencies operate across three distinct interfaces: between en-try rules and obligation structures (G1 ↔G4), between verification methods and accreditation methodologies (G2 ↔G3), and between planning assump-tions and enforcement conditions (G0 ↔G3/G4). 

Category registration propagates mismatched obligations (G1 ↔G4). The clas-sification assigned at Gate 1 does not merely determine market access; it strongly shapes the performance obligations enforced at Gate 4. A port-folio registered as demand response inherits event-based curtailment obli-gations designed for interruptible industrial loads, regardless of whether its actual composition includes state-dependent storage or weather-correlated generation [39, 40]. This can create a self-reinforcing form of lock-in, whose persistence reflects a jurisdictional boundary rather than purely technical complexity. Classification rules operate under wholesale FERC/RTO au-thority, while retail contracts that shape actual obligations fall under state jurisdiction. Reforming G4 to recognize capability-based commitments re-quires G1 to first enable capability-based registration, yet wholesale-level G1 reform cannot compel corresponding changes to retail obligation structures across state jurisdictions. Order 2222 reveals this boundary clearly: it man-dates RTO-level access for heterogeneous DER aggregations, yet explicitly preserves state authority to prohibit demand response from being bid into wholesale markets by third-party aggregators [7]. Aggregators must attest 43 compliance with retail regulatory rules, but FERC cannot prescribe the con-tent of those rules. European CRMs exhibit an analogous split: wholesale classification rules and distribution-level connection terms are governed by separate authorities, so that capacity market prequalification cannot con-strain DSO curtailment decisions [103, 105]. The result is expanded access within a categorical structure that remains mismatched to hybrid resource capabilities. 

Verification granularity bounds capacity crediting (G2 ↔G3). The capacity value a resource can claim at Gate 3 is structurally bounded by the observ-ability established at Gate 2, but the coupling mechanism is more specific than a simple data quality constraint. Class-level ELCC accreditation, the prevalent methodology in several major U.S. capacity markets, assigns iden-tical capacity credits to all resources within a technology category regard-less of individual performance [24, 26]. This is a deliberate design choice: class-level averaging provides investment signal stability and administrative simplicity, but it also means that individual metering quality is largely irrel-evant to accreditation outcomes by construction. A DER aggregation with high-resolution device-level telemetry receives the same capacity credit as one relying on aggregator-reported baselines, because the accreditation method-ology pools both into the same class average. The structural trap is therefore not that better G2 data cannot be collected, but that G3 has no pathway to consume it: transitioning to resource-level accreditation requires G2 to pro-vide auditable individual-level performance records as a precondition, while investment in G2 upgrades remains economically irrational as long as G3 assigns class-level credits regardless of data quality. In that sense, reforms at 44 either gate are likely to remain limited unless the other changes as well. 

Forecast vintage misaligns accreditation with enforcement conditions (G0 ↔G3 /G4). Gate 3 accreditation studies are anchored to net load forecasts and stress-period definitions established at Gate 0, while Gate 4 enforcement oc-curs against realized system conditions that may have diverged materially. The temporal gap is structural: procurement cycles operate on multi-year horizons by design, while passive BTM solar deployment evolves on sub-annual timescales [2, 35]. Specifically, ELCC-based accreditation assigns capacity credits based on historically defined tight hours; when BTM pen-etration shifts net-load peak timing, these hours no longer correspond to Gate 4 enforcement triggers [42]. Resources accredited against one set of tight-hour conditions may face performance obligations under later net-load peaks that differ materially from the conditions used to derive their cred-its. Adaptive refresh mechanisms that propagate observed BTM deployment into stress-period redefinition are structurally absent from current procure-ment frameworks, because forecast updates and accreditation recalculations operate on separate administrative cycles with no binding synchronization requirement. The misalignment can therefore become self-reinforcing, be-cause forecast updates and accreditation recalculations operate on separate administrative cycles with no binding synchronization requirement [42]. 

5.2. Coordination Requirements for Scalable Integration 

The three coupling mechanisms identified in Section 5.1 each implicate a distinct coordination failure: an absence of binding protocols at jurisdictional interfaces, a methodological decoupling between verification and accredita-45 tion, and a temporal misalignment between planning assumptions and en-forcement conditions. Addressing them likely requires design principles that operate across gates rather than within them. 

Specify institutional interfaces explicitly (G1 ↔G4; G0 ↔G3/G4). Both cou-pling mechanisms share a common structural feature: the information flows between relevant institutions are often informal or incomplete rather than fully codified. Resolving the G1 ↔G4 jurisdictional boundary would require establishing a mechanism through which retail regulatory authorities inform RTOs of which retail programs are excluded from wholesale participation, which is information that is currently neither required nor standardized across state jurisdictions [7, 11]. Resolving the G0 ↔G3/G4 temporal mis-alignment requires specifying what deviation thresholds in observed BTM deployment trigger an accreditation review, and in what format forecast up-dates must be provided for accreditation models to consume them [42]. Cod-ifying these interfaces in tariffs and operating procedures may not require full jurisdictional realignment; it may instead require existing institutional actors to publish standardized outputs at defined intervals, converting infor-mal expectations into enforceable obligations. 

Establish graded compliance options (G2 ↔G3). The G2 ↔G3 coupling cou-pling is unlikely to be resolved through observability improvements alone, because no existing accreditation methodology treats verification precision as an explicit input to capacity credit calculations. Recent reforms demon-strate that markets can move beyond pure class-level pooling: MISO’s FERC-approved Daily Loss of Load methodology introduces resource-level perfor-mance differentiation from the 2028/2029 planning year [26]. However, this 46 differentiation is based on historical availability during tight hours, not on the auditability of the underlying data. A DER aggregation whose perfor-mance record derives from aggregator-reported baselines receives identical accreditation treatment to one backed by device-level telemetry, provided re-ported availability figures are the same. Breaking the coupling would require G3 methodologies to explicitly discount capacity credits where G2 produces unauditable inputs, and to reward higher-precision verification with differ-entiated credit tiers; but this is only feasible if G2 produces standardized precision declarations that G3 can consume. 

Integrate adaptive refresh mechanisms (G0 ↔G3/G4). California’s Slice-of-Day framework represents the most developed attempt to address temporal misalignment by disaggregating capacity obligations across hours of the day, yet procurement targets remain anchored to forecasts finalized years earlier [72]. This gap illustrates why adaptive refresh is a distinct design require-ment from product disaggregation: revising the shape of obligations without revising the conditions under which accreditation was performed leaves the G0 ↔G3/G4 misalignment structurally intact. Operationalizing adaptive re-fresh would require three codified elements: deviation thresholds specifying when observed BTM deployment diverges sufficiently from planning assump-tions to trigger a review; standardized data formats enabling forecast updates to propagate into accreditation models without full planning cycle restarts; and institutional assignment specifying which body initiates the review and within what timeframe. Partial antecedents for each principle exist within current frameworks, but they address different layers of the coordination problem and have not 47 been pursued jointly. Institutional interface specification requires regulatory action at the federal-state boundary; graded compliance restructuring re-quires methodological reform within RTO tariffs; adaptive refresh requires administrative re-engineering of planning cycles. The absence of a mechanism that links these three layers helps explain why progress on any single dimen-sion has not yet translated into scalable DER participation, and suggests that scalable integration will likely depend on institutional actors assuming coordination obligations that current market designs do not yet require. 

6. Concluding Remarks 

This review introduces a four-gate compliance pathway as a unified ana-lytical lens for tracing how technical DER capability is filtered through the institutional processes governing RA participation. Applied across five juris-dictions, the framework suggests a consistent pattern: capacity value is often lost at the interfaces between forecasting, classification, verification, accredi-tation, and enforcement, and this pattern persists despite substantial hetero-geneity in market design and regulatory structure. The cross-jurisdictional consistency suggests that many of these barriers are structural features of CRM-based compliance architectures rather than artifacts of any one insti-tutional arrangement. Three coupling mechanisms help explain why gate-level reforms have often been insufficient to resolve them: classification rules shape obligation structures across a jurisdictional boundary that wholesale reform does not easily cross; accreditation methodologies often make verifi-cation precision only weakly relevant by design; and planning cycles accredit resources under conditions that may no longer align with those reflected in 48 enforcement. The compliance pathway framework points toward three research direc-tions. In energy-only markets such as ERCOT, where accreditation is implicit in scarcity pricing rather than codified in forward compliance rules, elements of the framework may still be informative: the structural dependencies be-tween classification rules and obligation structures, and between planning assumptions and scarcity price formation, operate through the same mech-anisms identified here, yet they remain undercharacterized because existing analyses treat energy-only and CRM-based markets as analytically distinct. Within the jurisdictions examined here, a further question remains: which combinations of coordination reforms create the most favorable conditions for scalable integration, and how early-mover jurisdictions may generate spillover effects that accelerate reform elsewhere. Empirical validation represents a third priority, with the G2 ↔G3 structural trap offering the most tractable test case. If class-level accreditation is largely insensitive to verification pre-cision, then aggregator-reported and directly metered resources within the same class would be expected to receive similar capacity credits, a pattern that market outcome data could help confirm or refute. These directions share a common premise: the barriers documented here are not static. As DER deployment accelerates and compliance frameworks struggle to keep pace, the structural dependencies identified across the compliance pathway may become more consequential rather than resolving on their own. In that sense, realizing the adequacy potential of DERs depends increasingly not only on what these resources can do technically, but also on whether the institutions governing their participation evolve to reflect those capabilities. 49 Acknowledgements
