# Risk-Aware Allocation of Transmission Capacity for AI Data Centers 

Shaoze Li 1, Bohang Fang 1 and Cong Chen 1

Abstract ‚ÄĒ Rapid growth in AI-driven data center loads is creating significant challenges for transmission grid intercon-nection. This paper proposes robust and risk-aware frameworks to quantify transmission capacity as firm and flexible capac-ities. We efficiently solve the robust optimization problem to determine firm capacity when minimizing unserved data center demand. Building upon this, we introduce a risk-aware alloca-tion for flexible capacity, showing that tolerating a minimal probability of service interruption and blackout can unlock substantial flexible capacity of transmission networks and accelerate data center interconnection. To efficiently allocate scarce transmission capacities among competing data centers, we adopt the simultaneous ascending auction, characterizing products by capacity, risk level, and location . Under additive or symmetric concave valuation functions, the auction converges to a competitive equilibrium and achieves efficient allocation. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) has elevated data centers to critical infrastructure, driving an unprecedented surge in electricity demand [1]. In the United States, data centers accounted for approximately 4.4% of to-tal electricity consumption in 2023, with this share expected to reach 6.7%‚Äď12% by 2028 [2]. Because grid expansion is slow and capital-intensive, a structural mismatch is emerging between rapidly increasing loads and limited transmission capacity, leading to prolonged interconnection queue times for new data centers [3]. To accelerate data center interconnection, both grid op-erators and data centers are driving a shift from traditional 

firm capacity to flexible capacity . Historically, grid opera-tors have allocated firm capacity by reserving transmission network capacities for worst-case peak demand, resulting in substantial under-utilization of the grid [4]. Meanwhile, data centers‚ÄĒtraditionally viewed as inflexible 24/7 loads‚ÄĒare increasingly exploring operational flexibility. For example, Google has committed to providing 1 GW of demand re-sponse capacity [5], enabled by workloads such as batch processing and AI model training that can tolerate low-probability interruptions without compromising overall ser-vice quality [6]. By leveraging data center flexibility, grid operators can adopt flexible capacity allocations, thereby accelerating interconnection and alleviating queue delays. Extensive literature has explored how the existing grid can accommodate growing AI loads. While recent optimization-based approaches have explored device coordination strate-

Notably, [9] and [4] show that even infre-quent interventions involving flexible loads can substantially increase the total load served by transmission networks. However, these studies do not explicitly incorporate proba-bilistic operational risk, leaving the interaction between risk-aware flexible capacities and underlying network constraints under-characterized. Besides flexible load, another challenge is allocating scarce network access among competing data center develop-ers. Traditional queue-based regimes process interconnection requests chronologically [10], while recent reforms employ batch or cluster-based procedures that consider multiple requests simultaneously and weigh project readiness more heavily [11]. These reforms reduce speculative requests, withdrawals, and repeated restudies, yet both remain fun-damentally non-market mechanisms, allocating access by procedural priority rather than revealing the heterogeneous valuations that data centers place on scarce capacity. To unlock transmission network capacity, mitigate inter-connection queue delays, and address the competition among data centers with diverse valuations, we first quantify the firm and flexible capacities within the transmission network. Then we introduce an auction-based mechanism to allocate scarce withdrawal rights among data centers. Specifically, we adopt the Simultaneous Ascending Auction (SAA), a well-established multi-item auction in public-sector resource allocation, most notably in spectrum auctions [12]‚Äď[14]. SAA can discover prices and allocate scarce withdrawal capacity according to data centers‚Äô heterogeneous valuations, thereby improving the efficiency of resource allocation [12]. Our primary contributions are two-fold. First, we propose a robust optimization-based allocation to determine the firm capacity under strict physical network constraints. As established in Theorem 1, this optimization not only minimizes the unserved demand ratio for data cen-ters but also maximizes the total available capacity. Building upon this, we introduce a risk-aware allocation method for the flexible capacity . This approach demonstrates that accept-ing a marginal risk of blackout can unlock substantially more data center hosting capacity compared to firm capacity. Second, we design an efficient auction-based market mechanism to allocate firm and flexible capacity. Each bid-ding product is characterized by three attributes: (capacity, risk level, location) for the transmission network access. We apply the SAA to allocate these firm and flexible capacity products to competing data centers. Furthermore, we provide theoretical guarantees that if the data center bidders‚Äô value

B. Risk-aware Model for Flexible Capacity 

Problem (1) presented in subsection II-A derives a with-drawal capacity that guarantees network stability across all possible scenarios. However, by strictly accounting for low-probability extreme events, this approach inevitably yields overly conservative capacity estimates. Consequently, this severely restricts the grid‚Äôs available hosting capacity for the surging power demands of modern data centers. From the perspective of data center operators, tolerating a marginal risk of blackouts is often an acceptable trade-off in exchange for larger power allocations and expedited grid interconnection. This flexibility is largely due to the operational characteristics of data centers: non-real-time or flexible computing workloads can be deferred during periods of grid stress. Furthermore, temporary and low-probability power interruptions can be effectively mitigated using on-site or rented energy storage systems. Therefore, it is reasonable for data centers to accept a small risk of power outages in order to unlock substantially greater hosting capacity. Consequently, we introduce the following risk-aware model to extract extra flexible capacity. The conditional value at risk (CVaR) is adopted as our risk measure because it not only controls the probability and the magnitude of constraint violations, but also requires no restrictive assumptions on the underlying uncertainty distribution.

In Section II, we use a robust model to unlock the firm capacity of the transmission grid and a risk-aware model to unlock flexible capacity. However, even with the additional flexible capacity unlocked, aggregate supply remains insuf-ficient to fully satisfy the surging demand from data centers. Therefore, the next challenge is to efficiently allocate these scarce resources. In practice, data center developers assign different values to establishing a data center in a specific region depending on their core business operations. Data center developers with higher valuations are willing to pay a premium for withdrawal capacity because they view data center expansion as critical to their future growth. Even at a higher purchase price, they can still maintain a high net utility. Consequently, it is essential to allocate this available capacity efficiently based on the data center developers‚Äô private valuations. To achieve this, we employ the SAA, a well-established mechanism widely utilized in the public sector to allocate this withdrawal capacity. Next, we first introduce the fundamental procedures of SAA and then detail the theoretical analysis. 

A. Simultaneous Ascending Auction 

The SAA is an auction for multiple items in which bidding occurs in rounds. At each round, data center developers submit bids simultaneously for any items in which they are interested. The capacity bidding item is defined as a tuple characterized by three attributes: capacity, risk level, and location. Building on model (1) and (4) in Section II, we determine both the firm capacity and an additional flexible capacity with a risk level for each location. Consequently, for each candidate location, we construct two distinct indivisible bidding products: 1) Firm capacity cfi represents capacity with zero risk. It is formulated as the tuple (cfi , 0, i ), where cfi denotes the firm capacity, 0 indicates the risk level, and i

represents the location index. 2) Incremental flexible capacity cci represents additional flexible capacity subject to the predefined outage risk 

r. It is formulated as the tuple (cci , r, i ), where cci is defined as cri ‚ąí cfi .It is worth noting that the firm capacity cfi and the incremental flexible capacity cci could be partitioned into smaller and discrete indivisible units, similar to spectrum auctions [17]. This allows the capacity at a single location to be distributed among multiple data center developers by SAA. However, to ensure a clear exposition of the bidding process, we focus on the formulation that the capacities at each location are not further subdivided in the remainder. During any given round, data center developers submit their bids based on their private value. Importantly, any new bid must meet a minimum bid increment, which is computed by adding a predetermined bid increment ŌĶ to the item‚Äôs cur-rent standing high bid. Once the round closes, the auctioneer posts the bidding results. For each capacity item, the new standing high bid becomes the maximum of the previous standing high bid and the highest new bid in the current round. The bidder who placed the highest bid is recorded as the new standing high bidder. In practical applications, some supplementary mechanisms, such as activity rules, are often enforced to promote the auction.
