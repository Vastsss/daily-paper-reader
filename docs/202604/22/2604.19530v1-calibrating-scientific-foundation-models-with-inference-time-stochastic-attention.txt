# Calibrating Scientific Foundation Models with Inference-Time Stochastic Attention

Abstract 

Transformer-based scientific foundation models are increasingly deployed in high-stakes settings, but current architectures give deterministic out-puts and provide limited support for calibrated predictive uncertainty. We propose Stochastic At-tention , a lightweight inference-time modification that randomizes attention by replacing softmax weights with normalized multinomial samples controlled by a single concentration parameter, and produces predictive ensembles without re-training. To set this parameter, we introduce a calibration objective that matches the stochastic attention output with the target, yielding an ef-ficient univariate post-hoc tuning problem. We evaluate this mechanism on two scientific founda-tion models for weather and timeseries forecasting along with an additional regression task. Across benchmarks against uncertainty-aware baselines, we find that Stochastic Attention achieves the strongest native calibration and the sharpest pre-diction intervals at comparable coverage, while requiring only minutes of post-hoc tuning versus days of retraining for competitive baselines. 

1. Introduction 

Scientific modeling has already had to confront an uncom-fortable fact: some of its most accurate modern predictors are mechanistically opaque. Across scientific domains, deep learning models are no longer viewed merely as flexible function approximators, but as increasingly useful comple-ments to classical modeling pipelines. In weather forecast-ing, for example, recent learned systems have shown that data-driven models can rival, and in some cases surpass, long-standing forecasting workflows on prominent bench-marks (Fig. 1a) (Nguyen et al., 2023). What this shift has not yet resolved is how such models should be trusted when uncertainty matters. If scientific practice is willing to accept

The challenge is not simply to attach uncer-tainty to deep models, but to develop forms of uncertainty that remain useful under the very conditions that make these models successful: heterogeneous data, noisy and misspeci-fied settings, and internal computations whose strength is real even when their mechanics are not fully transparent. That gap is easy to acknowledge in principle, but harder to formalize in a way that is actually useful. A common intu-ition is that if predictive distributions look reasonable under standard scoring rules or if intervals achieve nominal cov-erage, then uncertainty has been “handled.” We argue that this view is incomplete. Even in a simple setting such as a sinusoidal relation, models that recover the same underlying trend can still express very different kinds of uncertainty:one may be sharply overconfident, another calibrated but too diffuse, and a third more usefully balanced (Fig. 1c). This is not uniquely a scientific-modeling problem; it is a more general difficulty in uncertainty quantification that becomes especially consequential in scientific prediction. Useful un-certainty therefore cannot be reduced to a single score. It lives at the intersection of accuracy, calibration, sharpness, and cost: a model may be accurate yet systematically over-confident, calibrated yet too diffuse to support action, or sharp only because it understates risk (Gneiting et al., 2007; Kuleshov & Deshpande, 2022; Gruber & Buettner, 2022). This multi-objective structure is precisely what makes un-certainty difficult to reason about, and it helps explain why the literature has produced many promising tools without a unifying pattern for what “useful” uncertainty should look like in practice. The field has approached this problem from two largely separate directions. One line of work focuses on how uncer-tainty is evaluated, relying on proper scores, coverage-based diagnostics, or calibration metrics as proxies for reliability (Guo et al., 2017; Gneiting et al., 2007; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Another focuses on where and how uncertainty is introduced, through output-level recalibration, weight-space approximations, ensembles, or stochastic forward-pass mechanisms (Guo et al., 2017; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Maddox et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2024; Heo et al., 2018; Pei et al., 2022). 2. A lens for evaluating uncertainty in scientific foundation models 

In practice, uncertainty quality is often compressed into one favored score, one calibration statistic, or one probabilistic formalism. That compression is convenient, but it obscures the structure of the problem. What scientists actually need from uncertainty is more demanding: the central predic-tion must remain useful, the predictive distribution must be statistically trustworthy, the reported intervals must be informative rather than vacuous, and the overall procedure must remain realistic at the scale of modern transformer-based models. Throughout this paper, we organize that requirement through four complementary axes: accuracy ,

calibration , sharpness , and cost .

2.1. Accuracy, calibration, and sharpness are complementary 

Accuracy, calibration, and sharpness are often invoked to-gether, but they do not collapse into one another. Accuracy concerns the quality of a representative prediction, typically the predictive mean or another point summary under a task-relevant loss. Calibration concerns whether the predictive distribution is statistically consistent with realized outcomes in the long run. Sharpness concerns the concentration of that predictive distribution and therefore whether the reported uncertainty is informative rather than vacuous (Gneiting et al., 2007; Kuleshov & Deshpande, 2022). The key point is not simply that these notions differ, but that they can pull against one another. A method may re-tain strong point accuracy while remaining systematically overconfident. Another may achieve nominal coverage only by broadening its intervals until they become practically uninformative. A third may look attractive under a sharp-ness summary precisely because it understates risk. Fig-ure 1b is meant to make that non-collapse visible: accuracy, calibration, and sharpness define distinct desiderata, and improving one of them does not automatically improve the others. Figure 1c makes the same point in a deliberately simple setting, where predictors recovering the same un-derlying sinusoidal relation still express qualitatively dif-ferent uncertainty—calibrated and sharp but inaccurate, ac-curate and calibrated but diffuse, accurate and sharp but miscalibrated, or balanced across all three. In that sense, uncertainty quality is genuinely multi-objective rather than reducible to a single scalar target. This perspective is consistent with the probabilistic forecast-ing literature, where calibration and sharpness have long been treated as complementary rather than interchangeable properties of predictive distributions (Gneiting et al., 2007). It also clarifies why uncertainty can remain unsatisfying even when the central prediction is strong: a good mean forecast is necessary, but it is not enough. 

2.2. Cost is part of the decision 

For scientific foundation models, cost belongs in the same coordinate system. This is not a practical footnote added after the statistical discussion. Uncertainty methods differ not only in the distributions they induce, but in the training runs, posterior approximations, checkpoint collections, or repeated stochastic passes they require. In large-model settings, those differences can determine whether a method is realistically usable at all. That distinction matters because some of the strongest base-lines derive their gains from substantial computational in-vestment. Deep ensembles require repeated model training (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). SWAG and MultiSWAG approximate predictive uncertainty through trajectory- or checkpoint-based posterior structure (Maddox et al., 2019; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020). Variational Bayesian methods such as IVON enrich the stochastic training objective, again at the cost of additional optimization complexity (Shen et al., 2024). These are meaningful baselines, but they do not occupy the same operating point as a lightweight inference-time mechanism. A method that improves a scalar score only after multiple additional training runs is not solving the same practical problem as one that achieves useful cal-ibration with minimal internal overhead. Cost therefore belongs to the uncertainty-design problem itself, not as an afterthought, but as part of what makes one uncertainty method practically preferable to another. 

2.3. When and where randomness and calibration happen 

Once uncertainty is viewed through these complementary axes, it becomes natural to ask how current methods actually intervene. One useful way to organize the landscape is by 

when uncertainty or calibration happen and where they act. The first distinction is temporal. Some methods introduce uncertainty during training, for example by learning weight-space posteriors or stochastic optimization dynamics that alter the model itself (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Shen et al., 2024). Others act at inference time, producing predic-tive variability through repeated stochastic forward passes, checkpoint sampling, or architectural stochasticity (Maddox et al., 2019; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Heo et al., 2018; Pei et al., 2022). A third class leaves the model untouched and instead calibrates uncertainty after prediction, as in tem-perature scaling or conformal-style procedures (Guo et al., 2017; Kuleshov et al., 2018; Vovk et al., 2005; Ye et al., 2024). The second distinction is structural. Randomness or calibration may be applied at the output level, in weight space, or inside the model’s computation. 3Calibrating Scientific Foundation Models with Inference-Time Stochastic Attention 

These choices are not interchangeable. Output-level recal-ibration can improve uncertainty statistics without chang-ing how the model internally routes information. That is the logic behind temperature scaling and conformal wrap-pers: they reshape reported uncertainty after the predictor has already done its work (Guo et al., 2017; Vovk et al., 2005). Weight-space methods and ensembles intervene much more globally, often at substantially higher compu-tational cost, by averaging over parameters, trajectories, or multiple trained models (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Maddox et al., 2019; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Shen et al., 2024). Stochastic forward-pass approaches sit somewhere in between, injecting randomness into the network itself while largely preserving the trained predictor (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). Within that class, attention-level stochasticity is qual-itatively different from indiscriminate perturbation because it acts at a computational bottleneck that directly governs how information is routed (Heo et al., 2018; Pei et al., 2022). Organizing the literature this way does more than tidy up cat-egories. It shows that methods differ not only in strength but in mechanism: they intervene at different stages of the pre-dictive pipeline and perturb different objects. It also clarifies a practical point that is often treated as an embarrassment rather than a fact of current modeling practice. In many large-model settings, predictive accuracy is established first and uncertainty is calibrated or attached afterward. We do not regard that separation as a conceptual failure. The more relevant question is whether the second-stage uncertainty mechanism remains tied to the computation in a meaningful way, rather than merely wrapped around its outputs. That is the question that motivates the later focus on the attention bottleneck. 

2.4. Proper scores are useful, but not the whole story 

This brings us to the role of metrics. Proper scores are valu-able because they provide compact summaries of predictive quality and reward alignment between forecast distributions and realized outcomes. In practice, however, they are often asked to do more conceptual work than they can bear. A lower negative log-likelihood, CRPS, or related score can reflect improved uncertainty, but it can also reflect increased spread, altered sharpness, or other changes that are diffi-cult to disentangle directly (Gneiting et al., 2007; Gruber & Buettner, 2022). In other words, these scores blend together aspects of uncertainty quality that the present paper is trying to keep distinct. That does not make proper scores unimportant. On the contrary, we report them precisely because they remain in-formative summaries. But they are not sufficient as the sole definition of useful uncertainty. A method can improve a blended score and still land at an undesirable operating point from the perspective of calibration or practical infor-mativeness. This matters especially in highly parameterized models operating under misspecification, shift, and noisy su-pervision, where a predictive distribution can become more diffuse without becoming more meaningful. For that reason, we treat proper scores as complementary to, rather than replacements for, calibration diagnostics. Throughout the paper, PIT-based diagnostics and coverage-based views play the primary role in assessing probabilistic consistency, while sharpness summaries and proper scores help characterize how that calibrated uncertainty is being achieved. At that point, the next question becomes unavoid-able: if useful uncertainty does not follow automatically either from a single metric or from adding stochasticity somewhere in the model, then by what principle should the amount of stochasticity be chosen? Section 3 introduces the mechanism, and Section 4 turns to that calibration problem directly. 

3. Stochastic attention 

If uncertainty is to reflect plausible internal variability rather than arbitrary perturbation, attention is the natural place to introduce randomness. Our starting point is that determin-istic attention already admits a probabilistic interpretation: the softmax weights form a simplex-valued distribution over tokens, and the corresponding attention output is its expec-tation. Stochastic attention replaces that exact expectation by a finite-sample approximation drawn from the same cate-gorical distribution. This yields a one-parameter family of stochastic predictors indexed by a concentration parameter 

ν, with small ν producing more dispersed attention out-comes and large ν recovering the deterministic computation. The construction is probabilistic in a frequentist rather than fully Bayesian sense: the model parameters remain fixed, while repeated attention-space sampling induces an implicit predictive distribution around a frozen transformer.

This also makes the scope of the construction precise. Stochastic attention is introduced only at inference time; we do not retrain the transformer, and we do not optimize through discrete samples. The backbone is first trained or fine-tuned for predictive accuracy, and the attention-space randomness is calibrated afterward. Accordingly, stochastic attention should be viewed neither as output-only recalibra-tion nor as posterior inference over all network weights, but as a post-hoc probabilistic mechanism inside the transformer computation itself. Two Monte Carlo quantities play distinct roles. The con-centration parameter ν controls the stochastic distribution itself by determining the amount of attention-space variabil-ity within a single forward pass. By contrast, M controls only how accurately we estimate summaries of the predic-tive distribution induced by that mechanism. Thus, ν is the method’s operating parameter, whereas M is simply the evaluation budget used to estimate predictive summaries. Taken together, this defines a one-parameter family of stochastic predictors {fθ,ν }ν∈Ξ around the frozen determin-istic backbone fθ , where Ξ ⊆ N is the admissible search domain for ν. The remaining question is how to choose the operating value of ν.

4. Choosing a calibrated operating point 

The concentration parameter ν should be chosen by what we want the predictive distribution to achieve, not by heuristic noise tuning. Under the lens of Section 2, useful uncertainty is not determined by randomness alone, but by a balance among accuracy, calibration, sharpness, and cost. In sci-entific settings, that balance is especially consequential: under-dispersion yields overconfident predictions, while over-dispersion yields uncertainty that is statistically safer but operationally uninformative. Both are calibration fail-ures, and both matter in practice. For that reason, we treat calibration as the first requirement for selecting the stochastic-attention operating point. Proper scores remain useful and are reported later, but they blend calibration with predictive spread, so the operating point that optimizes a single scalar score need not be the one with the clearest probabilistic calibration. Our goal here is therefore not to re-optimize model parameters, but to choose ν so that the predictive variability induced by stochastic attention is com-mensurate with the residual variability observed on held-out data.

Its scope should nonetheless be stated clearly. We do not claim that this objective is a universal calibration rule for all predictive tasks. It is designed for continuous regression settings, where residual magnitudes and predictive disper-sion are naturally compared in a common metric space. It is therefore best understood as a calibration principle for the specific one-parameter stochastic family induced by stochas-tic attention, not as a replacement for all proper scoring rules or all probabilistic training objectives. This also clarifies the main design trade-off. A single shared concentration parameter ν keeps the method lightweight and easy to calibrate, but it limits expressivity across heads, layers, coordinates, and inputs. We take that trade-off de-liberately here: the goal is not to build the most expressive possible probabilistic model, but to identify a calibration-first operating point that remains simple enough to deploy while preserving the strength of the deterministic backbone in expectation. 

4.3. Optimization and computational role of ν

In practice, LSA (ν) is evaluated on the held-out calibration split Dcal . For each candidate ν ∈ Ξ, we estimate the inner expectation using repeated stochastic forward passes of the frozen model. As in Section 3.3, ν controls the stochastic predictive distribution induced by attention-space sampling, whereas the number M of repeated stochastic forward passes controls only the Monte Carlo accuracy with which summaries of that distribution are estimated. When all stochastic attention layers share a single concen-tration parameter ν, LSA (ν) becomes a one-dimensional calibration problem. We optimize it using the BO-under-uncertainty routine of Yadav & Zhang (2025), which fits a Bayesian generalized linear model surrogate to noisy evalu-ations and analytically proposes the next candidate ν; full details are deferred to the appendix. In practice, each BO iteration evaluates a single proposed ν on mini-batches from 

Dcal using M stochastic forward passes per batch. Com-bined with the one-dimensional search and analytic BO suggestion step, this makes ν-selection fast in practice and far cheaper than retraining-heavy uncertainty baselines. 

5. Experimental design 

5.1. Models and tasks 

The experimental design mirrors the paper’s central premise: uncertainty quality should be read through several comple-mentary dimensions, and different empirical settings are useful for different parts of that argument. We therefore do not present the datasets as interchangeable benchmarks. TimesFM on the ETT-small datasets (Das et al., 2024) serves as a diagnostic forecasting setting. It is univariate, visu-ally interpretable, and well suited to showing what under-dispersion, over-dispersion, and more useful uncertainty ac-tually look like. In practice, it is also a difficult fine-tuning regime because the adaptation data are limited, so we use it primarily as a transparent testbed rather than as the quanti-tative center of the paper. ClimaX is the flagship scientific case: a structured, high-dimensional forecasting problem in which uncertainty must remain meaningful under realistic scientific demands, and where the practical stakes of calibra-tion are clearest (Nguyen et al., 2023). FT-Transformer on UCI regression then broadens the scope beyond forecasting, using the standard Gal-style splits to provide a compact and reproducible transformer regression setting (Gal & Ghahra-mani, 2016). Full training, split, and implementation details are deferred to Appendix C. 

5.2. Baselines 

In tandem with the established notion that uncertainty meth-ods differ not only in strength, but in where they intervene and how they generate predictive variability, we compare against weight-space methods, ensemble-style methods, and stochastic forward-pass or architectural methods. SWAG and IVON represent two different routes to weight-space uncertainty, while MultiSWAG plays the role of a strong ensemble-style baseline built from repeated SWAG training runs (Maddox et al., 2019; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Shen et al., 2024). That choice is intentional: MultiSWAG is not merely another competitor, but a representative of the broader family of expensive ensemble-based uncertainty methods that often set a high empirical bar. We also include MC Dropout as a lightweight stochastic forward-pass base-line (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), Contextual Dropout as a stronger context-dependent dropout alternative (Fan et al., 2021a), and HSA as the closest attention-level stochastic baseline, since it injects uncertainty inside attention rather than only through weights or outputs (Pei et al., 2022). Ad-ditional baseline summaries are provided in Appendix G. 

5.3. Metrics and diagnostics 

We report point accuracy, calibration diagnostics, sharp-ness summaries, and score-based investigations. Point accu-racy remains necessary because an uncertainty method that substantially weakens the underlying predictor loses prac-tical relevance. Calibration is assessed primarily through PIT behavior, empirical coverage, and, where appropriate, Wasserstein-1 distance to the uniform distribution. For scalar targets, PIT is computed from the empirical pre-dictive CDF induced by repeated predictive samples. For structured outputs such as gridded ClimaX forecasts, PIT is computed coordinate-wise and then aggregated across coordinates and test cases; the exact computation for each setting is given in Appendix C. Coverage and W1 then provide compact summaries of the same phenomenon. Sharpness is summarized through interval widths and related spread statistics, but it is never interpreted in isolation. A narrow predictive interval is only useful once the associated predictive distribution is already credible; otherwise, appar-ent sharpness may simply reflect under-dispersion (Gneiting et al., 2007; Kuleshov & Deshpande, 2022). We also report proper scoring rules and related ablations as complementary investigations. For UCI regression this includes ensemble CRPS, while for ClimaX, we report marginal CRPS and en-ergy score. These are informative because they test whether calibration-first operating points remain competitive under blended distributional scores (Gneiting et al., 2007; Gruber & Buettner, 2022). 

5.4. Fairness and cost protocol 

The comparison protocol separates native uncertainty qual-ity from post-corrected uncertainty quality. As already em-phasized, sharpness is not meaningfully comparable when calibration differs substantially across methods, making this distinction essential. The first comparison is therefore al-ways native. Stochastic attention remains native throughout because its operating point is chosen by a calibration-first criterion; once that native calibration is established, its sharp-ness can be interpreted directly in end-to-end comparison. For baselines whose native predictive distributions are mate-rially miscalibrated, we also report post-scaled comparisons where appropriate, not to blur the distinction, but to show what happens once calibration is brought to a more compa-rable level. This lets later sharpness and score comparisons be read as differences in uncertainty quality rather than arti-facts of obvious under- or over-dispersion as presented in Sections 6 and 7. Cost is treated in the same practical spirit. Retraining-heavy baselines, checkpoint-based ensembles, and lightweight inference-time mechanisms do not occupy the same deploy-ment regime, even when evaluated on the same task. We therefore interpret uncertainty quality together with the cost required to obtain it. 6. Results on scientific forecasting 

6.1. TimesFM: a diagnostic view 

Before turning to the flagship scientific case, it is useful to see why calibration-first evaluation matters visually. Fig-ure 2 shows native predictive intervals for all six methods on one TimesFM case (ETTh1, H=96 ). SA achieves na-tive coverage of 61 .5% , far from the 95% target but already closer than any baseline (IVON 15 .6% , SWAG 16 .7% , Mul-tiSWAG 20 .8% , Contextual Dropout 19 .8% , HSA 38 .5% ). Baselines are not merely imperfect; they are systematically under-dispersed. Narrow intervals that miss most outcomes are not sharp; they are overconfident. The correspond-ing equal-coverage visualization, where all baselines are rescaled to SA’s empirical coverage for a fair sharpness comparison, is in Appendix E. This is the structure that the rest of the evaluation must navigate. 

6.2. ClimaX: native calibration at the attention bottleneck 

ClimaX is where the calibration-first lens lands most clearly. Figure 3a shows native PIT histograms for all six methods. SA produces a near-uniform distribu-tion ( W1=0 .0560 ); every baseline shows a severely U-shaped PIT: SWAG ( 0.2195 ), IVON ( 0.2132 ), MultiSWAG (0.2188 ), HSA ( 0.1787 ), Contextual Dropout ( 0.1048 ). This under-dispersion is not a tuning failure. We veri-fied that SWAG’s predictive mean is strong: SWAG-mean reaches ACC =0 .9645 and SWAG-BMA reaches 0.9648 ,matching the deterministic baseline. Even after a dedi-cated variance-scale sweep over {1, 2, 4, 8, 16 , 32 , 64 } for increased predictive spread, the best natively calibrated SWAG (scale 64) achieves W1=0 .116 at coverage 60 .9% —still more than twice SA’s W1 at comparable native coverage (Appendix D.1). The SA–HSA comparison is especially informative: both target the attention bottleneck, but HSA modifies attention during training and suffers a 22% accuracy degradation (ACC drops to 0.7476 ). SA preserves the pretrained spatial routing by intervening only at inference time, achieving ACC =0 .902 at the BO-optimized ν=4 . This is a modest ac-curacy cost relative to the deterministic baseline ( 0.964 ), and it diminishes rapidly as ν increases: at ν=25 , SA reaches ACC =0 .966 , essentially matching SWAG-BMA ( 0.965 ).

7. Results beyond forecasting 

The forecasting results establish a strong operating point for SA, but they leave open whether the mechanism depends on forecasting structure, LoRA adaptation, or foundation-model pretraining. Table 2 addresses that question directly: on eight UCI regression datasets with a from-scratch FT-Transformer (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), SA achieves the lowest native W1 on 5 of 8 datasets (Panel A), with Naval es-sentially tied with MC Dropout ( 0.022 vs. 0.020 ). After tem-perature scaling baselines toward comparable coverage, SA produces the narrowest intervals on 7 of 8 datasets (Panel B). The exception is Naval, where HSA’s lower point-prediction error translates into tighter scaled intervals. Per-dataset PIT histograms and sharpness comparisons for all eight datasets are in Appendix F; on representative datasets like Yacht (normalized RMSE =0 .05 ), SA’s PIT is closest to uniform (W1=0 .021 ) and its intervals remain the sharpest after scal-ing. Ensemble CRPS evaluated natively (Appendix F.0.1) confirms that SA is among the two best methods on 4 of 8 datasets, with MultiSWAG winning 5 of 8 through lower per-sample prediction error from 10 independent training runs—the same pattern observed on ClimaX. The UCI results also reveal the method’s operating regime. SA’s calibration tracks backbone accuracy predictably: on low-RMSE datasets (Naval 0.04 , Yacht 0.05 , Energy 0.07 ), BO selects high ν (33 –76 ) and the PIT is near-uniform; as RMSE grows (Kin8nm 0.40 , Power Plant 0.24 ), ν col-lapses and coverage falls short. On Protein ( 0.67 ) and Wine (0.88 ), attention-level perturbations cannot span the residual uncertainty regardless of ν. This limitation is shared by all post-hoc methods, which degrade when the backbone is insufficiently accurate. But it defines a clear operating regime: SA is most valuable around strong pretrained or well-adapted backbones, precisely the setting scientific foun-dation models occupy. 

8. Discussion 

8.1. The calibration–cost trade-off 

The empirical picture across three structurally different set-tings converges on a consistent operating point. SA achieves strong native calibration, the sharpest or near-sharpest inter-vals once calibration is comparable, and does so at a fraction of the cost of retraining-heavy alternatives. These properties follow from three design choices that reinforce each other: stochasticity enters only at inference time, preserving the backbone’s predictive strength; it acts at the attention bot-tleneck where information is routed rather than at arbitrary locations; and its magnitude is set by a calibration objective, turning ν into a principled operating point rather than a free knob. The cost comparison sharpens this point. MultiSWAG achieves comparable sharpness through 10 independent training runs whose aggregate compute exceeds SA’s by over three orders of magnitude. Dropout-based methods share SA’s inference-time spirit but lack a calibration mech-anism tied to the model’s internal computation, leaving them natively overconfident. HSA targets the same bottleneck but modifies it during training, disrupting pretrained representa-tions. SA sidesteps all three failure modes by design. 

8.2. Limitations of a single-parameter design 

One global ν shared across all heads, layers, and coordinates is what makes SA lightweight, but it also limits expressivity. 8.3. Uncertainty and computational structure 

Beyond the practical trade-offs above, the results point to a qualitative distinction worth making explicit. The uncer-tainty SA produces is not sensitivity to parameter pertur-bation or random subnetwork selection; it is sensitivity to how the model allocates computational resources across tokens. That this form of variability, tied directly to the model’s internal information routing, already delivers better calibration at lower cost suggests that aligning the source of stochasticity with the model’s computational structure is itself a useful inductive bias for uncertainty quantification. 

9. Conclusion 

We introduced inference-time stochastic attention, a mecha-nism that replaces exact attention expectations with finite-sample multinomial draws controlled by a single concentra-tion parameter ν. Choosing ν through a calibration objective yields predictive distributions that are well calibrated na-tively, competitive in sharpness at comparable coverage, and obtainable without retraining. Across ClimaX weather forecasting, TimesFM time-series prediction, and FT-Transformer tabular regression, SA consistently achieves the strongest native calibration and the sharpest intervals on a coverage-comparable basis, re-quiring only inference-time passes on a frozen backbone. Under proper scoring rules, a different operating point (ν=25 ) ranks first, confirming that calibration-first and score-optimal evaluations are complementary. The main limitation mirrors the main simplification: a single global 

ν bounds expressivity when the backbone itself is insuf-ficiently accurate. Extensions to richer parameterizations remain a natural next step, but the simplest version already identifies a practically useful operating point for uncertainty in scientific foundation models.

C. Experimental Setup and Reproducibility Details 

This appendix section summarizes the experimental settings used in the paper and provides the key implementation details needed to reproduce the reported uncertainty diagnostics. We consider three evaluation settings: (i) transformer-based time-series forecasting with T IMES FM, (ii) global weather forecasting with C LIMA X, and (iii) UCI regression as a compact testbed for evaluating calibration and sharpness beyond the scientific forecasting examples. Baseline-method summaries are given separately in Appendix G. 

C.1. TimesFM: Architecture and Forecasting Backbone. TIMES FM is a decoder-only transformer for time-series forecasting that operates on non-overlapping input patches (Das et al., 2024). Each patch is mapped to a token representation and processed by stacked causal self-attention layers. Forecasts are decoded in output patches and extended autoregressively for longer horizons. Unless otherwise stated, deterministic reporting uses the point-forecast decoding head. TimesFM supports multiple decoding heads; for deterministic reporting we use the point-forecast head (decode index = 5 ). 

Datasets and splits. We evaluate on the ETT-small benchmarks ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, and ETTm2 (Zhou et al., 2021), using the standard Informer split boundaries: ETTh*: training 0–8640, validation 8640–11520, test 11520–14400; ETTm*: training 0–34560, validation 34560–46080, test 46080–57600. Following our adaptation protocol, normalization statistics are computed on the full training segment, while model adaptation uses only the first 10% of the training portion. Each column is treated as an independent univariate series. 

Forecasting protocol and metric. We use context length 512 and forecasting horizons of 96 and 192. Forecasting is performed autoregressively in patch units. Predictive accuracy is reported using MAE, averaged across series and forecast horizon. 18 Calibrating Scientific Foundation Models with Inference-Time Stochastic Attention 

Lightweight task adaptation. To obtain a strong deterministic checkpoint while keeping the pretrained backbone fixed, we use LoRA adapters attached to the query–key–value projection, attention output projection, and the two linear layers in the feed-forward block. All pretrained backbone parameters are frozen and only LoRA parameters are trained. 

C.2. ClimaX: Architecture and Global Forecasting Protocol Backbone. CLIMA X is a ViT-style foundation model for gridded weather and climate fields (Nguyen et al., 2023). It tokenizes each variable field into spatial patches, aggregates variable information through cross-attention, and processes the resulting token sequence with a ViT backbone. 

Task and metrics. We evaluate global forecasting at a 72-hour lead time. Deterministic predictive accuracy is reported using latitude-weighted MSE, latitude-weighted RMSE, and latitude-weighted anomaly correlation coefficient (ACC), following the standard evaluation protocol for C LIMA X. 

Model adaptation. We start from the pretrained 5.625 ◦ checkpoint and use LoRA adapters on the attention projections and MLP linear layers. The pretrained backbone is frozen, while normalization layers remain trainable. Best and final checkpoints are selected using validation latitude-weighted RMSE. 

C.3. UCI Regression Benchmarks Purpose. In addition to the forecasting experiments, we evaluate stochastic attention on standard UCI regression tasks to test whether the method extends beyond the two scientific foundation-model case studies. This provides a compact regression setting in which calibration, sharpness, and proper scoring rules can be compared across multiple datasets under a common protocol. 

Evaluation protocol. For each UCI dataset, we begin with a deterministic transformer-based regressor and then apply the same post-hoc stochastic-attention calibration procedure used in the main paper. We report deterministic predictive accuracy, along with uncertainty diagnostics based on PIT, empirical coverage, interval width, and, where applicable, proper scoring rules. Native and post-scaled comparisons are reported separately in the appendix results. 

C.4. Predictive Samples for Calibration Diagnostics 

All uncertainty methods considered in the paper induce predictive distributions through samples. To unify evaluation across methods, we form an empirical predictive distribution bF from repeated predictive samples for each test case. PIT values are then computed from bF for scalar outcomes, and coordinate-wise for structured outputs, aggregating over coordinates when needed. 

Stochastic attention. For stochastic attention, each predictive sample corresponds to a single stochastic forward pass of the frozen model, with attention rows sampled according to Algorithm 1. The concentration parameter ν is calibrated on held-out data using Algorithm 2, after which repeated stochastic forward passes are used to estimate calibration and sharpness diagnostics. 

SWAG-LoRA and MultiSWAG. For SWAG-LoRA, predictive samples are obtained by sampling LoRA weights from the SWAG approximation and performing one forward pass per draw. For MultiSWAG, samples are aggregated across multiple independently trained SWAG-LoRA models, yielding a mixture approximation. 

IVON-LoRA. For IVON-LoRA, predictive samples are generated by sampling LoRA weights from the learned Gaussian variational approximation and running one forward pass per sample.

G.2. Contextual Dropout 

Contextual dropout generalizes standard dropout by making the dropout distribution depend on the input covariates, rather than using a single global dropout distribution shared across all samples (Fan et al., 2021b). G.6. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) 

LoRA fine-tunes a pre-trained model by freezing its original weights and introducing a low-rank parameterization for the weight update in selected linear layers (Hu et al., 2022). Concretely, for a pre-trained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k, LoRA reparameterizes the adapted weight as 

W = W0 + ∆ W, ∆W = BA , (40) where B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k with rank r ≪ min( d, k ). The pre-trained weights W0 are held fixed, and only the LoRA parameters (A, B) are trained. For an input x, the resulting forward pass is 

h = Wx = W0x + BAx . (41) LoRA commonly scales the update by α/r , yielding h = W0x + αr BAx , and initializes A with a small random matrix while setting B = 0 so that ∆W = 0 at initialization. 

G.7. SWAG-LoRA, MultiSWAG-LoRA, and IVON-LoRA 

We implement SWAG-LoRA and MultiSWAG-LoRA by combining the SWA/SWAG and MultiSWAG procedures described above with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022; Izmailov et al., 2018; Maddox et al., 2019; Onal et al., 2024). The core SWAG and MultiSWAG constructions remain unchanged; the key difference is that the posterior approximation is placed only over the trainable LoRA adapters rather than over the full set of model weights. This restriction is what makes the Bayesian treatment computationally feasible for large pre-trained models. Let ϕ denote the vector of trainable LoRA parameters (i.e., the collection of all (A, B) matrices included in our LoRA configuration). In SWAG-LoRA, we keep the pre-trained backbone weights fixed and apply SWA/SWAG to ϕ, constructing the same diagonal-plus-low-rank Gaussian approximation but over the LoRA parameter trajectory. At inference, we sample 

ϕ, insert the sampled LoRA parameters into the model (leaving the backbone unchanged), and average predictions across samples, using the same batch-norm refresh convention as SWAG when applicable. This avoids the substantially more expensive task of estimating a posterior over all network parameters. MultiSWAG-LoRA follows the same extension as MultiSWAG: we build multiple independent SWAG-LoRA components from separate runs (e.g., different random seeds/initializations) and average predictions across components and samples, yielding Bayesian marginalization across multiple basins while remaining in LoRA parameter space. In practice, this is often implemented as “ L SWAG solutions, M samples per solution,” producing an ensemble of size L × M , with the same batch-norm refresh convention as SWAG when applicable. We also implement IVON-LoRA by replacing the standard LoRA optimizer (e.g., AdamW) with the IVON variational-learning procedure on the LoRA parameters, while keeping the backbone fixed (Shen et al., 2024; Cong et al., 2024). Thus, the variational posterior is placed directly over ϕ rather than fit post hoc after training, and the diagonal posterior variance is learned through IVON’s scale/curvature vector during optimization. This makes IVON-LoRA a direct Bayesian optimizer over the LoRA adapters, with only minor extra cost from posterior sampling during training. At evaluation time, we either use the variational mean (IVON-LoRA@mean) or approximate the posterior predictive by averaging predictions over multiple samples ϕ(m) ∼ q(ϕ) (IVON-LoRA), analogously to the sampling-based predictions described for IVON.
