Abstract 

In the electric system, extreme weather events can cause trips or physical damage to trans-mission lines, leading to large-scale load shedding. To mitigate power shedding, we propose a framework that pre-positions the commitment of production units—particularly slow-start units—to cope with transmission topologies that may result from such events. Our goal is to minimize load shedding under the worst-case scenario. The novel contributions of this paper are twofold: (1) a more precise description of the physical laws than those used in previous works reported in the literature, and (2) a highly efficient solution algorithm compared to state-of-the-art, off-the-shelf solvers. We formulate this framework as a two-stage robust op-timization model. In the first stage, generation units are scheduled, and in the second stage, power dispatch decisions are made to minimize load shedding under the worst-case scenario. Convexified AC power flow constraints are incorporated to ensure system reliability and se-curity. The resulting formulation is a tri-level mixed-integer nonlinear optimization problem. To address the computational challenges, we propose a problem-specific outer approximation algorithm embedded within a column-and-constraint generation framework. Computational results demonstrate that our model and algorithm can produce solutions within a standard optimality gap in reasonable time for moderately large instances. 

Keywords: OR in energy, Conic programming, Interior point methods, Large scale optimization, Robust Optimization

1. Introduction 

1.1. Motivation, formulation and solution technique 

Extreme weather events result in substantial economic loss worldwide. Campbell and Lowry (2012) indicated that major power outages, mainly caused by storm-related damage to electricity transmission lines, were estimated to cost about $20–$55 billion annually in lost orders, spoiled inventory, delayed production, and other disruptions. On a global scale, extreme weather events including hurricanes, floods, droughts, and storms imposed more than $2 trillion dollars over the past decade, with the United States accounting for nearly half of this total (International Chamber of Commerce and Oxera, 2024). Severe weather also inflicts significant damage on energy infrastructure. For example, hur-ricanes Katrina and Rita damaged or destroyed thousands of miles of transmission lines and hundreds of substations; in Southeast Louisiana and Mississippi, 1.1 million customers lost power after Katrina (United States Senate Committee on Energy and Natural Resources, 2005). In 2017, Hurricane Maria caused power outage affecting almost all the customers in Puerto Rico until September 20, 2017 (Infrastructure Security and Energy Restoration, 2017). More recently, Hurricane Ida (2021) forced the complete failure of all eight transmis-sion lines supplying New Orleans, cutting power to nearly 895,000 customers in Southeastern Louisiana (News-Record, 2021). These examples highlight how transmission line failures due to wind, flooding, or structural collapse can cascade through the system, requiring emergency redispatch of generation or involuntary load shedding to preserve system stability. To reduce the negative impact of extreme weather events, many strategies have been studied and adopted, including infrastructure hardening, operational improvements (Abdel-malak and Benidris, 2022; U.S. Department of Energy, 2018), and robust planning (Sang et al., 2019; Mohammadi and Sahraei-Ardakani, 2020). There have been significant invest-ments to harden the infrastructure, e.g., $3 billion spent by Florida Power and Light (FPL) 3(Florida Power & Light Company, 2017). At the operations level, researchers have inves-tigated both proactive and emergency control actions that change generator commitments and dispatches in anticipation (i.e. redispatch) to component outages (Wang et al., 2016; Abdelmalak and Benidris, 2022). In this paper, we focus on providing an AC-based robust power generation schedule against the impact of severe weather on transmission lines. Our main purpose is imposing on this scheduling problem robust security against major weather events, which result in an adaptive robust optimization (tri-level) model involving three interrelated problems. Such approach is important to ensure appropriate security against increasingly frequent weather events. We optimize the scheduling of power production units (unit commitment problem) representing the transmission network via AC constraints in second-order-conic format. As it is customary, we slightly relax this formulation to make it convex, which may result in minor imprecisions in the final solution. Such imprecisions typically affect flow and production values, not the commitment of the units. The first-level problem of the three-level adaptive robust optimization model determines the actual unit commitment of the generating units seeking minimum cost and anticipating the worst possible weather event. Given the commitment identified by the first-level problem, the second-level problem detects the worst-case weather event that results in maximum cost, anticipating the best possible operation (minimum cost) of the system by the system operator. Finally, the third-level problem operates the system seeking minimum cost once the worst weather event (as determined by the second-level problem) materializes with the schedule from the first-level problem. We note that this is a complicated min-max-min optimization problem, with conic, not linear constraints. Since the third-level problem is a multi-period optimal power flow problem, which is second-order-conic and convex, we can replace it by its dual problem without approximation, in turn, merged with the second-level problem, which returns in a two-level problem. This two-level problem can be solved using the column-and-constraint generation algorithm first proposed by Zeng and Zhao (2013). The column-and-constraint generation algorithm involves a master problem whose size dramatically increases with the number of iterations and that is mixed-integer second-order-conic, and a subproblem that is second-order-conic continuous and whose size is moderate. Since the master problem is the computational bottleneck, to alleviate its computational burden an effective problem-specific outer approximation technique is employed. 41.2. Literature Review 

Second-order-conic (SOC) relaxation for unit commitment problem are proposed in (Bai et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2018). While the SOC relaxation convexifies the continuous network laws, commitment decisions need to be made, resulting in a computationally challenging in mixed-integer SOC Problem (Bonami et al., 2011) due to the high cost of solving conic sub-problems within branch-and-bound framework. To mitigate these issues, some authors have proposed strengthening the SOCP relaxation using valid inequalities and bound tightening (Kocuk et al., 2016; Coffrin et al., 2015), as well as decomposition algorithms to separate commitment decisions and AC feasibility (Bai et al., 2015; Constante-Flores et al., 2022; Tuncer and Kocuk, 2022). Despite being substantially harder to solve than DC unit commitment problem formu-lated as a mixed-integer linear program (Carrión and Arroyo, 2006; Conejo and Baringo, 2018), SOC-relaxed AC unit commitment provides commitment that are more consistent with physical network constraints. DC unit commitment relies on linearized DC power flow, ignoring reactive power, voltage magnitudes, and losses (Wood et al., 2013), and may there-fore yield commitment schedules that are infeasible or require significant corrective actions when evaluated under full AC laws (Bai et al., 2015; Molzahn et al., 2019). In contrast, SOC-based AC unit commitment captures key AC feasibility aspects while maintaining convexity in the continuous relaxation, producing tighter lower bounds and more reliable commitment decisions even if the relaxation is not exact (Jabr, 2006). As a result, SOC AC unit com-mitment is widely viewed as a principled compromise between the scalability of DC unit commitment and the accuracy of full nonconvex AC unit commitment (Tuncer and Kocuk, 2022). Developing commitment schedules that are robust against contingencies has become in-creasingly important in modern power system operations. Robust network-constrained unit commitment models aim to identify commitment decisions that remain feasible and cost-effective under the worst-case scenario of uncertain parameters (Jiang et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2025; Bertsimas et al., 2012; Amjady et al., 2016). An and Zeng (2014) deployed column-and-constraint generation to solve this problem. More recently, distributionally ro-bust optimization has been introduced to unit commitment formulations, offering protec-tion against ambiguity in probability distributions. Applications span both linear (Zhao and Jiang, 2017; Duan et al., 2017) and nonlinear settings (Dehghan et al., 2021). More specifically, stochastic unit commitment with DC-based formulations have been extended to incorporate weather-driven contingencies: for example, DC-based formulations that consider probabilistic transmission line disconnections based on weather forecasts (Sang et al., 2019; Mohammadi and Sahraei-Ardakani, 2020). Additionally, data-driven methods have emerged, 5with machine learning being applied to improve both scenario generation and solution qual-ity (Mohammadi et al., 2021). In addition to traditional robust and stochastic formulations, resilience can be integrated into the unit commitment framework to address the challenges posed by extreme weather events. (Zhao et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024) Security-constrained unit commitment commonly incorporates the N − 1 security cri-terion, requiring the system to remain feasible following the outage of any single critical component, such as a generator or transmission line (Sundar et al., 2019). Extensions to the more general N − k criterion, which considers simultaneous outages of up to k components, have been studied to address extreme events and correlated failures, but they significantly increase computational complexity due to the combinatorial growth in the number of con-tingencies (Street et al., 2010; Bienstock et al., 2014). As a result, practical implementations often rely on screening, contingency selection, or cutting-plane and Benders-type decompo-sition methods to identify only the most critical contingencies during the solution process. In this work, we focus on robust decision that provide protection against the failure of transmission lines due to hurricanes, and the number of failure lines is not given, dislike the model for N − k criterion. We emphasize that severe weather is the leading reason of power outages in the United States (Campbell and Lowry, 2012; Sang et al., 2018). 

1.3. Contributions 

Considering the above literature review, the contributions of this paper are threefold: 1) Within an adaptive robust optimization framework, we model the operation problem (multi-period optimal power flow) as a second-order-conic problem, not as a linear one. Most works reported in the literature use linear formulations. 2) We model hurricane trajectories (and the subsequent transmission lines disabled) to be incorporated within the proposed adaptive robust framework. 3) To ease the computational burden of the master problem of the column-and-constraint generation algorithm, we use a novel problem-specific outer-linearization technique, which results in significant computational savings. This is the major contribution of our work. We conduct extensive computational simulations to show its relevance. 

1.4. Paper organization 

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces the AC net-work–constrained unit commitment problem, presents its second-order cone reformulation, and derives the corresponding dual after fixing the binary decisions. Section 3 develops the proposed three-level adaptive robust optimization framework. Section 4 describes the 6column-and-constraint generation scheme together with the proposed outer-approximation strategy. Section 5 reports comprehensive numerical experiments that validate the proposed formulation and demonstrate the performance of the solution approach. Section 6 concludes the paper.

2.1. Problem statements and model assumptions 

In this section, we detail the formulation of the considered unit commitment problem with the following features: 1. Flows through lines are represented using relaxed SOC expressions. 2. Lines can be disabled due to hurricane trajectories and adopt a mixed-integer linear formulation similar to that proposed by Kocuk et al. (2017) regarding line switching. 3. Our formulation includes unserved active and reactive power (slack variables), which prevents infeasibility.

5. Numerical experiments 

In this section, we numerically validate the proposed solution approach using both the IEEE 24-bus system, and the Central Illinois 200-bus test system (Birchfield et al., 2016). All data can be found in (Flores, 2022), Appendix E. To guarantee reactive power feasibility under hurricane conditions, we set “reactive power generators” for nodes without any gen-erator, which can either consume or produce reactive power as needed. Furthermore, we consider a number of time period T = 24 , i.e. the hours of a day. We conduct all experiments on an AMD Ryzen Windows64 machine including an R5-5600G 3.90GHz CPU with 6 physical cores, 12 logical processors, and 16 GB RAM. We use two commercial solvers: Gurobi version 12.0.1 (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2024) and Mosek 11.0.9 (ApS, 2025) with their default setting. We build models and solve them with JuMP v1.25.0 (Lubin et al., 2023) in Julia v1.11.5 (Bezanson et al., 2017).

6. Conclusions 

Considering the proposed robust model for the scheduling of electricity production units, and the computational analysis carried out, the observations below are in order: 23 1. The methodology proposed, which guarantees robust security against major weather events and embodies a detailed second-order-conic representation of the power flow equations, effectively identifies optimal schedules that are robust. 2. The column-and-constraint-generation algorithm used to address the considered min-max-min problem relies on solving moderate size subproblems (continuous and convex) and a master problem (mixed-integer convex) that is effectively solved using an outer-linearization algorithm. 3. Solving the mixed-integer second-order-conic master problem, which constitutes the bottleneck of the technique proposed, by using a tailored outer approximation tech-nique results in moderate solution times, which are comparable with the timeframe required by unit commitment problems. 4. A simple example is used to illustrate the methodology proposed, and an case study is used to demonstrated its real-world applicability. 5. The methodology proposed is novel in two respects: (i) it used second-order-conic convex models, no linear ones as the available literature does, and (ii) it embodied a particularly effective outer approximation technique to solve the master problem of the column-and-constraint-generation algorithm, the bottleneck of most work reported in the literature.
