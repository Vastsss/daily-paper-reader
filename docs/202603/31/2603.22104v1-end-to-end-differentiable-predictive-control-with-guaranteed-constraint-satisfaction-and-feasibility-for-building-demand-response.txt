End-to-End Differentiable Predictive Control with Guaranteed Constraint Satisfaction and
feasibility for Building Demand Response
Kaipeng Xua, Zhuo Zhia, Ruixuan Zhaoa, Keyue Jianga
aDepartment of Electronic and Electrical Engineering, University College London, Torrington Place, London WC1E 7JE, United Kingdom
Abstract
The high energy consumption of buildings presents a critical need for advanced control strategies like Demand Response (DR).
Differentiable Predictive Control (DPC) has emerged as a promising method for learning explicit control policies, yet conventional
DPC frameworks are hindered by three key limitations: the use of simplistic dynamics models with limited expressiveness, a
decoupled training paradigm that fails to optimize for closed-loop performance, and a lack of practical safety guarantees under
realistic assumptions. To address these shortcomings, this paper proposes a novel End-to-End Differentiable Predictive Control
(E2E-DPC) framework. Our approach utilizes an Encoder-Only Transformer to model the complex system dynamics and employs
a unified, performance-oriented loss to jointly train the model and the control policy. Crucially, we introduce an online tube-based
constraint tightening method that provides theoretical guarantees for recursive feasibility and constraint satisfaction without requiring
complex offline computation of terminal sets. The framework is validated in a high-fidelity EnergyPlus simulation, controlling
a multi-zone building for a DR task. The results demonstrate that the proposed method with guarantees achieves near-perfect
constraint satisfaction—a reduction of over 99% in violations compared to the baseline—at the cost of only a minor increase in
electricity expenditure. This work provides a deployable, performance-driven control solution for building energy management and
establishes a new pathway for developing verifiable learning-based control systems under milder assumptions.
Keywords: Differentiable Predictive Control, End-to-End Learning, Constraint Satisfaction, Building Demand Response, Robust
Control, Encoder-Only Transformer
1. Introduction
Buildings represent one of the largest global energy con-
sumers, accounting for approximately 40% of total energy de-
mand and over 30% of CO2 emissions [1, 2].
Among vari-
ous building loads, Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning
(HVAC) systems are particularly significant, consuming nearly
50% of total building energy [3]. The substantial energy foot-
print and inherent thermal storage capacity of buildings present
a prime opportunity for Demand Response (DR) programs. As
defined in [4], DR involves changes in electricity usage by end-
users in response to price signals. For buildings, this allows
the thermal mass to act as a virtual battery; by pre-heating or
pre-cooling during off-peak hours, energy consumption can be
shifted away from high-price periods to reduce costs and support
grid stability [5, 6].
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is the preeminent strategy
for implementing HVAC-based DR, owing to its unique abil-
ity to handle multivariable constraints, anticipate future system
behavior, and optimize performance over a finite horizon [7].
The efficacy of MPC, however, is critically dependent on its
internal predictive model. While first-principles models are of-
ten time-consuming to develop [8], data-driven models using
machine learning (ML) offer a more flexible and often more ac-
curate alternative for capturing complex whole-building thermal
dynamics [9, 10].
To circumvent the high online computational cost of tradi-
tional implicit MPC, Differentiable Predictive Control (DPC)
has been introduced as a scalable deep learning alternative that
learns an explicit neural control policy offline [11, 12]. However,
conventional DPC frameworks suffer from several fundamental
limitations that hinder their performance and reliability. First,
the dynamics model ( 𝑓𝑥) and the control policy (𝜋𝑢) are trained
in a decoupled manner. The model is pre-trained to minimize
predictive error, which does not guarantee optimal closed-loop
control performance. This separation can lead to policies that
are suboptimal for the true system. Second, prior DPC works
often rely on simpler dynamics models, such as linear State-
Space Models (SSMs), which may lack the expressive power
to accurately capture the complex, non-linear dynamics of real-
world systems like buildings [11]. Third, and most critically,
establishing formal guarantees for DPC policies remains a sig-
nificant challenge. Existing theoretical frameworks that provide
such guarantees are often predicated on the system dynamics
model being known and fixed a priori, and they frequently rely
on strong assumptions—such as the existence of a pre-designed
terminal set and a stabilizing terminal controller [12]—that are
difficult to satisfy in practice and are fundamentally incompati-
ble with an E2E learning paradigm.
To overcome these limitations, this paper proposes a novel
End-to-End Differentiable Predictive Control (E2E-DPC)
framework with robust constraint satisfaction guarantees.
The main contributions of this work are threefold:

2. Related Work
2.1. Differentiable Predictive Control
DPC has emerged as a powerful and scalable deep learn-
ing alternative to explicit Model Predictive Control (eMPC),
designed to learn explicit neural control policies for complex
systems offline [11, 12].
The foundational concept of DPC
is the formulation of a fully differentiable closed-loop system,
typically realized as a computational graph. In its conventional
setup, a system dynamics model, 𝑓𝑥, is first trained to minimize
prediction error and is subsequently held fixed. This model is
then combined with a parameterized neural control policy, 𝜋𝑢,
allowing for the E2E differentiation of an MPC-inspired loss
function with respect to the policy’s parameters [11, 12]. This
allows the policy to be trained directly via gradient-based meth-
ods, moving the computational burden of optimization from
online to offline and enabling rapid online deployment.
While this approach has demonstrated strong empirical per-
formance across various domains, including power systems [14]
and urban traffic networks [15], establishing formal guarantees
for the learned policies has been a central challenge. Early DPC
frameworks either lacked theoretical guarantees or relied on soft
constraint penalties that could be violated in practice [11]. Sub-
sequent research has focused on integrating safety and stability
certifications directly into the DPC learning process. For in-
stance, some approaches have proposed jointly learning a neu-
ral Lyapunov function alongside the policy to certify regions
of stability (termed NLDPC) [16], while others have incorpo-
rated control barrier functions (CBFs) to enforce safety guaran-
tees [17, 18].
A significant step towards formal guarantees was the de-
velopment of a probabilistic validation framework [12], which
provides a high-confidence statistical bound on the policy’s per-
formance. However, the theoretical underpinnings for the deter-
ministic stability proofs in this line of work often rely on strong
assumptions. For example, the framework in [12] requires the
existence of a known terminal set and a stabilizing terminal
controller (Assumption 2 in their work) to ensure closed-loop
stability. A more fundamental limitation is that these guarantees
are predicated on the system dynamics model, 𝑓𝑥, being known
and fixed a priori to allow for the necessary offline calculations
(e.g., of invariant sets). This prerequisite is fundamentally in-
compatible with the E2E learning paradigm proposed in our
work, where the model 𝑓𝑥is trained concurrently with the policy.
This motivates our development of a new theoretical framework,
based on a tube-based constraint tightening approach, which is
specifically designed to provide guarantees under the more flex-
ible E2E training scheme and with milder assumptions.
2.2. Performance-Oriented and End-to-End Training
A central theme in learning-based control is the shift from
decoupled training paradigms towards performance-oriented,
E2E learning. In the conventional decoupled approach, a dy-
namics model is first trained to maximize its predictive accuracy
(e.g., by minimizing MSE), and is then treated as a fixed com-
ponent for training a separate control policy. In contrast, an E2E
framework, as proposed in this work, jointly optimizes the pa-
rameters of the dynamics model (𝜃𝑓) and the control policy (𝜃𝜋)
with respect to the final closed-loop control objective. This al-
lows the performance objective to directly influence the learned
representation of the system dynamics, potentially leading to
models that, while not perfectly accurate in a standalone sense,
are better suited for the specific control task at hand.
However, this joint optimization transforms the learning
problem into a multi-task scenario for the shared model pa-
rameters 𝜃𝑓. These parameters are simultaneously updated by
two distinct and often competing objectives: minimizing the
one-step identification error to ensure predictive accuracy, and
minimizing a composite loss reflecting the long-term, closed-
loop control performance. This second objective typically in-
cludes terms for the primary control goal (e.g., economic cost)
as well as penalties for violating operational constraints. It is
well-documented that naively summing gradients from these
different objectives can lead to destructive interference, where
gradients point in conflicting directions, thereby slowing con-
vergence or destabilizing the training process [19].
Several strategies have been proposed in the literature to
address this challenge. Broadly, they fall into two categories.
The first, loss-balancing methods, aims to dynamically ad-
just the weights of each task’s loss function, for instance by
2


normalizing the gradient magnitudes [20] or by using task un-
certainty as a weighting mechanism [21]. The second, gradi-
ent surgery methods, directly manipulates the task gradients
to ensure non-conflicting updates. Projecting Conflict Gradi-
ents (PCGrad) [22] is a prominent example, which removes any
component of one task’s gradient that opposes another. Other
advanced techniques include finding a conflict-averse combi-
nation of gradients [23] or seeking a Nash equilibrium in a
multi-objective bargaining game [24].
In this work, a hybrid training strategy is adopted that lever-
agesthe strengths of bothdecoupled andjoint trainingparadigms
while mitigating the associated challenges. The training proce-
dure is structured as a form of curriculum learning [25], be-
ginning with an initial phase of model pre-training. This aligns
with findings in model-based reinforcement learning that pre-
training a world-model improves sample efficiency and overall
stability [26, 27]. Subsequently, during the joint training phase,
the PCGrad method [22] is employed to manage gradient con-
flicts.
This choice is motivated by PCGrad’s computational
efficiency, implementation simplicity, and its direct approach to
resolving the geometric conflict between gradients, making it
highly suitable for the complex optimization landscape of the
E2E-DPC framework.
2.3. Approaches to Robust Constraint Satisfaction in MPC
A central challenge in applying MPC to uncertain or learned
nonlinear systems is ensuring robust constraint satisfaction in
the presence of disturbances and model mismatch [13]. While
various strategies exist, they often involve a trade-off between
computational tractability and conservatism. Approaches like
min-max MPC, which optimize over worst-case disturbances,
can guarantee robust feasibility but often lead to computation-
ally intractable optimization problems [28]. This makes them
unsuitable for real-time applications or complex training loops.
As a practical compromise, tube-based MPC has emerged as
a powerful and widely-used paradigm [29]. The core idea is to
compute a "tube" that is guaranteed to contain the actual system
trajectory, centered around a nominal one. By enforcing tight-
ened constraints on the nominal trajectory, one can ensure that
the true system state will not violate the original constraints.
However, the construction of these tubes for nonlinear sys-
tems presents its own set of challenges. While straightforward
procedures exist for linear systems [29, 30], methods for non-
linear dynamics are generally difficult to obtain, often being re-
stricted to special system classes or resulting in overly conserva-
tive tubes [31–33]. Furthermore, many robust MPC schemes de-
pend on the existence of pre-computed terminal ingredients—a
terminal cost and a terminal set—to ensure recursive feasibil-
ity and stability. As noted in [13], designing such ingredients
involves potentially difficult and conservative offline compu-
tations and is often intractable for complex, highly non-linear
systems.
This dependency on pre-computed terminal ingredients is
particularly ill-suited for the E2E learning context of this work,
where the dynamics model is not fixed a priori but is contin-
uously refined during training. To overcome these limitations,
this work adopts the simpler, more flexible constraint tightening
approach proposed by Köhler et al. [13]. This method offers
several key advantages that make it exceptionally well-suited
for our framework. First, it is a constructive, online approach
that does not require complex offline computations of terminal
sets or stabilizing controllers. Instead, it leverages the system’s
local, incremental stabilizability properties, which can be esti-
mated online from the learned model’s Jacobians. Second, and
most critically, it generates a tube that grows to a bounded size
over the prediction horizon. This is a fundamental difference
from many Lipschitz-based approaches and makes the method
compatible with MPC formulations that do not rely on terminal
constraints. The simplicity and online nature of this approach
allow for the benefits of robust control to be seamlessly and
efficiently integrated into the E2E policy learning process.

3.2. Differentiable Closed-Loop System
The core of the E2E-DPC framework is a differentiable
model of the closed-loop system, enabling the use of gradient-
based optimization for joint training. This system is composed
of two primary learnable components: the system dynamics
model 𝑓𝑥and the control policy 𝜋𝑢.
3.2.1. System Dynamics Model ( 𝑓𝑥)
The dynamics model 𝑓𝑥(·|𝜃𝑓) predicts the system’s next
state based on its current state, the applied control action, and
exogenous disturbances. A significant departure from early DPC
works that often relied on SSMs with limited expressive power is
that this framework allows 𝑓𝑥to be represented by any suitable
deep neural network architecture, such as a Recurrent Neural
Network (RNN) or a Transformer. This architectural flexibility
grants the framework greater universality, enabling it to capture
complex, non-linear dynamics more accurately than traditional
linear models.

maps the current system state and external parameters directly

3.4. End-to-End Training Procedure
The joint optimization of the dynamics model and the control
policy is performed offline using a carefully designed, multi-
stage training procedure. This process, visually summarized in
Figure 1, is structured to ensure stability and effective learning
by first guiding the dynamics model into a neighborhood of its
optimal data-fitting state before coupling it with the complexities
of policy learning.
The procedure begins with an Initial Dynamics Model
Training phase, as depicted in the top panel of Figure 1. This
stage involves a standard supervised learning setup where the
policy network 𝜋𝑢is frozen. The dynamics model 𝑓𝑥is trained
in isolation using a ground-truth dataset. As illustrated, the for-
ward propagation (green arrow) computes the one-step-ahead
prediction, which is compared against the true next state to
calculate the identification loss, LID. The resulting gradients
are then backpropagated (red arrow) to update only the model
parameters 𝜃𝑓. This pre-training continues until the model’s
performance on a validation set shows no significant improve-
ment.
Once a reliable dynamics model is established, the Joint
E2E Training phase is initiated, as shown in the bottom panel
of the figure. In this stage, a virtual closed-loop trajectory is
generated by unrolling the system for the full prediction hori-
zon 𝑁. At each step, the control policy 𝜋𝑢generates an action,
which is then fed into the dynamics model 𝑓𝑥to predict the next
state. This entire forward propagation (green arrows) results
in a complete trajectory, which is evaluated by the composite
E2E loss function, LE2E.
The crucial step is the backward
propagation (red arrows), where the gradients from this single,
performance-oriented loss are backpropagated through the en-
tire unrolled graph to simultaneously update both the model
parameters 𝜃𝑓and the policy parameters 𝜃𝜋.

4. Guarantee Mechanism for Robust Constraint Satisfac-
tion
This section presents the theoretical foundation that equips
the E2E-DPC framework with robust constraint satisfaction and
recursive feasibility guarantees. The methodology consists of
two primary stages. First, a tube-based constraint tightening
scheme, adapted from the principles of robust control, is con-
structed. This scheme systematically shrinks the feasible sets
for the nominal system to create a robust tube wherein the ac-
tual system trajectory can evolve without violating the original
constraints. Second, a probabilistic validation framework is em-
ployed post-training to provide a formal, quantifiable certificate
of the learned policy’s performance, yielding a high-confidence
lower bound on its probability of satisfying all constraints. This
entire design and certification pipeline, from training to valida-
tion, is formally summarized in Algorithm 2.

guarantee. Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 provide a deterministic
guarantee: they ensure that for any single trajectory, if the nom-
inal system behaves as expected and the disturbance is within a
known bound, the actual system is guaranteed to be safe. How-
ever, in a learning context, we operate over a distribution of
initial conditions and uncertainties. Theorem 2 provides the
necessary probabilistic layer, certifying that the learned pol-
icy will achieve the desired safe behavior with high probability
when deployed on scenarios drawn from this distribution. This
combination bridges the gap between local, deterministic ro-
bustness and the global, statistical nature of a learning-based
control problem.
5. Case Study
To empirically validate the performance of the proposed
E2E-DPC framework and its theoretical guarantees, this section
presents a comprehensive case study in a high-fidelity simu-
lation environment. This study evaluates and compares three
distinct control strategies: 1) a Conventional DPC (DPC-C)
representing the traditional, decoupled training baseline; 2) an
E2E-DPC policy trained without guarantees according to Al-
gorithm 1; and 3) the full proposed E2E-DPC with Guarantees
(E2E-DPC-G), designed using the complete pipeline in Algo-
rithm 2. The control objective, adopted from prior work on this
system [35], is to regulate the thermostat setpoints of a resi-
dential apartment building to minimize electricity costs under
a Time-of-Use (TOU) tariff, while strictly maintaining indoor
thermal comfort.

5.3. Compared Methods and Implementation
The three controllers were implemented and trained accord-
ing to their respective methodologies.
To ensure a fair and
rigorous comparison, the core model architectures and many
training hyperparameters were kept consistent across all three
methods, with differences only where required by the specific
training paradigm. This deliberate alignment highlights the per-
formance impact of the training strategy itself. All necessary
hyperparameters are detailed in Table 2 to ensure reproducibil-
ity.
1. DPC-C: This baseline follows a traditional two-stage pro-
cess. First, the EoT dynamics model ( 𝑓𝑥) was trained
independently to minimize a composite loss of MSE and
a soft penalty for constraint violations. With a frozen 𝑓𝑥,
the MLP policy (𝜋𝑢) was then trained to minimize the
closed-loop control objective. For this baseline, no for-
mal guarantees are established. The high complexity and
non-linear nature of the EoT model make it intractable
to design traditional terminal constraints or verify condi-
tions such as Assumption 2 in [12], which presupposes
the existence of a pre-designed local Lyapunov function,
an invariant terminal set, and a feasible terminal control
law.
2. E2E DPC (E2E-DPC): This controller was trained fol-
lowing the procedure in Algorithm 1. Both 𝑓𝑥and 𝜋𝑢were
trained jointly with a performance-oriented loss function,
but without the online constraint tightening mechanism.
3. E2E-DPC with Guarantee (E2E-DPC-G): This is the
full proposed method, trained according to the complete
design and certification pipeline in Algorithm 2. This
includes the online construction of the tightening tube
within each training batch and augmenting the policy’s
input with the resulting tightening sequence.

6. Conclusion and Future Work
6.1. Conclusion
This paper introduced an E2E-DPC framework designed
to provide robust constraint satisfaction guarantees for complex
nonlinear systems. By shifting from the conventional decoupled
training paradigm to a joint, performance-oriented optimiza-
tion of both the dynamics model ( 𝑓𝑥) and the control policy
(𝜋𝑢), the proposed method allows the control objective to di-
rectly shape the learned system representation. Furthermore,
this work presented a novel methodology for integrating a tube-
based constraint tightening scheme, which can be computed
online, directly into the E2E learning process. This mechanism
provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for ensuring recursive
feasibility and robust constraint satisfaction without relying on
pre-computed terminal sets or controllers.
The effectiveness of this approach was empirically validated
in a high-fidelity building DR simulation. The experimental re-
sults, summarized in Table 3, demonstrate a clear trade-off be-
tween control performance and safety. While an unconstrained
E2E-DPC controller achieved the lowest electricity cost, it did
so at the expense of severe and frequent thermal comfort viola-
tions. In stark contrast, the proposed E2E-DPC with Guarantees
(E2E-DPC-G) successfullyreducedconstraintviolationsbyover
99% compared to the baseline, achieving near-perfect constraint
satisfaction. This outstanding safety performance was realized
with only a minor increase in electricity cost, proving the frame-
work’s ability to find a well-balanced solution. This work opens
a new direction for developing verifiable learning-based control
frameworks that are both high-performing and provably safe,
paving the way for the deployment of real-time, large-scale DR
in smart buildings. Moreover, the inherent structure of the E2E-
DPC, which provides an explicit and computationally efficient
policy, suggests its potential applicability to other safety-critical
domains requiring real-time optimization, such as autonomous
driving and robotics.
6.2. Limitations
Despite the promising results, this study has several limi-
tations that define the scope of its conclusions. First, the the-
oretical guarantees are contingent on the assumption that the
combined model mismatch and external disturbances are uni-
formly bounded. The final certification is also probabilistic in
nature, providing a high-confidence statistical bound rather than
a deterministic guarantee for all possible scenarios. Second, the
proposed framework was validated within a high-fidelity simu-
lation environment. The challenges associated with transferring
the learned policy to a physical building, including the sim-to-
real gap and unmodeled dynamics, were not addressed. Finally,
the scope of the case study was limited to a single building un-
der a fixed TOU tariff, without considering on-site renewable
generation or interactions within a larger energy grid.
6.3. Future Work
The findings and limitations of this study open up several
promising avenues for future research. A primary direction is
scaling the application from a single building to a multi-building
aggregation or a district-level energy system. The computational
efficiency of DPC, which avoids online optimization, makes it
a particularly strong candidate for large-scale control problems.
Unlike traditional learning-based MPC which often requires
computationally intensive distributed optimization techniques
(e.g., ADMM) for large systems, a centralized DPC training
approach could potentially manage such complexity more ef-
fectively. Integrating on-site renewable energy sources, such
as solar PV, would also introduce a more complex and realistic
control challenge.
Another key avenue is the extension of the proposed E2E-
DPCframeworkwithguaranteestoothercomplex, safety-critical
domains. Its ability to provide an explicit, fast-executing policy
that respects constraints makes it highly suitable for applications
in robotics and autonomous driving, where real-time decision-
making is paramount. Future work could explore adapting the
constraint tightening mechanism and training procedure to the
specific dynamics and safety requirements of these fields.
