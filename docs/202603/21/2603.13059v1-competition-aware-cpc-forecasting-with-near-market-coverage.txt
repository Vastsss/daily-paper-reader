Abstract. Cost-per-click (CPC) in paid search is a volatile auction outcome generated by a competitive landscape that is only partially observable from any single advertiser’s history. Using Google Ads auc-tion logs from a concentrated car-rental market (2021–2023), we forecast weekly CPC for 1,811 keyword series and approximate latent competition through complementary signals derived from keyword text, CPC trajec-tories, and geographic market structure. We construct (i) semantic neigh-borhoods and a semantic keyword graph from pretrained transformer-based representations of keyword text, (ii) behavioral neighborhoods via Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) alignment of CPC trajectories, and (iii) geographic-intent covariates capturing localized demand and market-place heterogeneity. We extensively evaluate these signals both as stand-alone covariates and as relational priors in spatiotemporal graph fore-casters, benchmarking them against strong statistical, neural, and time-series foundation-model baselines. Across methods, competition-aware augmentation improves stability and error profiles at business-relevant medium and longer horizons, where competitive regimes shift and volatil-ity is most consequential. The results show that broad market-outcome coverage, combined with keyword-derived semantic and geographic pri-ors, provides a scalable way to approximate latent competition and im-prove CPC forecasting in auction-driven markets. 

Keywords: Cost-per-Click Forecasting · Paid Search · Partial Observ-ability · Spatiotemporal Graph Neural Networks · Time-Series Founda-tion Models 

# 1 Introduction 

Paid search advertising allocates sponsored placements through real-time auc-tions triggered by user queries, and advertisers typically pay under a cost-per-click (CPC) pricing scheme (18; 9; 15). In sponsored search, CPC is not a fixed tariff but an auction-generated outcome determined by bid-dependent rank-ing, platform-estimated quality signals, and query-specific auction conditions

(20; 10; 22). For advertisers, this creates a difficult forecasting problem: CPC directly determines how much demand a fixed budget can buy, yet the competi-tive conditions that generate CPC are only partially observable from any single advertiser’s data. Forecasting CPC is difficult precisely because of this partial observability. Advertisers can observe realized CPC, clicks, impressions, and spend, but they cannot directly observe competitor bids, competitor quality signals, or the full auction state underlying price formation (10). As a result, the observed CPC se-ries contains only an indirect and noisy trace of the competitive landscape. This limits the effectiveness of purely autoregressive forecasting approaches, especially at medium and longer horizons where regime shifts, localized demand changes, and competitor reallocation become more important than short-run inertia. More broadly, while the sponsored-search literature has extensively characterized rank-ing and pricing mechanisms in generalized second-price environments (13; 24), it offers more limited guidance for forecasting under these conditions of indirect competition observability. To address this limitation, we approximate latent competition through ob-servable competition proxies . The key idea is that competition leaves different ob-servable traces: semantic similarity captures substitutable intent, CPC trajectory alignment captures shared behavioral exposure, and localized query intent cap-tures spatially segmented market structure. Using large-scale Google Ads auction logs from the car-rental sector, we operationalize these traces through semantic neighborhoods and a fixed semantic keyword graph, Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)-based behavioral neighborhoods, and geographic-intent covariates that encode localized demand and competitive heterogeneity. Together, these signals provide a scalable approximation to the competitive conditions that are not directly observable from any single advertiser’s history. The car-rental sector provides a particularly suitable empirical setting for this problem because demand is geographically localized, acquisition costs are oper-ationally important, and competition is concentrated among a relatively small set of major advertisers. The raw dataset contains approximately 1.66 billion advertising logs and, after preprocessing and temporal filtering, yields a weekly panel of 1,811 keywords observed over 127 weeks. This scale does not imply full market observability. Rather, it creates a setting that is still partially observed but sufficiently dense to learn structured competition signals from realized out-comes, making it well suited to test whether proxy-based competition modeling improves forecasting in practice. Our contribution is therefore architectural only in a limited sense; the main novelty lies instead in domain-informed proxy construction and forecasting de-sign under partial observability. We benchmark a broad model universe spanning classical statistical and machine-learning baselines, time-series foundation mod-els (including Chronos-2, TimeGPT, and Moirai), and spatiotemporal graph neural networks. We evaluate competition proxies both as exogenous covariates and as relational priors. The most consistent gains emerge at business-relevant medium and longer horizons, where competition-aware augmentation improves Competition-Aware CPC Forecasting with Near-Market Coverage 3

stability and error profiles relative to stronger univariate or weakly contextual baselines. Beyond average forecast accuracy, we show that the value of competition-aware forecasting is concentrated in the hardest parts of the market. CPC dy-namics are highly heterogeneous across keywords: some are relatively cheap and stable, while others are both expensive and volatile. This heterogeneity is eco-nomically important because forecasting failures on high-value, high-volatility keywords carry the greatest budget risk. We therefore complement aggregate evaluation with a competitive frontier perspective that links model performance to keyword-level CPC intensity and volatility, translating predictive gains into a more meaningful notion of operational robustness. The paper makes three contributions. First, it reframes CPC forecasting in paid search as a problem of partial competition observability, arguing that auction-generated prices can be forecast more effectively when latent competi-tion is approximated through observable proxy structure. Second, it operational-izes this idea at industrial scale by constructing semantic, behavioral, and geo-graphic competition proxies and evaluating them across a broad model universe spanning classical baselines, time-series foundation models, and spatiotemporal graph neural networks. Third, it provides substantive evidence that the value of competition-aware forecasting is horizon- and regime-dependent: geographic structure acts as a strong stabilizing prior for foundation models, semantic and graph-based structure are most useful for relational spillovers, and the largest gains concentrate in high-CPC, high-volatility keyword regimes. 

# 2 Related Work 

CPC and paid search auctions. In Generalized Second Price (GSP) auctions, ad slots are assigned by ranking advertisers on an effective score that combines bids with platform-estimated quality factors (16; 6). Realized CPC is therefore an endogenous auction outcome shaped by rival bids, click-through-rate (CTR) predictions, and latent demand shifts (1; 11). A large literature has characterized these pricing and ranking mechanisms, as well as the role of CTR estimation in sponsored search (32). However, this literature is more informative about how CPC is generated than about how CPC should be forecast when the compet-itive state is only indirectly observed from one advertiser’s data. The closest adjacent empirical work is (23), which studies keyword-level CPC forecasting with competitive-landscape covariates, but does not evaluate foundation-style forecasters, does not construct text-derived relational structure, and does not compare covariate-based and graph-based routes within a unified forecasting framework. 

Time-series forecasting and foundation models. Classical baselines such as ARIMA and Prophet remain common reference points in business forecasting (27), in-cluding advertising applications (23), but they are often ill-suited to the nonlin-ear and non-stationary dynamics of auction-generated CPC series. Deep learn-ing models, including LSTMs and Transformer-based forecasters, capture richer 4

temporal structure (7; 28), yet typically require task-specific training and care-ful feature design. More recently, time-series foundation models (TSFMs) such as TimeGPT (12), Chronos (4), and related pretrained forecasters have shown strong zero-shot and few-shot performance across domains. However, their ap-plication to advertising cost forecasting remains limited, and in particular their interaction with competition-aware exogenous covariates has not been system-atically studied. 

Graph-based forecasting and relational competition modeling. Graph Neural Net-works have become a standard tool for spatiotemporal forecasting when depen-dencies across series can be represented relationally, especially in domains such as traffic, energy, and sensor networks where graph structure is naturally avail-able. By contrast, CPC forecasting in paid search lacks an obvious physical topology: dependence arises from substitution, shared intent, and localized mar-ket overlap rather than geographic adjacency or network connectivity. Existing work in online advertising has focused primarily on auction design, ranking, and CTR prediction rather than relational forecasting of advertising costs (31; 32). This means that, in CPC forecasting, the central graph-modeling question is not only how to propagate information over a graph, but how to construct a rela-tional graph reflecting competition landscape in the first place. That requirement creates a natural role for fixed semantic graphs and neighborhood-based compe-tition proxies. 

Research gap. Three gaps follow from this literature. First, sponsored-search re-search provides rich auction-theoretic intuition but limited guidance for forecast-ing under partial competition observability. Second, advertising-cost forecasting has not yet systematically incorporated modern time-series foundation models in a keyword-level CPC setting. Third, existing graph-based forecasting work offers little evidence on whether text-derived, behavior-derived, and geography-derived competition structure can improve advertising-cost prediction, either as exogenous covariates or as relational priors. Our paper addresses these gaps with an applied forecasting framework that operationalizes semantic, behavioral, and geographic competition proxies and evaluates them across both covariate-augmented TSFMs and spatiotemporal GNNs. The contribution is therefore not a new universal forecasting architecture, but a competition-aware forecasting design that makes latent market structure usable across heterogeneous model families. 

# 3 Business Context and Data Characteristics 

Paid search advertising is one of the most important performance channels in digital marketing, allocating sponsored placements through real-time auctions triggered by user queries (18; 15). In these auctions, advertisers pay predomi-nantly under a cost-per-click (CPC) scheme, meaning acquisition costs are di-rectly tied to user interactions rather than impressions (9; 3). For car rental Competition-Aware CPC Forecasting with Near-Market Coverage 5

firms, this mechanism creates a practical risk: CPC shocks compress contribu-tion margins and can make previously profitable demand segments unprofitable within days. Car rental is a particularly strong use case because it combines (i) intense competition among a relatively small set of major rental groups and interme-diaries, (ii) high online price transparency that amplifies competitive pressure, and (iii) pronounced seasonality and demand concentration around airports and major cities. In advertising terms, this means that competition repeatedly oc-curs over overlapping high-intent keywords rather than being dispersed across a highly fragmented advertiser base. Because auction prices are shaped by com-petitors’ behavior and latent market shifts, CPC dynamics are only partially controllable from a single advertiser’s viewpoint (23). This makes keyword-level CPC forecasting an operational tool for budget planning, bid adjustment, and proactive spend steering under uncertainty. 

3.1 Data source and raw event schema 

We build on a large-scale dataset of keyword-level Google Ads performance logs for the car rental industry (2021–2023), provided by a European business intel-ligence company. The raw dataset contains approximately 1.66 billion log-level observations. Each observation corresponds to an advertising event with (i) tex-tual fields (keyword, matched search query, landing-page URL), (ii) categori-cal context (e.g., device category, search type), and (iii) numeric performance metrics (impressions, clicks, and advertising cost). This granularity reflects the operational reality of auction-driven paid search and allows us to construct a temporally indexed keyword panel suitable for forecasting. After filtering, the retained domain universe remains concentrated around a limited set of continuously active major brands and intermediaries. Descriptive inspection of the retained domains shows a steep concentration pattern, with a small head of domains accounting for disproportionately large activity and a long tail of sporadic or low-coverage domains. Thus, while the dataset does not imply full market observability, it provides broad realized outcome coverage and repeated competitive interactions within the focal vertical. 

3.2 Sample construction and panel design 

The raw volume makes single-pass, in-memory preprocessing infeasible, so we implement a batch-wise pipeline with parallel execution (26). We first apply a relevance filter by restricting the data to observations with car-rental intent (keywords and matched queries containing car and rental ) and drop records without sufficient information to observe CPC. 

Domain-level filtering. Landing-page URLs are parsed into registrable domains using tldextract (17). To remove spurious traffic from unrelated sites intermit-tently matched to car-rental queries, we apply a domain-quality filter: domains 6

with more than 15 missing dates and fewer than 1,000 total mentions are ex-cluded. This step is substantively important, not only as data cleaning but also as sample construction: it retains a more stable competitive set of major rental agencies, aggregators, and booking intermediaries while reducing low-coverage noise from irrelevant or sporadically matched domains. 

Keyword normalization and selection. To reduce artificial sparsity caused by lexical variants, keywords are normalized to a canonical form: lowercasing, punc-tuation and redundant symbol removal, ASCII transliteration, and whitespace standardization. From the cleaned partitions, we construct a keyword–week in-dex and retain only keywords observed in at least 110 distinct weeks out of a 127-week window to ensure robust temporal presence. This results in a fore-casting universe of 1,811 keyword series, reducing short-lived or intermittently observed terms that more likely reflect transient campaigns or data artifacts rather than persistent market behavior. 

3.3 Weekly aggregation and target definition

t, we compute weekly impressions, clicks, and advertising cost as sums over raw events, and define weekly CPC as:

The resulting panel contains 218,924 keyword–week obser-vations and serves as the input to all subsequent forecasting models. This weekly aggregation provides a practical compromise between auction-level noise reduc-tion and managerial relevance, as weekly CPC aligns more closely with campaign planning and budget-steering cycles than raw event-level outcomes. 

3.4 Exploratory analysis: heavy tails and the “competitive frontier” 

The aggregated KPI distributions exhibit strong right skewness and heavy-tailed behavior typical of paid-search markets: compressed interquartile ranges coex-ist with extreme upper tails in impressions, clicks, cost, and CPC. For CPC, the weekly mean is 2.86 while the maximum reaches 80.16, with a pronounced upper tail (p99 = 12.13) and skewness of 3.34. Traffic and spend metrics are sub-stantially more extreme (e.g., impressions_sum skewness ≈ 26 .98 ), reflecting a Pareto-like structure where a small “head” of generic, high-competition queries generates disproportionate volume relative to a large long tail of niche searches (8). Importantly, outlier frequency is not consistent with Gaussian assumptions: values exceeding a 3 σ threshold occur systematically in the high-volume segment, indicating that extreme deviations are structural rather than rare anomalies (21). # 4 Methodology: Competition-Aware Forecasting under Partial Observability 

Keyword-level CPC forecasting in sponsored search is a prediction problem un-der partial observability. While advertisers observe realized CPC, impressions, clicks, and spend, they do not observe competitor bids, budget constraints, qual-ity scores, or the full auction state that generates prices. Our objective is there-fore not to recover the latent auction state structurally, but to approximate competition through observable proxies that improve multi-horizon CPC fore-casting. Our methodological contribution lies primarily in proxy construction and proxy representation under partial observability, rather than in proposing a new forecasting architecture. As illustrated in Figure 2, we operationalize latent competition through observable semantic, behavioral, and geographic signals, and study two implementation routes. In the first, these proxies enter forecast-ing models as exogenous covariates. In the second, competition is represented relationally and supplied to spatiotemporal graph neural networks (STGNNs) through a fixed semantic adjacency matrix. Across both routes, the target is weekly CPC at forecast horizon h ∈ { 1, 6, 12 }.8

4.1 Competition Proxy Extraction 

We derive three complementary proxy families from observable data: semantic structure, behavioral alignment, and geographic intent. Each proxy is designed to capture a different aspect of competition that is not directly observable from any single advertiser’s history. 

Semantic neighborhoods. Keywords with similar user intent often compete for overlapping advertising inventory even when they differ lexically. To capture this intent-level proximity, we encode each keyword ki using pretrained transformer-based text representations from all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (25); the role of the language model is representational rather than generative, i.e., it supplies fixed semantic embeddings rather than free-form reasoning or text generation. This yields an embedding ei ∈ R384 . Pairwise cosine similarity is then used to identify semanti-cally related keywords. These semantic neighborhoods serve two purposes in our framework: they provide neighborhood-based competition covariates and they define the topology of a fixed semantic keyword graph. Competition-Aware CPC Forecasting with Near-Market Coverage 9

Behavioral neighborhoods via DTW. Textual similarity does not exhaust compet-itive relatedness. Keywords with weak lexical overlap may still experience similar CPC dynamics because they are exposed to common demand shocks, campaign timing, or auction reallocation. We therefore construct behavioral neighborhoods using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW), which measures similarity between CPC trajectories while allowing local temporal misalignment (14). To avoid patho-logical alignments, we impose a Sakoe–Chiba band constraint. In the covariate route, these neighbors are summarized into leakage-free behavioral competition features constructed from historical CPC trajectories only, capturing behav-iorally synchronized keywords whose competitive dynamics evolve similarly over time. 

Geographic intent. In the car-rental setting, auction pressure is strongly local-ized. Queries tied to airports, cities, or countries reflect distinct demand pools and competitive conditions. We extract geographic intent from keyword text, cu-rated gazetteers, and hierarchical geographic mapping. Each keyword is assigned structured location indicators at the continent, country, and city levels. Because geographic granularity may trade off specificity against sparsity, we retain multi-ple hierarchical resolutions and evaluate them empirically rather than assuming 

ex ante that finer localization is always more informative. These geography-derived variables act as spatial competition proxies, encoding localized demand conditions and marketplace heterogeneity. 

4.2 Proxy Representation: Covariates and Relational Structure 

The extracted competition proxies enter the forecasting problem through two different representations. The first route treats competition information as ex-ogenous conditioning variables. These covariates include leakage-free summaries of neighboring CPC histories derived from semantic and DTW neighborhoods, together with structured geographic-intent indicators and core operational con-trols. This route allows forecasting models to condition on observable approxi-mations to latent competition without imposing an explicit graph topology. The second route represents competition relationally. Using the keyword embeddings 

ei, we construct a fixed semantic graph Asem ∈ RN ×N over the keyword uni-verse. For each node, directed edges are assigned to its k nearest semantic neigh-bors under cosine similarity, with k = 10 selected as a stable and competitive graph sparsity level in preliminary experiments. The adjacency matrix is row-normalized before model training. We focus on the semantic graph because it provides a stable, text-grounded, and directly comparable relational prior across forecasting architectures through which STGNNs propagate cross-keyword in-formation. 

4.3 Forecasting Architectures 

We evaluate three model classes: classical and neural non-graph baselines, covariate-augmented time-series foundation models, and spatiotemporal graph neural net-works. 10 

Classical and neural baselines. We benchmark a broad suite of traditional fore-casting models, including statistical approaches (SARIMAX), tree-based ma-chine learning models (XGBoost, Random Forest, LightGBM), deep neural net-works (MLP, LSTM, GRU), and a tabular foundation model (TabPFN). These baselines establish the reference performance attainable from purely autoregres-sive and weakly contextual histories. 

Covariate-augmented foundation models. We benchmark state-of-the-art time-series foundation models (TSFMs), including Chronos-2 (2), TimeGPT (12), and Moirai (29), under exogenous conditioning. In this setting, competition proxies are supplied as covariates alongside the historical CPC series and core opera-tional inputs. This route allows us to test whether large pretrained forecasters can absorb competition-aware structure without explicitly imposing a relational topology. 

Spatiotemporal graph neural networks. To model cross-keyword dependence ex-plicitly, we evaluate STGNNs that consume the fixed semantic graph Asem . The input panel is represented as a spatiotemporal tensor 

X ∈ RN ×T ×F ,

where N is the number of keywords, T the input history length, and F the number of features. The model outputs multi-horizon forecasts ˆYt+h ∈ RN .We focus on three representative STGNN architectures. DCRNN models competition as diffusion over the predefined graph within a recurrent forecasting framework (19). GConvLSTM integrates graph convolutions directly into LSTM gating, providing a stable convolutional-recurrent formulation. GraphWaveNet combines fixed graph structure with an adaptive component, allowing limited refinement of the exogenous relational prior (30). All STGNNs are trained glob-ally on the CPC panel using a leakage-free chronological split and optimized with Mean Absolute Error (MAE), which is more robust than squared-error objectives under the heavy-tailed distribution of CPC.

Three primary patterns emerge. First, isolated or weakly contextual keyword histories reach a clear performance ceiling: the strongest baseline, TabPFN, at-tains 35.04% sMAPE, while SARIMAX remains substantially worse. Second, competition-aware TSFMs lower the global error floor materially, with Chronos-2 utilizing geographic intent covariates reaching 27.14% sMAPE, the strongest overall result. This indicates that coarse geographic localization acts as a power-ful stabilizing prior for universal forecasters. Third, explicit relational modeling adds substantial value: DCRNN, leveraging the semantic keyword graph along-side semantic-neighbor CPC signals, reduces sMAPE to 30.42%, providing a structurally distinct alternative that systematically outperforms the non-graph baseline family. 

5.4 Competitive Frontier Analysis 

To assess where competition-aware proxy design matters most, we conduct a structured ablation across the GNN family and segment the keyword panel by CPC intensity and volatility. This segmentation yields well-populated partitions, with the high-CPC / high-volatility “competitive frontier” quadrant containing 402 strategically critical keywords. Ablation results confirm that the effectiveness of proxy integration depends heavily on the planning horizon and the targeted regime. At the 6-week horizon, the strongest specification combines geography with semantic-neighbor CPC in-formation ( Core + Geo + Sem CPC ), improving overall sMAPE from 31.61% to 30.71%. More importantly, this proxy combination lowers the error in the highly unstable, high-volatility segment by 1.3 percentage points relative to core-only inputs. At 12 weeks, the simpler Core + Continent setup becomes the optimal prior, reducing overall error from 38.32% to 37.93%, highlighting that coarse geography serves as a highly robust anchor against long-term drift. The data reveals that indiscriminate feature stacking is actively harmful in noisy auction environments. At the 6-week mark, naive aggregation of all avail-able proxies produces the worst performance (34.0% sMAPE, 3.3 points worse than the optimal selective setup); at 12 weeks, this performance gap widens to 5.2 points. As such, these findings prove that proxy design matters most in the hardest-to-forecast regions of the market, and that selective, theoretically grounded augmentation is strictly superior to exhaustive feature accumulation. 

# 6 Conclusion 

This paper framed CPC forecasting in paid search as a problem of partial com-petition observability . Because advertisers cannot directly observe competitor bids, quality signals, or the full auction state, realized CPC series provide only an indirect and noisy trace of the competitive landscape. Our results show that augmenting forecasting models with competition-aware proxies materially im-proves forecast stability at the business-relevant medium and longer horizons where purely autoregressive approaches become less reliable. Across experiments, three findings emerge consistently. First, coarse geo-graphic intent is the most robust competition prior: continent-level encoding im-proves stability across backbones and horizons, while finer country- and city-level encodings tend to fragment the signal and reduce robustness. Second, semantic-neighbor CPC signals provide complementary gains, particularly at the 6-week planning horizon and especially for diffusion-based and adaptive graph models that are well suited to capturing cross-keyword competitive spillovers. Third, broad feature accumulation is not beneficial; targeted proxy selection consis-tently outperforms indiscriminate stacking, indicating that competition-aware augmentation must be selective rather than exhaustive. These gains are most meaningful in the part of the market where forecast-ing errors are most costly. The competitive frontier analysis shows that im-provements concentrate in high-CPC, high-volatility keywords, where auction pressure is strongest and budget risk is highest. This is an important practical result: competition-aware modeling does not merely improve average accuracy, but improves robustness where operational consequences are greatest. More broadly, the findings show that domain-informed proxy construction, combined with spatiotemporal graph forecasting, provides a scalable way to re-cover latent competition structure from observable signals. The strongest graph-based specifications outperform the best non-graph baselines by a substantial margin at the 1-week and 6-week horizons, with a smaller but still meaningful advantage at 12 weeks. This suggests that the value of the proposed approach comes not only from model flexibility, but from relational priors that better reflect the auction-driven structure of CPC formation. Several limitations remain. The analysis is based on a single vertical with a relatively concentrated competitive landscape, so generalization to broader or less concentrated paid-search categories remains an open question. In addition, the semantic graph is fixed over time and therefore cannot capture evolving keyword relationships, market entry and exit, or shifting competitive regimes. Future work should examine dynamic graph construction, richer competitor-side signals where available, and validation across additional industries and auction settings. Overall, the paper shows that competition structure in auction-driven adver-tising markets is partially recoverable from observable signals such as keyword text, CPC trajectories, and geographic intent. Under the informational asym-metry imposed by advertising platforms, advertisers can therefore improve CPC forecasting by investing in principled competition proxy construction rather than relying solely on autoregressive extrapolation. Coarse geography is the most robust competition prior Across backbones at 6 weeks, continent-level geographic intent is consistently the strongest geographic encoding. As shown in Table 4, averaged across models, continent dummies out-perform country dummies by 0.61 sMAPE points and city dummies by 0.93 points. The main reason is not semantic richness, but statistical stability: coarse geography captures durable differences in market intensity and seasonality with-out fragmenting the training signal.
