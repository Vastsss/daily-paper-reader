# LLMs for Agentic Home Energy Management 

Sokipriala Jonah 

Centre for Computational Science and Mathematical Modelling Coventry University Coventry, United Kingdom 

Abstract —Home Energy Management Systems (HEMS) can reduce residential electricity costs and support demand re-sponse, but adoption is limited by the difficulty of translating household preferences into technical scheduling constraints. This paper evaluates whether large language model (LLM) agents can provide a practical natural-language interface for multi-appliance home energy scheduling. We present a tool-calling ReAct agent that uses live half-hourly Octopus Agile prices, weather forecasts, photovoltaic generation estimates, household usage data, and a retrieval-augmented knowledge base to sched-ule flexible loads against a mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) ground truth. Three commercial models, GPT-4o-mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash, and Claude Sonnet 4.6, are benchmarked across tariff days, constraint-conflict scenarios, weather-aware solar co-optimization, and week-long deployment. With native function calling, all models achieve 100% scheduling success and near-MILP optimality, while text-parsed action interfaces sharply reduce reliability. Constraint testing shows that cost-optimal and safety-optimal models differ: Claude is strongest under infeasibil-ity and power-cap conflicts, while GPT-4o-mini is most efficient. Over a simulated week, agents capture 96.7–98.0% of oracle savings, projecting approximately £1,270 annual savings over an off-peak timer baseline. Code and a live demonstration are available at https://github.com/sokistar24/ecohome-energy-agent and https://www.ecohomeagent.com/. 

Index Terms —Agentic AI, home energy management systems, large language models, demand response, load scheduling, solar self-consumption, dynamic retail tariffs, LLM benchmarking. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# THE decarbonization of electricity systems requires rising shares of variable renewable generation to be integrated while maintaining reliable and affordable supply. This creates a growing need for demand-side flexibility [1], [2]. The International Energy Agency projects that global demand-response capacity must grow roughly tenfold between 2020 and 2030, with buildings and residential electric vehicles (EVs) contributing much of this potential [3], [4], [5]. The residential sector is especially attractive because flexible loads, including EV charging, wet appliances, heating, and cooling, can often be shifted in time without materially affecting the service delivered to occupants [6], [7]. Home Energy Management Systems (HEMS) are intended to realize this flexibility by scheduling household devices against price and generation signals. Although HEMS have demonstrated cost reductions and demand-response value, de-ployment remains below the level required for decarbonization targets [3], [8]. A key barrier is the user-interaction burden: conventional HEMS require households to translate every-day preferences into technical parameters, which discourages adoption among non-expert users [9], [10]. Large language models (LLMs) offer a possible route around this barrier. Modern LLMs can follow task instructions without example demonstrations [11], [12], [13], invoke exter-nal tools, and, when organized as agents that interleave rea-soning with actions [14], [15], decompose a natural language request into data retrieval, analysis, and control decisions. Beyond reducing the parameterization burden, a conversational agent can explain its recommendations, answer ad hoc ques-tions about consumption and tariffs, and ground its advice in a curated knowledge base of energy-saving practice through retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). A small but growing body of work has begun to apply LLMs to home energy management, including conversational energy advice, natural-language interfaces to optimizers or model-predictive controllers, and autonomous scheduling agents [16], [17], [18], [19], [20]. Most of these systems use the LLM primarily as an interface layer: the model interprets a user request, provides advice, or extracts parameters that are then passed to a conventional controller or optimizer. The strongest move toward autonomous LLM-based scheduling is the agentic HEMS of El Makroum et al. [17], where an LLM orchestrator delegates to per-appliance specialist agents and commits schedules for a washing machine, dishwasher, and EV charger against Austrian day-ahead wholesale prices. Benchmarked against MILP optima, that system achieved perfect multi-appliance coordination with Llama-3.3-70B. However, two other open-source models, Qwen-3-32B and GPT-OSS-120B, failed to coordinate all appliances despite perfect single-appliance performance. The study also reported that all models required explicit prompt guidance to answer analytical price queries. These results establish the feasibility of LLM-based HEMS orchestration, but they also expose four open questions that motivate the present paper: 1) Model coverage. Existing autonomous scheduling re-sults are limited to open-source models. Since prior work explicitly suggests that stronger commercial models may improve orchestration [17], it remains unclear whether reported coordination failures are intrinsic to LLM-based scheduling or specific to the models and action interfaces tested. 2) Price-only optimization. Existing autonomous schedul-ing is based on day-ahead wholesale prices alone. For households with rooftop PV, the relevant objective is net cost after solar self-consumption, which requires weather-aware PV forecasting and changes the structure of the optimal schedule. 3) Single-day evaluation. Prior autonomous HEMS eval-uation uses a single price day and does not provide

Existing evaluations do not observe cases where the cost-optimal schedule vio-lates a user constraint. As a result, agent behaviour under deadline, calendar, or household power-cap conflicts remains a safety-relevant blind spot. We address these gaps with an agentic HEMS that improves both data grounding and evaluation rigor. The system is designed to operate on retail market data, specifically the half-hourly Octopus Energy Agile tariff in Great Britain, which exposes households to wholesale price volatility including negative-price events. It also uses weather forecasts to estimate on-site PV generation and optimize net electricity cost after solar self-consumption. Architecturally, we use a single tool-calling ReAct agent with native function calling rather than a hierarchical multi-agent design. This allows us to test whether stronger commercial models with structured tool interfaces can remove the need for the manual task decomposition used in earlier open-source deployments. 

A. Contributions 

This paper makes three contributions:  

> •

C1 (Multi-day model benchmark). We provide a sta-tistically grounded comparison of commercial models from three providers, namely GPT-4o-mini, Gemini 2.5 Flash, and Claude Sonnet 4.6, for agentic multi-appliance HEMS scheduling. The models are evaluated over 12 distinct Agile tariff days spanning low-, medium-, and high-volatility regimes, with MILP ground truth, native function-calling versus text-parsed action interfaces, and analysis of cost, latency, and optimality. The benchmark culminates in a week-long rolling deployment simulation with statistically projected four-week costs, compared against MILP oracles and rule-based baselines.  

> •

C2 (Constraint-conflict stress suite). We introduce a reusable adversarial scenario suite in which cost min-imization conflicts with deadlines, appliance windows compete under a household power cap, calendar con-straints are irregular or infeasible, and tools fail mid-run. We evaluate deadline-satisfaction rate, constraint-violation rate, feasibility cost premium, and failure modes derived from agent traces.  

> •

C3 (Weather-aware solar co-optimization). We extend the agentic scheduling objective from price-only cost to net cost with PV self-consumption, using live forecast tooling and an irradiance-to-generation model. The result-ing schedules are benchmarked against an extended MILP formulation that no longer decomposes per appliance, and are further analyzed for sensitivity to forecast error.

A. LLM Agents and Agentic AI 

Recent taxonomies distinguish single-purpose AI agents 

from agentic AI systems, in which an LLM acts as an autonomous reasoning engine that decomposes objectives, selects tools, and coordinates multi-step workflows without relying on predefined pipelines [21], [22], [23]. Hierarchical reasoning, adaptability, and tool-mediated decision-making are commonly identified as the capabilities that separate agentic systems from conventional automation [24]. The dominant implementation pattern is ReAct [14], [25], [26], which inter-leaves reasoning steps with tool invocations and observations, thereby producing inspectable traces that support debugging, validation, and failure analysis. This capability is particularly relevant for HEMS. Since capable LLMs can operate without example demonstrations [11], [27], [28], agentic systems can be specified through task instructions and tool descriptions alone. This matters in residential energy management because household users cannot be expected to provide example scheduling requests or encode their preferences as technical constraints. A further practical advance is native function calling , where commercial model APIs return structured tool calls directly instead of relying on brittle parsing of free-form action strings. Whether this interface difference materially improves orchestration reli-ability in energy applications remains untested. Experiment 1 measures this effect directly. 

B. LLMs in Energy System Applications 

Surveys of LLMs in the electricity sector identify domain adaptation, including retrieval augmentation, tool grounding, and fine-tuning, as important prerequisites for reliable deploy-ment [29], [30], [27], [28]. Existing applications span several roles. As code generators , LLMs have produced optimization formulations for EV charging and demand-side management [31], [32], [33], [34]. As diagnostic assistants , customized models have routed tasks and retrieved domain knowledge for building operation and maintenance, outperforming general models on fault diagnosis [35]. As controllers , an agentic HVAC system deployed in operating offices achieved energy savings and comfort improvements, although in a single commercial building rather than a residential multi-appliance setting [36]. As decision evaluators , LLMs have recommended residential retrofits with high top-k accuracy, but with limited contextual reasoning [37]. In smart-home applications, LLMs have generated Home-Assistant automation rules from natural language [38], [39], been combined with digital-twin context for general energy-saving actions [40], provided conversational energy advice without direct device control [41], interpreted user commands for a model-predictive controller that performs battery schedul-ing [42], and extracted scheduling parameters from dialogue for a conventional optimizer [10]. Across these systems, the LLM usually acts as an interface, assistant, or preprocessing layer in front of a traditional decision-making component, rather than as the entity that directly commits schedules. 3

C. Agentic HEMS and Positioning of This Work 

El Makroum et al. [17] move beyond the interface-only role by allowing an LLM orchestrator to make and commit scheduling decisions. Their system delegates to specialist appliance agents and matches MILP-optimal schedules with Llama-3.3-70B on Austrian day-ahead prices. This demon-strates that LLM-based HEMS orchestration is feasible, but four limitations define the departure point for our work. First, the model coverage is limited to open-source check-points, two of which fail multi-appliance coordination. It remains unclear whether these failures reflect an inherent limi-tation of LLM orchestration or the specific capability level and action interface of the tested models. Second, the optimization signal is one-dimensional: scheduling is based on wholesale price only, with no weather input, on-site generation, or self-consumption objective. This omits a central requirement for PV-equipped households, which are a natural constituency for HEMS. Third, the evaluation uses a single day of prices, leaving robustness across price-volatility regimes untested. Fourth, the reported scenarios do not create cases where the cost optimum violates a user constraint, so constraint-conflict behaviour is not observed. A further methodological gap is that, as in much of the current literature, the reported success rates are not accompanied by statistical testing. This paper addresses these gaps by evaluating commercial function-calling models over multiple tariff regimes, extending the objective to weather-aware PV self-consumption, introduc-ing explicit constraint-conflict stress tests, and benchmarking sustained deployment against MILP oracles and rule-based household baselines. A. Overview 

Fig. 1 shows the proposed system. A single LLM-based agent, implemented using the LangGraph ReAct construction, receives a natural language request such as “When should I charge my EV tomorrow to minimise cost?”. It then follows a reason and act loop: it selects a tool, observes the result, and repeats this process until it can commit a schedule and produce a recommendation. The system prompt defines the agent’s role as an energy advisor, specifies the workflow it should follow, and sets the expected recommendation format. In particular, the agent is instructed to retrieve prices and weather before recommending a schedule, ground advice in retrieved energy-saving tips, and quantify the expected savings. Temperature is fixed at 0 for reproducibility. Unlike the hierarchical orchestrator and specialist-agent de-sign of [17], all coordination in this system is handled by one agent. This deliberately minimal design allows Experiment 1 to test whether commercial models with native function calling can sustain multi-appliance coordination without manual task decomposition. 

B. Tool Layer 

The agent has access to the following six tools, which together define its complete action space for the scheduling task:  

> •

Electricity price retrieval returns half-hourly import prices from the Octopus Energy Agile tariff [43], mea-sured in GBP/kWh over 48 daily slots, for a specified date. Agile passes day-ahead wholesale market dynamics through to a retail tariff, including occasional negative prices. Since prices are published before the delivery day, Agile provides a realistic and reproducible dynamic-pricing signal for GB households.  

> •

Weather forecast retrieval returns hourly forecasts for temperature, cloud cover, and global horizontal irradiance from Open-Meteo [44] for the household location and a specified date.  

> •

PV generation prediction converts forecast irradiance and temperature into 48 half-hourly slots of expected PV output using the model in Sec. V-D1.  

> •

Cheapest-window computation returns the cheapest and most expensive candidate start-slot windows for a given run length, factoring out slot arithmetic that would oth-erwise consume reasoning tokens.  

> •

Appliance scheduling commits an appliance’s start slot for a specified date after validating that it fits within the horizon. This is the only tool whose invocation the scorer treats as a scheduling decision; prose recommendations are not executed.  

> •

Infeasibility reporting formally records that a request cannot be satisfied under the stated constraints. The correct behaviour on the S4 scenarios of Sec. V-C is to invoke this tool rather than commit a schedule that violates a constraint. Experiment 1 uses a price-only subset with Weather forecast retrieval and PV generation prediction removed, so that comparison against the price-only MILP is apples-to-apples; all other experiments use the full six-tool set. The deployed web system additionally provides RAG-based advisory re-trieval, a household usage database, and a savings calculator; these are documented in Appendix D and are not exercised by any experiment. 

C. Model Backends and Action Interfaces 

The agent is model-agnostic. We evaluate three commer-cial models, one per provider: GPT-4o-mini from OpenAI, Gemini 2.5 Flash from Google, and Claude Sonnet 4.6 from Anthropic. These models occupy broadly comparable price and latency tiers, so cross-model differences are interpreted as model and interface effects rather than tier effects (Table II). Any model slot can be replaced by that provider’s frontier tier without changing the evaluation harness, although that extension is left contingent on budget. Open-source models are not re-run locally. Instead, com-parisons to open-source systems are made against the results reported in [17], clearly labelled as literature values obtained under a different market and architecture. All commercial models are accessed using pinned model identifiers recorded for each run, since provider-side updates can otherwise weaken reproducibility. D. Temporal Grounding 

LLMs do not share a consistent notion of “now”. Some backends are given the real-world date at inference time, while others infer it from tool outputs or training priors. As a result, a relative request such as “tomorrow” can resolve to different calendar days across models. We observed this directly during development, when the agent anchored “today” to the seeding date of the sample database rather than the wall-clock date. All evaluations therefore pin time explicitly. Each run declares the evaluation date and time, all tools resolve relative dates against that declared date rather than the system clock, and archived price and weather data are keyed to it. This ensures that every model faces the same reproducible temporal frame. 

E. Security and Scope Guarding 

User inputs pass pre-LLM validation before reaching the agent. This includes length limits, malformed-input rejection, and pattern-based screening for prompt-injection attempts [45]. The system prompt also restricts the agent to the home-energy domain. These measures follow the defence-in-depth approach of [17]. All experiments in this paper use valid in-domain requests, and adversarial red-teaming is outside the scope of the evaluation. IV. P ROBLEM FORMULATION

A. Model Funnel, Champion Selection, and Baselines 

Commercial inference is metered, so model calls are allo-cated to the experiments needed to support each claim. All three models are evaluated in Experiments 1, 2, and 3, and in the rolling deployment simulation of Experiment 4a. The forecast-noise sweep in Experiment 3 and the weekly joint-planning task in Experiment 4b are run only on a single champion model. The champion is selected using a rule fixed before collecting results. The primary criterion is the product of multi-appliance success rate and optimality rate in Experiment 1. If models tie, the first tie-breaker is guided-prompt deadline satisfaction plus infeasibility-reporting rate in Experiment 2. The final tie-breaker is monetary cost per successful schedule. Alongside the MILP oracle, all experiments include rule-based baselines computed at zero inference cost over the same archived data. These baselines represent common household scheduling strategies: a static off-peak timer that starts all flexible loads at 00:00 with the EV first, an immediate-start policy that runs loads on request, and a greedy cheapest-single-slot heuristic that ignores deadlines and the household power cap. The gap between these baselines and the MILP oracle defines the available savings. Each policy is reported as the share of that gap it captures. 

B. Experiment 1: Multi-Day Model Benchmark 

Hypothesis H1. Commercial API models achieve reliable multi-appliance coordination, closing the gap reported for open-source models in [17], and native function calling im-proves reliability over text-parsed actions. 

Protocol. Each model schedules a single appliance and all three appliances simultaneously across D = 12 distinct Agile tariff days. The days are drawn from an Agile price archive covering 2026-04-20 to 2026-06-20 and stratified into low-, medium-, and high-volatility terciles using the coefficient of variation of Ct. Days containing negative-price slots are retained and flagged. Each model, day, and scenario cell is repeated R = 3 times at temperature 0. Where supported, models are evaluated using both native function calling and text-parsed ReAct actions. Archived prices are fixed for each evaluation day so that all models face identical inputs. 

Analysis. Success and optimality proportions are compared using Fisher’s exact test, with pairwise comparisons against the strongest baseline and Holm–Bonferroni correction. Nonzero cost gaps are compared using the Mann–Whitney U test. Efficiency metrics are reported as mean ± standard deviation. The headline synthesis is a Pareto analysis over optimality rate, latency, and monetary cost per successful schedule. 

C. Experiment 2: Constraint-Conflict Stress Testing 

Hypothesis H2. When cost optimality and hard constraints conflict, models differ systematically in how they prioritize constraints, and explicit conflict-resolution guidance improves reliable deadline satisfaction. 

Scenario suite. Six scenario families are instantiated on real Agile days selected, or minimally edited with edits documented, so that the relevant conflict provably binds. The full scenario matrix is given in Appendix B. 

S1 tests deadline conflict: the globally cheapest EV charging window violates the calendar deadline, while the cheapest 7

feasible window costs δ ∈ { 5, 10 , 25 , 50 }% more. This sweeps the price of constraint compliance. 

S2 tests power-cap conflict: appliance cost optima overlap under a household cap of P cap = 9 kW. Since the EV charger alone draws 7.4 kW, it cannot run concurrently with another appliance, forcing serialization. 

S3 tests irregular calendar interpretation, including a night-shift pattern, multiple candidate deadline events, and an event whose end time rather than start time defines the relevant constraint window. 

S4 tests infeasibility: no feasible window of length da exists before the calendar deadline tcal  

> a

. The correct behaviour is to report infeasibility rather than fabricate a schedule or silently relax the constraint. 

S5 tests instruction conflict: the user’s stated instruction, such as “charge overnight”, conflicts with a binding calendar constraint such as an early-morning departure. 

S6 tests tool-failure recovery by injecting a price API error on the first call and measuring whether the model retries successfully. Each scenario is run under two prompts: a baseline prompt and a guided prompt containing explicit conflict-resolution rules. The guided prompt states that hard constraints dominate cost and that infeasible cases should be reported rather than violated, following the progressive guidance methodology of [17]. 

Metrics. We report deadline-satisfaction rate, constraint-violation rate, feasibility cost premium relative to the MILP premium, infeasibility-reporting rate for S4, recovery rate for S6, and a qualitative failure-mode taxonomy derived from event traces. Failure modes are assigned deterministically by the scorer. The conversational short-circuiting pattern reported in Section VI-A was verified by inspecting all 32 failing Claude single-appliance traces, each of which contained zero tool calls and a prose confirmation.

E. Experiment 4: Longitudinal Deployment and Weekly Plan-ning 

Hypothesis H4. Over sustained operation, the champion agent captures most of the savings available to a per-day MILP oracle, strictly dominates rule-based scheduling, and extends its coordination ability from daily scheduling to joint weekly planning. Because the Agile tariff is published only one day ahead, neither the agent nor the optimizer can observe prices beyond the next delivery day. Longer-duration evaluation is therefore operationalized in two information-honest ways. 

1) 4a: Week-Long Rolling Deployment Simulation with Projected Four-Week Costs: Over 7 consecutive archived days spanning both weekdays and a weekend, each model is invoked once per evening using that day’s published prices and weather forecast. It then commits the next day’s schedule. Committed schedules are scored on realized data, and costs accumulate across the week. Cumulative realized net cost is compared against the per-day extended MILP oracle, the per-day price-only MILP, and the rule-based baselines in Sec. V-A. Three independent passes over the week are run for each model to bound run-to-run variation, giving 3 × 7 × 3 = 63 

model-day runs. Because token usage scales approximately linearly with simulated days, the week is treated as the primary analysis unit. Monthly values are therefore statistical projections rather than direct 28-day simulations. The 28-day cumulative cost and regret relative to the oracle are estimated as 28 times the mean daily value, with a 95% confidence interval obtained by nonparametric bootstrap over the 21 day-level observations per model. This projection assumes that the evaluation week is rep-resentative of the month. Day-of-week effects are covered by construction, while seasonal and price-regime representa-tiveness are discussed as limitations in Sec. VII. A full 30-day champion-model run is a direct extension of the harness and is limited only by inference budget. Metrics include cumulative regret relative to the oracle, share of oracle savings captured relative to the off-peak timer, day-level failure count, worst-day gap, and projected four-week costs with confidence intervals.

daily EV charging with weekday departure deadlines. It must allocate cycles to days as well as to half-hourly slots across a 7-day horizon, giving T = 336 slots. This is treated as a capability test rather than a deployment scenario. Both the agent and the weekly MILP receive the full archived week of prices, so the information available to both methods is equal. The weekly MILP extends the formulation in Appendix A by adding cycle-to-day allocation variables. A. Experiment 1: Multi-Day Model Benchmark 

Function calling achieves MILP parity. With native func-tion calling, all three models commit every requested appliance across all 36 runs, giving 100% success. GPT-4o-mini and Gemini 2.5 Flash match the MILP optimum in every run, with 100% optimality and γ = 0 .00% . Claude Sonnet 4.6 also achieves 100% success, but reaches 86% optimality with a mean gap of 0.12% . Its small residual gap is associated with longer reasoning traces and occasional over-selection of non-optimal windows in the price-only setting. These results confirm H1: commercial function-calling LLMs can deliver optimal or near-optimal multi-appliance scheduling directly from natural language, without manual parameter configura-tion. 

Text-parsed actions reduce reliability. Removing native tool calling degrades every model. The gap between the function-calling and text-parsed rows for the same model provides direct evidence that action-interface design materially affects orchestration reliability.

D. Experiment 4: Longitudinal Deployment and Weekly Plan-ning 

Over a week of rolling daily operation on real prices and weather (Table VII), all three agents capture 96.7% to 98.0% of the savings available to the extended MILP oracle. They are therefore, in practical cost terms, close to running the solver itself. GPT-4o-mini’s weekly cost is £24.63, within £0.01 of the price-only MILP. All three agents also strictly dominate the rule-based policies that households are more likely to deploy in practice. The off-peak timer leaves approximately £26/week on the table relative to the oracle. The immediate-start policy is 76.8% worse than the timer, while the greedy cheapest-slot heuristic is also worse than the timer because it ignores multi-slot appliance durations. Projected to four weeks, the agents cost approximately £98 to £100, compared with £205 for the timer. This corresponds to a saving of about £106/month, or roughly £1,270/year, for the representative household. The projection intervals for the three agents overlap almost completely. This suggests that model selection for deployment should be driven less by week-level cost and more by the efficiency results from Experiment 1 and the safety results from Experiment 2. The four-week projection intervals are intentionally wide because they are obtained by bootstrap resampling from a single archived week. The point estimates are stable within that week, but the projection remains appro-priately cautious. Extending the evaluation to the full archived horizon is a straightforward future extension. 

Weekly joint planning. In the harder weekly-planning task, the champion model must place two washing-machine cycles, three dishwasher cycles, and five weekday EV charg-ing sessions from a single week-ahead prompt. The agent completed all ten planned repetitions after the earlier rate-limited runs were resumed. The resulting schedules commit to the full multi-cycle plan rather than solving each appliance in isolation. The weekly MILP confirms that the allocations are feasible and near-optimal. The remaining shortfalls are concentrated in cycle-to-day allocation rather than within-day slot choice, indicating that the agent has learned the local timing structure of low-cost operation but still loses some value when assigning flexible loads across the weekly horizon. VII. D ISCUSSION 

A. Model Selection and the Economics of Reliability 

The benchmark reframes model selection for deployed agentic HEMS as a trade-off between reliability, cost, and latency, measured per scheduling event rather than per token. Since a household is likely to schedule only a small number of flexible loads each day, even the most expensive backend has a low absolute cost per event. The more important deployment question is therefore not which model is cheapest, but which model remains reliable when the scheduling problem becomes adversarial or constraint-heavy. Experiment 2 is the discriminating test. In benign multi-appliance scheduling, all three function-calling models are near-optimal. Under constraint conflict, however, they sepa-rate sharply. Claude Sonnet 4.6 is the strongest constraint-compliance model: it handles power caps, infeasibility re-porting, and instruction-calendar conflicts correctly without explicit guidance. GPT-4o-mini is the efficiency leader in Experiment 1, but fails power-cap serialization and instruction-conflict resolution even with guidance. The efficiency-optimal model and the safety-optimal model are therefore not the same. A deployment should choose the backend according to the failure mode that matters most for the intended use case. This also has privacy implications. Open-source backends could permit local inference over household data, but they were not re-run in this study. Quantifying the reliability cost of local inference on this benchmark remains future work, with the literature-reported open-source results of [17] serving as the current reference point. 11 0

B. Constraint Conflicts as a Safety Property 

A HEMS that silently trades a departure deadline for a cheaper charging window fails the user in a way that a cost metric alone cannot capture. Constraint compliance under conflict should therefore be treated as a first-class evaluation axis for agentic energy systems, alongside optimality and efficiency. The observed failures motivate a simple deployment guardrail: every LLM-committed schedule should pass through a deterministic feasibility validator before device actuation. This validator should check deadlines, horizon limits, house-hold power caps, and infeasibility conditions independently of the model. The need for this safeguard is clear from Experiment 2. GPT-4o-mini violates the 9 kW power cap in all S2 runs under both prompts, and follows the casual overnight-charging instruction over the binding calendar deadline in all S5 runs. Guidance improves some behaviour, including infeasibility reporting in S4, but does not repair these safety-critical failures. Prompt engineering can reduce some failure modes, but it is not a reliable substitute for deterministic post-hoc validation. 

C. Value and Limits of Weather Integration 

Weather-aware co-optimization is valuable when forecast quality is adequate and PV surplus overlaps with schedulable flexibility. The forecast-noise analysis makes this operational: the weather-aware objective should be enabled when forecasts are reliable enough to improve load placement, and used conservatively when forecast uncertainty is high. The approach is transferable to any market with dynamic import prices and a defined export rate. The GB Agile and SEG setting is a demanding case because retail prices are volatile and can become negative, which stresses both the agent and the MILP ground-truth formulation. The regime-dependent result is equally important. Weather integration helps on overcast days, is largely redundant on mixed days, and can be harmful on sunny days when negative import prices make grid import more attractive than PV self-consumption. Weather-aware HEMS should therefore not blindly maximize self-consumption. Instead, the system should decide when PV-aware scheduling is economically useful and when the price signal alone is sufficient. Natural extensions include battery storage and thermal loads. Storage would deepen the coupling in (1), while thermal loads would require building-physics constraints rather than only user-defined appliance windows. These extensions are important because heating, cooling, and storage are likely to provide larger flexibility volumes than wet appliances alone. 

D. Flat Agents versus Hierarchical Orchestration 

This study uses a single tool-calling ReAct agent, whereas [17] uses an orchestrator with per-appliance specialists. The flat design achieves 100% multi-appliance success under na-tive function calling for all three commercial models. This suggests that hierarchical per-appliance decomposition is not necessarily an architectural requirement for small residential scheduling tasks. Instead, it may have acted as useful scaf-folding for weaker models and text-parsed action interfaces. The function-calling versus text-parsed comparison supports this view. The same models that coordinate reliably with native function calling degrade substantially when forced to use free-form text actions. This indicates that interface design is a major determinant of orchestration reliability. However, hierarchy may become valuable again as the number of appliances, constraints, and planning horizons increase. Testing appliance-count scaling is therefore an important direction for future work. 

E. Threats to Validity and Limitations 

Internal validity. Commercial models can change server-side. We pin model identifiers and record run dates, but exact 13 

reproduction still depends on provider stability and access. Results are also conditional on the prompt family used in this study. The baseline and guided prompts in Experiment 2 test prompt sensitivity for constraint conflicts, but do not exhaust the wider prompt-design space. The base load Bt and PV parameters are fixed for one household configuration. 

External validity. The evaluation uses one household con-figuration, one market setting, and simulated rather than real users. The four-week costs in Experiment 4a are projections from a single archived week and assume that this week is representative of the month. Day-of-week variation is included by construction, but seasonal effects and wider price-regime variation remain limited. Results may differ under other tariffs, export rates, weather regimes, and household routines. The calendar scenarios in Experiment 2 are deliberately stressful, but still do not capture the full diversity of real household behaviour. 

Construct validity. The MILP ground truth assumes that the objective in (1) captures the relevant notion of user value. In practice, households may care about comfort, habit, noise, convenience, and trust, which are not fully represented by net electricity cost. As problem richness increases, optimization-based benchmarking may also become harder to scale. This broader evaluation challenge is shared with the wider agentic HEMS literature [17]. VIII. C ONCLUSION 

This paper extends agentic LLM-based home energy man-agement along four dimensions that remain underdeveloped in prior work: model coverage, optimization signal, evalu-ation breadth, and constraint robustness. The proposed sys-tem grounds a single tool-calling ReAct agent in live GB retail prices from Octopus Agile, live weather forecasts, an explicit PV generation model, household usage history, and a RAG knowledge base. The agent schedules flexible appliances against a net-cost objective with solar self-consumption, using an extended MILP as ground truth. The results show three main findings. First, native function calling enables all three commercial models to close the multi-appliance coordination gap, achieving MILP-optimal or near-optimal scheduling at negligible per-event cost. In contrast, the text-parsed action interface substantially reduces reliability and exposes conversational short-circuiting, where a model produces a fluent schedule confirmation without committing the required action. Second, constraint compliance under con-flict is more discriminating than benign-case optimality. The efficiency-optimal model and the safety-optimal model are not the same, which motivates a model-independent deterministic feasibility validator before any schedule is actuated. Third, weather-aware co-optimization provides a regime-dependent benefit: it improves net cost and self-consumption on overcast days, adds little on mixed days, and can be counterproductive on sunny days with negative import prices. Over a week-long deployment simulation, the agents capture 96.7–98.0% of the MILP oracle’s savings, projecting approximately £1,270 per year relative to off-peak timer scheduling. Future work will extend the framework to thermal loads and battery storage, test appliance-count scaling for flat and hierarchical agent designs, incorporate adaptive learning from user feedback, support multimodal onboarding from appliance labels and energy bills, and evaluate the system through field pilots with real households.
