## Abstract 

Building sensors are embedded in physical topology, spatial hier-archy, and operational context, yet existing forecasters often treat them as isolated time series or rely on fixed covariate sets. We present TopoBrick , a training-free framework for zero-shot build-ing IoT (Internet-of-Things) forecasting. TopoBrick uses building knowledge graphs to construct a compact structural skeleton and employs an agentic topology sampler to select target-specific exoge-nous variables. The selected variables are organized by deployment-time availability, separating past-known sensor states from future-known calendar, schedule, and meteorological exogenous variables. Across three real-world buildings, TopoBrick outperforms strong zero-shot foundation-model baselines and remains competitive with fully trained building-specific models. Ablations show that topology-aware sampling is more reliable than random, ontology-only, or fixed-hop selection, especially for physically coupled HVAC and weather-driven sensing variables. The code of this work is avail-able in https://github.com/Dawnlxc/TopoBrick.git

## 1 Introduction 

Modern buildings are dense cyber-physical systems instrumented with hundreds to thousands of sensing, control, and operational points. These points measure indoor temperature, humidity, pres-sure, airflow, electrical demand, equipment modes, setpoints, valve and damper commands, and outdoor conditions. Accurate forecast-ing of these building sensor time series is fundamental to demand flexibility, fault detection, predictive control, and grid-interactive operation. Unlike generic multivariate time-series benchmarks, building sensor data is not a flat collection of independent channels. Each sensor is embedded in a physical and spatial structure: it is attached to equipment, located in a room or zone, served by air or water loops, and often coupled with setpoints, actuator com-mands, and measured responses through control loops. Therefore, the relevance of an auxiliary signal depends not only on its nu-merical history, but also on where it is located in the building and how it is physically or operationally connected to the target sensor. This structure is largely ignored by existing forecasting methods. Channel-independent models forecast each sensor separately, while multivariate models learn dependencies from a fixed variable set. Both settings assume that the input variables are already known and aligned. Time-series foundation models further enable zero-shot forecasting without task-specific training, but they still treat exogenous variables as a pre-defined flat set. They do not provide arXiv:2607.06349v1 [cs.AI] 7 Jul 2026 Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Lin et al. 

a principled mechanism for deciding which of a building’s many available points should be used as exogenous variables for a given target. Conversely, training a building-specific model may exploit local patterns, but it requires historical data, tuning, and integration effort for every newly deployed building, limiting scalability across heterogeneous building portfolios. A natural source of structural prior is the building knowledge graph. Semantic building schemas such as Brick and Project Haystack represent a building as a graph of equipment, locations, systems, and Points, with relations describ-ing containment, point attachment, and physical flow. In principle, this graph provides the missing physical and spatial context for forecasting. In practice, however, directly using a building KG is non-trivial. Building graphs are heterogeneous and hierarchical: nodes such as equipment or location do not carry time-series readings, while only Point nodes stream data. Simple graph-distance heuris-tics such as fixed 𝑘 -hop neighborhoods can be misleading since a physically relevant point may be several structural hops away, while a high-degree equipment or building-level node can expand to many unrelated points. Learned graph encoders introduce addi-tional training and schema-alignment requirements, which conflict with the zero-shot deployment setting. The challenge is therefore not only to forecast sensor values, but also to identify target-specific exogenous variables from a heterogeneous building KG without building-specific training. This paper formulates zero-shot building sensor forecasting as a topology-aware exogenous-variable selec-tion problem. The key idea is to use the building KG as a routing layer: rather than conditioning on all available points or retriev-ing sensors only by ontology class or graph distance, the model should select exogenous variables that are physically grounded, operationally meaningful, and available under realistic deployment constraints. We present TopoBrick, a training-free framework for zero-shot building sensor forecasting. TopoBrick first distills the raw building KG into a compact building skeleton that preserves equipment-flow, spatial-containment, and structural relations while separating Point leaves from topology traversal. Given a target Point, an agentic topology sampler reasons over the target-centric skeleton context and generates structured sampling actions for selecting relevant exogenous variables. A KG-grounded verifier audits these actions before deterministic materialization into time-series inputs. The selected variables are then organized by deployment-time avail-ability: past-known sensor and equipment states are provided only over the historical window, while future-known calendar, schedule, and meteorological variables are provided over both the history and prediction horizon. This allows a frozen time-series founda-tion model to use building-specific context without updating model parameters or leaking future observations. The contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

• Formulate zero-shot building sensor forecasting as a topology-aware exogenous-variable selection problem over heteroge-neous building knowledge graphs. 

• Propose TopoBrick, a zero-shot pipeline that constructs com-pact building skeletons and uses an agentic topology sampler with KG-grounded verification to select target-specific ex-ogenous variables. 

• Design a deployment-faithful forecasting setup that sepa-rates past-known and future-known exogenous variables, and evaluate it on 3 real-world buildings against zero-shot time series foundation models and fully trained building-specific forecasters. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 introduces the proposed methodol-ogy. Section 4 presents the experimental results. Sections 5 and 6 provide the discussion and conclusion, respectively. 

## 2 Related Works 2.1 Time Series Forecasting 

Time series forecasting has progressed rapidly with the advent of deep learning. Early Transformer-based approaches such as In-former [ 22 ] introduced sparse attention mechanisms to handle long sequences efficiently. Subsequent work questioned the necessity of attention: DLinear [ 21 ] showed that simple linear models can rival or surpass complex Transformers on standard benchmarks, prompt-ing renewed attention to model design. PatchTST [ 12 ] demonstrated that patching the input series and treating channels independently substantially improves Transformer performance, while iTrans-former [ 8] inverts the attention mechanism to operate across vari-ables rather than time steps. TimeMixer [ 16 ] leverages multi-scale mixing to capture patterns at different temporal resolutions, and TimeXer [ 17 ] explicitly incorporates exogenous variables into the forecasting process. More recently, large language models (LLMs) have been adapted for time series. GPT4TS [ 23 ] repurposes a frozen pretrained lan-guage model as a general time series backbone, TimeLLM [ 5] re-programs the input series into the language modality to align with LLM representations, and GPT4MTS [ 4] extends this paradigm to multimodal time series. While effective, these methods generally require task dataset specific training. A complementary line of work develops time series foundation models that support cross-domain zero-shot forecasting without per-task training. Chronos [ 1 , 2] tokenizes time series and trains on large-scale corpora to enable zero-shot transfer. TimesFM [ 3]adopts a decoder-only architecture pretrained on diverse datasets, and Moirai [ 7, 18 ] provides a unified model handling arbitrary frequencies and variable counts. Despite this progress, these meth-ods are largely developed and evaluated on curated benchmarks where the variable set is fixed and pre-aligned, and they leave two assumptions unaddressed in the building IoT setting. First, channel-independent and implicitly multivariate models offer no principled way to decide which of a building’s hundreds to thousands of points should serve as exogenous variables for a given target, either ig-noring cross-sensor structure or conditioning indiscriminately on all channels, injecting noise and cost. Second, the trained mod-els incur per-building or per-dataset training cost that does not amortize across the long tail of heterogeneous buildings encoun-tered in deployment. Foundation models remove the training cost but still treat exogenous variables as a given flat set, with no no-tion of the spatial, physical, or control relationships that make an exogenous variable relevant. Our work builds on the zero-shot foun-dation model paradigm but supplies exactly this missing ingredient: a training-free, building-agnostic mechanism that uses building TopoBrick: Agentic Topology Sampling of Exogenous Variables for Zero-Shot Building IoT Forecasting Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY 

topology to select physically relevant exogenous variables and to separate past-known from future-known exogenous inputs in real operational settings. 

## 2.2 Building Time Series Analytics 

A growing body of work studies building operational data through the lens of semantic metadata, in particular the Brick ontology, which represents a building as a knowledge graph of equipment, locations, points, and their physical and spatial relations. Much of this research targets understanding the metadata itself: automatic classification of points and entities into Brick classes [ 14 ] addresses the noisy, inconsistent labeling found in real deployments, while systems work demonstrates end-to-end pipelines built on Brick-structured data [ 6 ]. Building knowledge graphs have also been used as a backbone for higher-level reasoning, including natural-language question answering over building KGs [ 10 , 11 ], where the graph supplies the semantic context needed to resolve queries about a building’s structure and operation. On the analytics side, building time series have been studied for tasks such as electricity load forecasting, including privacy-preserving formulations [ 20 ], and for understanding how operational patterns shift under excep-tional conditions such as pandemic lock-downs [ 13 ]. These studies establish both the practical importance of building forecasting and the value of semantic metadata, but they typically treat the knowl-edge graph as a source of labels or context for a single building, rather than as a structure to be exploited for transferable, cross-building forecasting. Critically, none of these lines combine the two capabilities our setting demands. Forecasting methods that use building metadata still rely on building-specific training or handcrafted covariate rules, while KG-centric methods focus on retrieval or question answering rather than driving a forecaster. The question of how to reason over a heterogeneous, hierarchical building KG with a large amount of non-time-series nodes and varying ontology completeness to select exogenous variables and forecast in a training-free, building-agnostic manner remains open. TopoBrick is designed to fill this gap, connecting the zero-shot foundation-forecasting paradigm to the rich but heterogeneous structure encoded in building knowledge graphs. 

## 3 Methodology 

Overview. TopoBrick performs zero-shot building sensor fore-casting by using the building KG as a routing layer for exogenous variable selection. Figure 1 illustrates the overall pipeline for Topo-Brick. Given a target Point node, the framework first constructs a compact building skeleton from the raw KG, then uses an agentic topology sampler to select physically and operationally relevant exogenous variables. The selected variables are verified against KG evidence, materialized into time-series inputs, and organized according to their deployment-time availability before being passed to a frozen time-series foundation model.

## 3.1 Agentic Topology Sampler 

The goal of the agentic topology sampler is to identify a compact set of exogenous variables that are relevant to a given target Point node. Building systems contain hundreds or thousands of heteroge-neous points, many of which are unrelated to a particular target. Directly conditioning on all available Points is therefore inefficient and can inject noise. TopoBrick instead uses the building KG to route exogenous variable selection through physical, spatial, and operational structure. The sampler operates on metadata and graph topology. It does not use future observations or forecasting errors during selection. Given a target Point 𝑝 , it returns an exogenous variable set E ( 𝑝 ), which is later aligned to the forecasting window. 

3.1.1 Building Skeleton Construction. Raw building KGs are het-erogeneous and dense. Point nodes are usually attached as leaves to equipment, rooms, zones, or systems, while the meaningful physical structure is carried by relations such as equipment flow, part-whole hierarchy, and spatial containment. Direct traversal on the full KG can be unstable: high-degree structural nodes may expand to many unrelated Points, while physically relevant Points may be several hops away through intermediate equipment or location nodes. To obtain a tractable structural representation, we construct a build-ing skeleton 𝐺 𝑠 = (𝑉 𝑠 , 𝐸 𝑠 ) from the raw KG. The skeleton keeps non-Point structural nodes and structural relations, while removing Point leaves from the traversal graph:

3.1.2 Target-centric Topology Context. For each target Point 𝑝 , we construct a target-centric topology context C( 𝑝 ) from the building skeleton. This context summarizes where the target is located in the building and which structural regions may contain useful ex-ogenous variables. It contains three components. Target anchor. The target anchor is the equipment, room, zone, or system node to which the target Point is attached. Points co-located on the same anchor often share a physical subsystem or control loop. Local topology. The local topology describes the structural neighborhood around the target anchor, including upstream equipment, downstream served entities, sibling equipment, and spatial containers. This provides physical context without requiring the agent to traverse the full KG. Global context. The global context summarizes building-level drivers, such as weather-related Points, building-level meters, and operational schedules. 3.1.3 Agentic Exogenous-variable Selection. Given C( 𝑝 ), the agent selects exogenous variables by reasoning over both target semantics and topology. The reasoning follows three principles. Physical rele-vance. The agent first identifies the physical quantity measured by the target Point and infers plausible driver categories. For example, temperature targets may depend on supply or return air tempera-ture, outdoor conditions, setpoints, and equipment states; pressure and airflow targets may depend on fans, dampers, valves, and loop states; electrical targets may depend on power, current, voltage, demand, and building-level load conditions. Topological grounding. 

The agent then grounds these driver categories in the skeleton. Rather than retrieving Points solely by ontology class or fixed graph distance, it selects variables from structurally meaningful re-gions, such as the target anchor, upstream equipment, served zones, sibling components, or building-level hosts. Controlled expansion. 

Finally, the agent determines how broadly each structural region should be expanded. Local variables are retrieved from the target anchor or nearby equipment, whereas global variables are retrieved only when the corresponding physical quantity is building-wide by nature, such as weather or aggregate demand. ## 3.2 Zero-shot Forecasting with Topology-aware Exogenous Variables 

After exogenous variables are selected, TopoBrick formats them as inputs to a frozen zero-shot forecaster. The key requirement is deployment-time availability: a variable can only be used over the forecast horizon if its future values will be available at inference time. 

3.2.1 Exogenous-variable Availability. The exogenous variables are divided into past-known exogenous and future-known exogenous 

based on their availability during prediction horizon. 

Past-known exogenous variables are observed only up to the pre-diction time. These include topology-selected building Points such as sensor measurements, equipment states, power demand, flow rates, pressures, valve positions, damper positions, operating modes, and status variables. They provide historical context but are masked over the prediction horizon. 

Future-known exogenous variables are available over both the historical window and the forecast horizon, including 

• Calendar features: deterministic functions of timestamps. 

• Operational schedules: HVAC schedules or setpoint pro-grams, which are known in advance from the building man-agement system. 

• Meteorological Forecasts: observed historically and fore-cast over the horizon by a separate weather forecaster. 

3.2.2 Meteorological Forecaster. Ground-truth weather observa-tions are only available up to the prediction time, but weather is often needed as a future-known driver for building forecasting. ## 4 Experiments 

Datasets. We evaluate on real-world comprehensive building oper-ational data spanning three buildings: one campus building LBNL59 

from UC Berkeley, USA [ 9 ], and two institutional buildings from Australia, BTS-B and BTS-C [15 ]. ## 4.3 Comparison on Sampling Strategy 

We compare the proposed agentic sampler against several alterna-tive sampling strategies to validate the information gains from the topology-aware sampler. 

Random Sampler. This strategy randomly selects building points as exogenous variables without considering either semantic align-ment or topological dependency. 

Same Ontology Sampler. This strategy selects points that share the same Brick ontology class as the target sensor. Although ontology-level matching can quickly retrieve semantically aligned signals, it is blind to topology. In large commercial buildings, sensors with the same ontology class may be distributed across unrelated equipment or locations, which has the possibility of introducing redundant or weakly informative variables. 

𝑘 -hop Sampler. Selecting points within a fixed 𝑘 -hop neighbor-hood of the target node as exogenous variables. It utilizes graph connectivity to capture local structural context. However, all neigh-boring nodes are treated uniformly, where irrelevant signals could be involved. To isolate the effect of the sampling strategy, we evaluate all sam-plers under the same forecaster and compare their error changes against a baseline without considering KG. We report the percent-age changes in normalized MAE and MSE, where negative values indicate improvement. Meteorological observations are used as oracle forecasts to remove uncertainty from external forecasting errors and ensure that the comparison focuses on the quality of exogenous variable selection. Figure 2 visualize the experiment results, which show the consis-tently achievement for the strongest improvements by the proposed agentic sampler. On LBNL59, TopoBrick reduces normalized MAE by 11.3%, 10.5%, 7.7%, and 6.6% from 24 to 96 steps, and reduces normalized MSE by 30.9%, 28.8%, 23.3%, and 19.1%. The gains are larger than those of both 2-hop and 3-hop sampling, indicating that merely using local graph neighborhoods is insufficient; selecting structurally meaningful variables is more important than expanding the neighborhood size. The advantage of TopoBrick agentic sampler is most pronounced on BTS-B. It reduces normalized MAE by 22.2%–29.3% and normal-ized MSE by 22.2%–31.2% across all horizons. In contrast, random sampling consistently increases error, confirming that injecting arbitrary building signals can introduce noise rather than useful context. The same-ontology sampler performs competitively on BTS-B, particularly for normalized MAE, but its improvement re-mains weaker than TopoBrick. ## 5 Discussion 

Building topology is useful because it provides a principled way to route exogenous variable selection. In heterogeneous buildings, the available points are numerous, unevenly distributed, and partially relevant to the given target. Adding arbitrary points can degrade performance, while fixed-radius graph neighborhoods are not con-sistently reliable. The main bottleneck is lack of a target-specific mechanism to decide the relevance instead of auxiliary variables. TopoBrick uses the building KG to impose this routing prior: the graph does not directly predict the future, but constrains what the forecaster should condition on. One notable thing is that topology-aware sampling is not a uni-versal improvement mechanism. It is most effective when the se-lected variables correspond to real physical or operational depen-dencies, such as HVAC temperature dynamics, water-loop vari-ables, pressure-related signals, and weather-driven thermodynamic measurements. In contrast, targets such as electrical demand, air-flow, and some pressure signals may be dominated by unobserved occupancy, stochastic equipment usage, supervisory control, or closed-loop actuation policies. In these cases, the KG may still iden-tify structurally plausible variables, but the true drivers may lack full representation in the available metadata or future-known in-puts. This boundary is important: TopoBrick should be interpreted as dependency-aware conditioning, not as a guarantee that every sensor class will benefit from topology. Another finding from this work is that relevance alone is insuffi-cient, where deployment-time availability also matters. Past-known sensor and equipment states can enrich the historical context, but their future values are unavailable during prediction. This makes them harder for a zero-shot forecaster to exploit over long horizons. Future-known variables, especially operational schedules and me-teorological forecasts, provide more consistent gains because they remain available throughout the prediction window. The weather-quality analysis further shows that better meteorological forecasts lead to stronger improvements, while imperfect forecasts can still be beneficial. This supports the need for an availability-aware for-mulation rather than treating all exogenous variables as equally accessible. In addition, whether the same target-centric topology sampler can support other building analytics tasks remains open. Tasks such as virtual sensing, sensor outage recovery, anomaly detection, fault localization, imputation, and control-oriented reasoning also require identifying physically related building points. Extending topology-routed exogenous variable selection beyond forecasting is therefore an important direction for future work. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This work proposes TopoBrick, a training-free framework for zero-shot building sensor forecasting. TopoBrick designs an agentic reasoning system for sampling the physical coupling exogenous variables for the given target, and organizes the selected variables according to their deployment-time availability. This enables a frozen time-series foundation model to condition on physically and operationally meaningful context without building-specific training. The experimental results on 3real-world buildings show that TopoBrick outperforms strong zero-shot baselines and remains competitive with fully trained building-specific forecasters. Abla-tion studies further demonstrate that topology-aware sampling is more reliable than random, ontology-only, or fixed-hop selection, and that future-known meteorological and operational variables are critical for deployment-faithful forecasting. The results indi-cate that building knowledge graphs can serve as effective routing layers for scalable forecasting in heterogeneous cyber-physical environments.
