Abstract 5

Accurate prediction of three-dimensional (3D) wind fields over complex mountainous terrain is essential for renewable energy deployment and regional weather modeling. Traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations face two fundamental bottlenecks: expert-intensive mesh generation around irregular topography, and iterative solvers that require hours to days even on high-performance clusters. Recent neural operator approaches accel-erate inference, but typically fail to resolve the sharp, localized velocity gradients induced by complex terrain features. Here, we present a transformer-based dual-attention neural-operator framework for 3D wind field prediction over complex mountainous terrain, and validate its effectiveness through two instantiations on representative point-based (mesh-free) and graph-based neural-operator architectures, namely Patch-solver and Patch-GTO. Trained on a large CFD-generated dataset spanning diverse terrain geometries and inflow conditions, the framework enables rapid prediction of steady-state wind field while main-taining competitive accuracy. It also demonstrates robust zero-shot transfer to real-world mountainous sites across several diverse locations, outperforming existing neural operator baselines by 10% in relative error. We further verify that incorporating sparse observational data (1% spatial coverage) reduces prediction error by 16.89% relative to the corresponding model without sparse data input and by 32.75% relative to advanced neural operator base-lines on unseen terrains. This framework establishes a generalizable computational paradigm across domains, promising to be a real-time tool for wind resource assessment over complex mountainous terrain and related atmospheresurface interaction studies.

tions offer faster computation but fail to capture essential flow structures, whereas fine-grid 35 

simulations improve accuracy at a prohibitive computational cost [13]. Consequently, con-36 

ventional methods are inadequate for delivering rapid and reliable 3D wind predictions over 37 

complex terrains. Recent hybrid approaches have attempted to alleviate these limitations by 38 

integrating sparse on-site measurements with CFD outputs to accelerate and correct wind 39 

resource assessments [14]. Nevertheless, a unified and computationally efficient predictive 40 

framework remains urgently needed. 41 

In recent years, deep learning (DL) has become a promising paradigm for the rapid 42 

prediction of wind resources, and it has achieved strong performance in global weather fore-43 

casting at spatial resolutions of about 0.25 ° (e.g., NVIDIA Earth-2 [15], Pangu-Weather [16], 44 

FuXi [17]). However, such coarse resolutions are insufficient for microscale, three-dimensional 45 

windfield prediction over complex terrain. Operator learning offers a complementary route. 46 

Unlike conventional numerical solvers that iteratively integrate the NavierStokes equations on 47 

a fixed mesh, neural operators approximate the underlying solution operator of the governing 48 

partial differential equations (PDEs) by learning a nonlinear map from inputs (e.g., terrain 49 

geometry, boundary conditions) to outputs (e.g., wind velocity field), enabling resolution-50 

2robust inference and rapid generalization across diverse inputs. Early milestones include the 51 

Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) [18], which parameterizes integral kernels in spectral space 52 

to achieve resolution-invariant inference and orders-of-magnitude speed-ups over classical 53 

CFD, and DeepONet [19], which realizes the operator-approximation theorem in a practical 54 

branchtrunk architecture for mapping input functions to output fields. Once trained, these 55 

models achieve inference speeds several orders of magnitude faster than conventional solvers 56 

while preserving high accuracy, and have demonstrated remarkable success in turbulence 57 

modeling, climate simulation, and geophysical field prediction [20, 21, 22, 23]. 58 

As applications move from idealized domains to real-world geometries, a central challenge 59 

is handling irregular, unstructured discretizations without sacrificing fidelity. Geometry- 60 

aware designs such as the Geometry-Informed Neural Operator (GINO) [24] encode shapes 61 

via signed-distance fields and shuttle information between graph representations and a reg- 62 

ular latent grid, enabling spectral updates while remaining discretization-robust on com- 63 

plex 3-D PDEs. In parallel, Transformer-based operators address long-range coupling and 64 

multi-input fusion on irregular meshes; for example, the General Neural Operator Trans- 65 

former (GNOT) [25] introduces heterogeneous normalized attention and geometric gating to 66 

accommodate multiple fields and topologies, while recent physics-aware tokenization strate- 67 

gies, exemplified by Transolver [26], group points into learnable slices and attend among slice 68 

tokens to retain domain-wide coherence at near-linear cost. AeroGTO [27] combines local 69 

message passing from graph neural networks with global self-attention from Transformers, 70 

establishing an efficient GraphTransformer Operator for large-scale aerodynamic predictions. 71 

Recent studies have increasingly incorporated locality, multiscale structure, and geometry 72 

awareness to alleviate the over-smoothing induced by purely global operations. MNO [28] 73 

introduces an explicit three-scale design that combines global dimension-shrinkage attention, 74 

graph attention and microscale pointwise attention. GAOT [29] emphasizes multiscale and 75 

geometry-aware representation learning through graph-based encoders and decoders, while 76 

using a vision transformer as the latent space processor. Miguel et al. [30] introduce lo- 77 

calized integral and differential kernels into FNO (global convolutions in the Fourier space) 78 

to capture local receptive fields while retaining discretization-agnostic operator learning and 79 

resolution generalization. Based on this philosophy, PCNO [31] combines integral and differ- 80 

ential operators on point clouds to capture local effects over complex and variable geometries. 81 

Collectively, these developments mark a shift from mesh-bound, instance-wise solvers toward 82 

fast, geometry-aware operator surrogates that transfer across resolutions and unstructured 83 

grids, opening a practical path to high-fidelity predictions in fluids, climate and geoscience. 84 

Despite these architectural advances in operator learning, existing benchmarks predom- 85 

inantly focus on relatively fixed geometric configurations, such as a limited set of baseline 86 

vehicle models in automotive aerodynamics (e.g., DrivAerNet [32] and DrivAerML [33]), 87 

simple two-dimensional airfoils (AirfRANS [34]) or cylindrical pipes [18], or canonical flow 88 

problems with controlled geometric variations. These standardized scenarios enable system- 89 

atic evaluation but do not adequately capture the challenges posed by spatially complex and 90 

highly heterogeneous terrain distributions encountered in mountainous wind field prediction. 91 

3Moreover, many existing approaches [3, 4, 24, 35, 36] simplify the solution domain to regu-92 

lar Cartesian grids, which introduces several limitations. Such uniform discretization tends 93 

to under-resolve extreme values in regions with sharp local gradients, particularly critical 94 

near ridges, valleys, and flow separation zones, and fails to represent the irregular geometric 95 

boundaries inherent in complex mountainous terrain. 96 

To this end, we present an end-to-end deep neural operator framework that enables rapid, 97 

high-fidelity prediction of 3D wind fields over complex mountainous terrain that would 98 

otherwise require computationally expensive CFD simulations. The framework integrates 99 

a dual-attention mechanism that simultaneously captures meter-scale terrain-induced flow 100 

features and global atmospheric patterns across the full domain, addressing the multiscale 101 

coupling inherent in mountainous wind prediction. Unlike prior approaches that define local-102 

ity through graph neighborhoods [28, 29] or inject local inductive bias through differential-103 

or kernel-based operators [30, 31], our method explicitly partitions the spatial domain into 104 

physically bounded voxels and applies learned self-attention within each voxel to capture the 105 

local flow structures induced by terrain blocking. Coupled with a separate slice-based global 106 

attention pathway, the framework further captures domain-wide flow organization, making 107 

it particularly well suited to mountainous wind prediction. 108 

We validate this dual-attention principle through two instantiations on representative 109 

point-based and graph-based neural-operator architectures, resulting in Patch-solver and 110 

Patch-GTO. In particular, for Patch-solver, as shown in Fig. 1, by representing terrain 111 

as point clouds rather than structured grids and formulating wind prediction as an opera-112 

tor learning problem, our approach directly maps arbitrary topographic geometries to full 113 

volumetric flow fields, eliminating the need for mesh generation and iterative PDE solvers. 114 

To validate our framework, we construct a high-fidelity CFD-based dataset spanning over 115 

500 complex terrain regions with diverse morphologies and inlet conditions, establishing a 116 

physically consistent benchmark for operator learning in atmospheric flow prediction. Ex-117 

tensive comparisons against advanced neural operators reveal that our method achieves at 118 

least 10% lower prediction errors on held-out terrains while maintaining real-time infer-119 

ence suitable for operational wind forecasting and renewable energy siting. In addition, the 120 

framework demonstrates three critical capabilities for operational deployment: (1) data as-121 

similation of sparse in-situ measurements (less than 1% spatial coverage), leading to further 122 

improvements in prediction accuracy; (2) mast-to-mast wind speed inference from annual-123 

mean measurements at a reference mast; and (3) physically interpretable learned repre-124 

sentations, as evidenced by entropy analysis revealing height-stratified attention patterns 125 

that mirror atmospheric boundary-layer evolutionconcentrated near complex terrain where 126 

flow separation dominates, and diffuse aloft where free-stream conditions prevail. Together, 127 

these results highlight the potential of the proposed neural operator method to accelerate 128 

wind-field prediction workflows while retaining strong physical fidelity. 129

2.1 Problem Setup 131 

Accurate prediction of wind fields over complex mountainous terrain is critical for various 132 

applications, including renewable energy siting and micro-meteorological modeling. Tradi-133 

tional CFD solvers, such as Fluent and OpenFOAM, can provide highly accurate predic-134 

tions, but they are computationally expensive and impractical for real-time applications, 135 

especially when considering the irregularity and high computational demand of mountain-136 

ous terrains. In addition, a critical bottleneck arises in wind resource evaluation workflows: 137 

converting point cloud data from terrain surveys into CFD-compatible meshes introduces 138 

expert-intensive delays that fundamentally limit the pace of resource assessment in un-139 

mapped mountainous regions. 140 

Generally, neural operators (Non-graph-based methods) offer a transformative alternative 141 

by learning solution mappings directly from terrain geometry to flow fields, eliminating mesh 142 

generation and iterative PDE solvers once trained. Mathematically, given terrain geometry 143 

as point cloud T ∈ RNs×Cin and query locations P ∈ RN ×3 in the 3D atmospheric domain, the 144 

problem is to learn a neural operator Φ that predicts the velocity field: 145 

ˆW ∈ RN ×Cin = Φ([ T, P]) (1) where P ∈ RN ×Cin represents the coordinates of points above the given terrain ( T ∈ RNs×Cin ,146 

where Ns is the number of points of the given terrain); ˆW = [ˆu, ˆv, ˆw] represents the predicted 147 

velocity components at each query point. 148 

However, applying neural operators to mountainous wind prediction poses a fundamental 149 

challenge: terrain-induced flows exhibit multi-scale coupling where global pressure-velocity 150 

interactions spanning kilometers determine overall circulation, while meter-scale terrain fea-151 

tures (ridges, valleys, steep slopes) induce localized flow separation and sharp velocity gra-152 

dients that must be simultaneously resolved. Accordingly, methods dominated by global 153 

aggregation [26] may smooth out local structures, whereas methods with a strong local in-154 

ductive bias [24] may insufficiently capture long-range dependencies. 155 

2.2 Transformer-based neural operators with dual-attention architecture 156 

We resolve this multi-scale challenge through a principled decomposition of the solution 157 

operator into complementary spatial pathways. Our physics-motivated dual-attention ar-158 

chitecture integrates two attention mechanisms that explicitly encode the scale separation 159 

inherent in terrain-flow interactions: 160 

(i) Local sectional attention partitions the atmospheric domain into spatially contiguous 161 

voxels and computes self-attention exclusively within each voxel neighborhood. This voxel-162 

based processing is designed to preserve sharp velocity gradients and capture terrain-confined 163 

flow structuresseparation bubbles, recirculation zones, and boundary-layer detachmentthat 164 

dominate near complex topography. 165 

6(ii) Global slice attention compresses the entire point cloud into a small set of learnable 166 

slice tokens via soft assignment, then applies self-attention among these tokens to capture 167 

domain-wide pressure-velocity coupling and large-scale circulation patterns. This compressed 168 

representation maintains atmospheric coherence while remaining computationally tractable. 169 

(iii) Learnable fusion combines the two pathways through a globally shared, trainable 170 

scalar gate, which balances the overall contributions of local sectional attention and global 171 

slice attention across the entire domain. The gate is optimized during training to find the 172 

best trade-off between terrain-confined local features and domain-wide flow patterns. 173 

Critically, both attention pathways are applied across the entire 3D computational do- 174 

main rather than being restricted to specific regions. However, their effective contributions 175 

are spatially heterogeneous. We validate this dual-attention principle by integrating it into 176 

two representative neural operator frameworks, Transolver [26] and AeroGTO [27], and de- 177 

velop two enhanced variants, Patch-solver and Patch-GTO, to deliver second-level inference 178 

latency and high-accuracy wind field predictions. A detailed description of the developed 179 

neural operators can be found in Section 4. 180 

2.3 Dataset 181 

We construct a large-scale steady-state wind field dataset from high-fidelity CFD simula- 182 

tions over diverse mountainous terrains based on Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) 183 

simulations to train and evaluate the proposed neural operators. The dataset setup is il- 184 

lustrated in Fig. 1b-d, and the simulation details are summarized in Appendix. A. The 185 

computational domain adopts a cylindrical geometry with a circular cross-section (Fig. 1b), 186 

consisting of three nested regions: (i) the inner region containing the actual terrain with 187 

fine mesh resolution, (ii) the transition region with gradually coarsening mesh, and (iii) 188 

the outer region with coarse mesh extending to the far-field boundaries. From this full do- 189 

main, we extract a core cubic region of 6000 × 6000 × 800 m (width × length × altitude) 190 

centered on the terrain, which serves as the training and inference domain for all neural op- 191 

erators. Boundary conditions vary with inflow angle θ to simulate different wind directions 192 

(Fig. 1c). The windward semicircle receives a logarithmic wind profile following atmospheric 193 

boundary layer theory [37]. The leeward semicircle employs a zero-gradient outflow condi- 194 

tion ( ∂U~∂n = 0, where n denotes the outward normal direction to the boundary) to allow 195 

natural flow development. 196 

The dataset comprises 45 distinct terrain geometries sampled from real mountainous 197 

regions using NASA’s Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) digital elevation model 198 

(DEM) at 30 m spatial resolution [38], each simulated under up to 16 uniformly spaced inflow 199 

angles ( θ = 0○, 22 .5○, 45 ○, . . . , 337 .5○), yielding 467 terrain-angle combinations. Each wind field 200 

sample in the dataset is generated using OpenFOAM simulations, requiring approximately 201 

1 hour of wall-clock time on a single core of an AMD EPYC 7543 32-Core Processor. 202 

To standardize the input representation and enhance learning efficiency, we apply a coor- 203 

dinate transformation to align all samples to a canonical 0○ inflow direction. Specifically, for 204 

each sample with an original inflow angle θ, we rotate both the terrain coordinates and the 205 

7velocity field by −θ about the z-axis, ensuring that the dominant wind direction consistently 206 

points along the negative y-axis across all samples. This alignment procedure transforms 207 

the 467 terrain-angle combinations into 467 distinct terrain-flow configuration patterns, each 208 

representing a unique interaction between topographic features and atmospheric flow under 209 

standardized boundary conditions. Each sample contains approximately 360,000 spatial 210 

points with associated three-dimensional velocity fields (u, v, w ). Since subsequent neural 211 

operator networks employ different architectures, two distinct input representations (Fig. 1d) 212 

are constructed in this paper. For graph-based methods, we explicitly construct edge connec-213 

tivity by combining the original mesh edges with additional edges established via k-nearest 214 

neighbor (KNN) search [27], forming an edge set E ∈ Z[Ne,2]. For non-graph-based methods, 215 

we employ Signed Distance Fields (SDF) [26] to implicitly represent geometric relationships 216 

through distance functions and surface normals. All coordinates are normalized to [0, 1],217 

and velocity components are standardized to zero mean and unit variance across the training 218 

set. Detailed specifications of the computational domain design, mesh statistics, and data 219 

pre-processing procedures are provided in Appendix B. 220 

The ratio between training and testing sets is set to 8:2. To validate the generalization 221 

performance of the trained models, an additional zero-shot evaluation dataset is constructed, 222 

comprising four geographically distinct mountainous sites that are spatially separated from 223 

the training regions and exhibit diverse topographic characteristics. Multiple wind inlet 224 

directions are simulated for each unseen terrain to capture various wind scenarios. After ap-225 

plying the same coordinate transformation and preprocessing pipeline, the zero-shot dataset 226 

contains 63 test cases in total, enabling a comprehensive assessment of model robustness and 227 

transferability across different terrain morphologies. 228 

In the following sections, we systematically evaluate the proposed neural operator frame-229 

work across five critical dimensions that collectively establish its practical viability for oper-230 

ational wind resource assessment. 231 

• We benchmark prediction accuracy against previous advanced neural operator baselines 232 

on the test set, analyzing both error distributions and average performance metrics 233 

across velocity components to quantify the improvements. 234 

• We examine how prediction performance varies with terrain complexity and altitude. 235 

• We assess the zero-shot generalization capability at geographically distinct mountain-236 

ous sites and evaluate prediction performance across multiple inflow direction condi-237 

tions to examine directional sensitivity. 238 

• We evaluate the framework’s capacity to assimilate sparse in-situ measurements, sim-239 

ulating realistic wind monitoring scenarios where limited observational data (0.1%-1% 240 

spatial points measured) is available. 241 

• We demonstrate the practical applicability of the framework through a real-world mast-242 

to-mast wind speed inference task, validating its capacity to capture terrain-modulated 243 

transfer relationships between meteorological masts at a complex mountainous site. 244

3 Discussion 722 

This work proposes a transformer-based dual-attention neural operator framework for 723 

real-time prediction of three-dimensional wind fields over complex mountainous terrain, ad- 724 

dressing fundamental limitations of both mesh-based DL methods and computationally ex- 725 

pensive CFD simulations. Through systematic validation across diverse topographic con- 726 

figurations and flow conditions, we demonstrate that this dual-attention design is a general 727 

architectural principle by instantiating it on two representative operator backbones. 728 

Across these two instantiations, the proposed dual-attention neural operators achieve ap- 729 

proximately 8% relative L2 error for wind speed magnitude averaged across the entire 3D 730 

wind field on the test set, while delivering sub-second inference for around 360,000 query 731 

points, representing roughly a 10% improvement over strong neural operator baselines. The 732 

performance gains are most pronounced in the near-surface boundary layer (10150 m), pre- 733 

cisely the rotor-swept zone critical for turbine siting. As elevation increases and terrain 734 

influence diminishes, prediction errors decrease monotonically, with all models converging to 735 

comparable accuracy ( <2% error) at 300 m where free-stream conditions prevail. This ver- 736 

tical stratification demonstrates that the dual-attention architecture intelligently allocates 737 

representational capacity to height regimes where terrain-flow coupling is strongest. 738 

The framework exhibits robust generalization capabilities essential for operational de- 739 

ployment. Zero-shot evaluation on four geographically disjoint mountainous sites spanning 740 

diverse morphologies yields 412% relative L2 errors without any site-specific retraining, with 741 

consistent performance advantages maintained across all 16 inflow directions tested. Fur- 742 

thermore, the framework’s capacity to assimilate sparse in-situ measurements (0.11% spatial 743 

coverage) proves particularly valuable: while baseline models struggle to leverage such sparse 744 

constraints effectively, Patch-solver converts minimal observational data into substantial ac- 745 

curacy gains, achieving 32.75% lower error than the strong baseline model with just 1% 746 

measurement coverage on unseen terrains. In addition, the framework also demonstrates 747 

practical utility in a real-world mast-to-mast wind inference task. Using terrain geometry 748 

alone as input, the predicted inter-mast wind speed ratios agree with annual-mean mea- 749 

surements at an average absolute relative error of 11.83%. Finally, entropy-based analysis 750 

provides an interpretable lens: the learned attention becomes increasingly diffuse with height, 751 

mirroring the physical transition from terrain-dominated boundary-layer flow to more homo- 752 

geneous free-stream regimes, which supports that the model captures meaningful structure 753 

rather than relying on spurious shortcuts. 754 

Despite these advances, several limitations warrant consideration for future work. First, 755 

the current framework is trained exclusively on CFD-simulated data, and bridging the gap 756 

to real-world observations remains an open challenge. Since CFD simulations rely on simpli- 757 

fied boundary conditions and turbulence models, discrepancies may exist between simulated 758 

and actual atmospheric flows. In practical applications, incorporating in-situ monitoring 759 

27 data through a correction model may help reduce this gap and further improve predictive 760 

reliability. Second, incorporating physics-informed loss terms into the operator learning 761 

framework may further constrain the prediction of these secondary velocity components and 762 

improve overall accuracy. In addition, when sufficient computational resources are available, 763 

training separate models or dedicated prediction branches for each velocity component may 764 

further improve the accuracy of the secondary components by allowing the network to learn 765 

component-specific flow features more effectively. 766 

Moreover, the present study focuses on steady-state wind fields under neutral atmospheric 767 

stability, whereas practical wind resource assessment increasingly demands time-varying pre-768 

dictions that capture diurnal cycles, transient weather events, and turbulent fluctuations rel-769 

evant to fatigue loading and grid integration. Extending the framework to unsteady flows, 770 

potentially through temporal attention mechanisms or coupling with mesoscale model out-771 

puts, represents a natural and important direction. 772 

4 Method 773 

We instantiate the proposed dual-attention principle in two complementary neural opera-774 

tor architectures that represent distinct paradigms in geometric deep learning: Patch-solver, 775 

a Transformer-based implementation that operates directly on point coordinates through 776 

global slice attention and local voxel-based sectional attention; and Patch-GTO, a graph-777 

operator variant that explicitly constructs edge connectivity by combining the original mesh 778 

edges with additional edges established via KNN search, and processes messages through 779 

attention-based aggregation modules. Both architectures share the core dual-attention de-780 

signdecomposing the solution operator into local terrain-confined and global domain-wide 781 

pathwaysbut differ in how they encode spatial relationships: Patch-solver relies on implicit 782 

geometric features (spatial coordinates, SDF field) processed through attention, while Patch-783 

GTO leverages explicit graph topology to aggregate information from spatially adjacent 784 

neighbors. In the following subsections, we detail the Patch-solver architecture and its key 785 

components, with Patch-GTO adopting an analogous dual-attention integration within the 786 

graph-operator framework. 787 

4.1 Patch-solver 788

A Numerical simulation of flow over complex terrain 1070 

This study uses a modified k-l turbulence model [46] specifically adapted for large-scale 1071 

flows over complex terrain. The model has been validated against field measurements from 1072 

the Bolund experiment [47, 48] and from an operational wind farm in southern China. 1073 

The core analysis framework is based on steady Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) 1074 

simulations, through which the wind field distributions over complex terrain are computed 1075 

under 16 different inflow directions. 1076 

It is worth noting that, as a steady-state modeling approach, RANS analysis typically 1077 

corresponds to the mean wind-field distribution over a certain averaging period in real mea- 1078 

surements, such as several hours, days, or even longer [44, 45], depending on the practical 1079 

application scenario. On the other hand, RANS simulations can provide physically consistent 1080 

wind-speed relationships between arbitrary locations across the terrain, which is particularly 1081 

valuable for subsequent engineering applications, such as inferring the actual wind speed at 1082 

any potential locations from measurements collected by meteorological masts. The detailed 1083 

numerical setup is described below. 1084

43 D Zero-shot resolution robustness evaluation 1197 

The core framework proposed in this work is designed to be resolution-robust, which is 1198 

one of the major advantages of neural operator methods over conventional models defined 1199 

on regular grids, such as convolution-based networks [55] and Vision Transformers [56]. 1200 

In particular, our framework directly operates on unstructured point clouds and learns a 1201 

continuous operator mapping from geometric-aware spatial samples to the corresponding 1202 

wind field, rather than relying on a fixed lattice resolution. Therefore, after training, the 1203 

model should ideally maintain stable predictive performance even when the number of query 1204 

points observed in a single forward pass changes. This property is especially important 1205 

for practical deployment, where the available point density may vary across computational 1206 

budgets, domain discretizations, and hardware constraints. 1207 

To provide a strict and spatially complete evaluation, we adopt a partition-and-merge 1208 

protocol in the zero-shot setting. For each full test point cloud, we divide the entire set of 1209 

query points into disjoint subsets with inference ratios of 10%, 20%, 30%, 40%, 50%, 70%, 1210 

90%, and 100%. Each subset is independently fed into the trained model, and the predicted 1211 

velocities are then restored to their original point indices and merged to reconstruct the 1212 

full-field solution over the complete domain. For the last batch, the remaining query points 1213 

are directly fed into the trained model. For example, when the ratio is set to 70%, the model 1214 

first processes 70% of the points and then processes the remaining 30%. This evaluation 1215 

protocol keeps the terrain geometry, the target flow field, and the final evaluation coordinates 1216 

unchanged, while varying only the number of points processed in each forward pass. This 1217 

property is particularly important for wind field prediction over complex terrain, where the 1218 

prediction error is often altitude-dependent. Therefore, performing inference on partitioned 1219 

subsets and then merging the predictions back to the full domain provides a more reliable 1220 

assessment of resolution robustness than directly evaluating on a randomly sparsified point 1221 

cloud. 1222 

The full zero-shot test results are summarized in Table D.1, including both prediction 1223 

errors and GPU memory usage during inference. Overall, the results show a clear trade-off 1224 

between predictive accuracy and memory efficiency as the inference ratio (spatial resolution) 1225 

decreases. It can be seen that lower inference ratios lead to consistently higher errors, while 1226 

substantially reducing the GPU memory required in each forward pass. Specifically, for 1227 

example, at an inference ratio of 10%, the MSE and relative L2 error of Umag increase to 1228 

1.230 and 9.109%, corresponding to relative increases of approximately 19% and 9.9% over 1229 

the full-resolution setting (100%). Meanwhile, the maximum GPU memory usage is reduced 1230 

from 3.675 GB to 0.445 GB, which is close to 12% of the full-resolution requirement. In 1231 

addition, the error curves become nearly flat once the inference ratio reaches 50% or above. 1232 

The differences among the 50%, 70%, and 90% settings are minimal for all reported metrics, 1233 

indicating that the model has already captured most of the effective spatial information 1234 

at these ratios. Taken together, these results demonstrate that the proposed framework 1235 

achieves strong zero-shot resolution robustness, while also offering flexible control over the 1236 

trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. 1237
