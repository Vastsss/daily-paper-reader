# PrismNet: Viewing Time Series Through a Multi-Modal Prism for Interpretable Power Load Forecasting 

Yuxuan Chen, Shuo Dai, Ruoyi Xu and Haipeng Xie, Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract — Load forecasting plays a pivotal role in the safe and stable operation of power systems. Conven-tional deep learning methods often struggle to adapt to few-shot scenarios frequently encountered in industrial applications. Existing multi-modal approaches typically overlook domain-specific cross-modal semantic alignment and lack sufficient mechanism interpretability. To address these challenges, this study proposes PrismNet, an in-terpretable multi-modal framework for power load fore-casting. First, a multi-modal augment module integrates text and image modalities to strengthen load time se-ries representations, empowering the model with few-shot learning capabilities. Subsequently, we design a Par-tial Information Decomposition (PID) guided multi-modal contrastive learning (CL) mechanism to achieve domain-specific cross-modal semantic alignment. This process elucidates the intrinsic interactions among modalities and offers a new lens for interpretability. Extensive experiments on real-world public datasets demonstrate that Prism-Net outperforms strong deep learning and multi-modal baselines, particularly in few-shot settings, while provid-ing a trustworthy and interpretable solution for safety-critical electric load scenarios. Our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PrismNet-9DFC. 

Index Terms — Power system; Load forecasting; Multi-modal; Partial information decomposition 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# ELECTRIC power is a fundamental energy source under-pinning modern industry, economic growth, and daily life. Accurate power load forecasting is essential for opti-mizing resource allocation [1], improving unit commitment strategies, and ensuring the secure and stable operation of the power system [2]. Electric load forecasting methods can be broadly cate-gorized into statistical modeling and data-driven approaches based on artificial intelligence. Statistical modeling primar-ily relies on autoregressive time series paradigms, such as ARIMA, exponential smoothing. These statistical models are often combined with additional pre-processing and post-processing steps to solve short-term load forecasting tasks in early studies [3], [4]. While such methods are theoretically straightforward and lightweight, they heavily depend on data stationarity and pronounced trend or seasonal patterns, and are

Data-driven approaches based on artificial intelligence have become mainstream in recent years. Early methods centered on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which are adept at capturing temporal de-pendencies and inter-variable relationships [5]. With the advent of the Transformer architecture [6], attention-based models have advanced rapidly for time series forecasting, giving rise to numerous variants [7]–[9] that have been widely applied to electric load forecasting [10], [11]. However, despite the impressive results of Transformer-based deep learning models, their cross-entity generalization and performance in few-shot settings remain limited, making them ill-suited to the data-scarce scenarios common in industrial practice. More recently, driven by advances in multi-modal large models, researchers have explored leveraging auxiliary modali-ties to achieve more accurate and more data-availability–robust forecasting. Specifically, LLM-based forecasting has been widely applied to industrial scenarios such as electric load, traffic flow, weather etc. Some approaches recast time series forecasting as prompt-based inference, converting sequences into textual prompts for an LLM and letting the LLM output

predictions [12], [13]. This bypasses large-scale retraining; however, pre-trained LLMs often struggle to capture fine-grained temporal dynamics. Other studies fine-tune or retrain parts of the encoder and decoder layers to encode numerical information effectively and exploit the model’s prior knowl-edge for few-shot learning (FSL) [14]–[16]. Nevertheless, the inherent modality gap between numerical signals and textual representations continues to limit performance. Beyond text, researchers have explored image-based rep-resentations for time series forecasting. Image modalities (e.g., line plots and grayscale maps) can explicitly preserve temporal dependencies and spatial correlations. Forecasting is then performed by embedding images into a vector space with pretrained visual encoders and combining them with Transformer-based architectures [17]–[19]. Nevertheless, since large vision models are optimized for image-specific features, their transfer to numerical modalities remains limited. Most prior work emphasizes using auxiliary modalities to boost prediction accuracy, but often neglects cross-modal se-mantic alignment, especially the alignment of domain-specific knowledge. This omission becomes more pronounced in indus-trial applications, where model interpretability is indispens-able. Without clear interpretability, the internal mechanisms through which multi-modal inputs enhance forecasting remain obscure. Such opacity undermines confidence in the results and hampers adoption in safety-critical electric-load scenarios. In this context, we propose PrismNet, a foundation model for interpretable multi-modal power load forecasting. The name is inspired by an optical prism that separates light into its constituent wavelengths; analogously, PrismNet leverages textual and visual views to uncover deeper patterns in raw time series data, yielding more comprehensive and explainable predictions. The framework comprises three components: 1) Multi-modal Augment module converts the raw load series into auxiliary text and image modalities. 2) Tri-Modality En-coding embeds all three modalities into a shared vector space and performs forecasting with a cross-attention Transformer. 

3) Interpretable Alignment module achieves domain-specific cross-modal semantic alignment via multi-modal contrastive learning. As shown in Fig. 1, unlike conventional deep models and prior multi-modal approaches, our method uses text and image to strengthen the representation of time series modality. Moreover, grounded in partial information decomposition, we reveal the underlying mechanisms of multi-modal interactions, providing a bridge between implicit learning paradigms and industry-friendly interpretability. The main contributions are threefold: 1) We introduce PrismNet, an interpretable multi-modal framework for power load forecasting that integrates text and image to strengthen load time series representations. 2) We develop a multi-modal contrastive learning strategy grounded in partial information decomposition, which eluci-dates the intrinsic interactions among modalities and offers a new lens for interpretability. 3) Extensive experiments on real-world public datasets demonstrate that PrismNet outperforms strong deep-learning and multi-modal baselines, while achieving superior few-shot learning capability and improved model interpretability. 

II. R ELATED WORKS 

A. Deep Learning-based Approaches 

This section focuses on deep learning-based approaches for time series forecasting. Early methodologies, including Recur-rent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Convolutional Neural Net-works (CNNs), demonstrated strong capabilities in capturing temporal and inter-channel dependencies [20]. More recently, the field has been dominated by Transformer-based architec-tures. Seminal works in this area include Informer [8], which introduced an efficient ProbSparse self-attention mechanism, Autoformer [7], which leveraged series decomposition and an auto-correlation mechanism, and FEDformer [9], which inno-vatively applied attention in the frequency domain. More re-cent innovations like DLinear [21] achieve efficient forecasting with a simple linear structure, while PatchTST [22] adapted the Transformer encoder to process time series patches. These state-of-the-art methods have found widespread application in various industrial domains. Guo, et al. [5] proposed a BiLSTM-based framework for load forecasting via multi-task learning. Zhao, et al. [10] and Wang, et al. [11] respectively utilized a hybrid attention Transformer and a Bayesian Trans-former for multi-energy load forecasting. However, despite their significant success, these deep learning-based methods still exhibit limitations in cross-domain generalization and per-formance under data scarcity, making them difficult to adapt to the few-shot scenarios prevalent in industrial applications. 

B. Multi-Modal for Time Series 

The integration of multi-modal analysis has recently emerged as a promising direction for time series forecasting. While early works like MIF-DNQR [23] demonstrated the benefits of fusing multi-source information for probabilistic load forecasting, their scope was confined to integrating het-erogeneous time series data. The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has paved the way for text-enhanced fore-casting. Approaches like PromptCast [12] and TRACE [13] formulated the task using natural language prompts. However, their efficacy was hindered by the intrinsic domain gap be-tween numerical series and textual representations. To address this, GPT4TS [24] and LLM4TS [15] re-trained tokenizers on time series data to enhance the LLM’s understanding of the numerical modality. Building on this, TimeLLM [16] and UniTime [14] leveraged pre-trained prior knowledge for successful few-shot forecasting. A key limitation, however, is that these methods primarily focused on refining time series embeddings rather than explicitly aligning representations across different modalities. Beyond text, image has been explored as another modal-ity to enrich time series analysis. VisionTS [17] employed a pre-trained visual autoencoder for zero-shot forecasting, however this method ignored the inter-variable interactions. TimeMixer++ [18] utilized multi-scale frequency representa-tions from images to improve the learning of temporal patterns. TimeVLM [19] employs a frozen large vision-language model (VLM) to obtain image embeddings, which are then used for multi-modal fusion forecasting. Nevertheless, existing meth-ods suffer from two key limitations: inadequate multi-modal CHEN et al. : PRISMNET: VIEWING TIME SERIES THROUGH A MULTI-MODAL PRISM FOR INTERPRETABLE POWER LOAD FORECASTING 3

> •

C. Interpretable Alignment 

Recent multi-modal studies on power-load forecasting often rely on simple fusion schemes [19], [25], while overlooking cross-modal semantic alignment, particularly domain-specific knowledge. This misalignment hinders cross-modal composi-tional reasoning. To address this, we design a multi-modal contrastive learning strategy that aligns representations across modalities and enhances model interpretability. Our design is inspired by partial information decomposition. PID posits that the information about a target can be decom-posed into contributions induced by different factors [26]. argue that the integrated gains achieved through interactions among modalities naturally accord with the PID perspective. Let {M1, M 2, ..., M n} denote a set of modalities, Y the pre-diction target, and Inf o (·) the informative content provided by the n-modal ensemble. Then Inf o (Y ; M1, M 2, ..., M n) can be viewed as the aggregate of three interaction types: uniqueness ,

redundancy , and synergy .1) Uniqueness. Modality Mi may contribute information that no other modality Mj , i ̸ = j provides. Let Ui denote the unique contribution of modality i.2) Redundancy. Two modalities Mi and Mj can carry the same or overlapping information. We use R to denote the redundant contribution. 3) Synergy. The joint use of Mi and Mj can reveal addi-tional information about Y that is unavailable from any single modality. We denote this cross-modal synergistic contribution by S. V. E XPERIMENTS 

In this section, we evaluate PrismNet on various tasks and address three questions: 1) Does multi-modal infor-mation enable accurate power load forecasting? 2) Does PrismNet perform well under few-shot settings? 3) To what extent does PrismNet demonstrate interpretable multi-modal enhancement? 

A. Experiment Setup 

Datasets. We use three real-world datasets, Electricity 1,NEST [28], StoreNet [29], covering user, building, and dis-tribution network loads. Details are provided in Appendix C. 

Baselines. We compare PrismNet with strong baselines: 1) Multi-modal models: Time-LLM [16], PromptCast [12], TimeCMA [25], TimeVLM [19] ,VisionTS [17]. 2) MLP-based model: Dlinear [21]. 3) Transformer-based models: PatchTST [22], FEDformer [9], Autoformer [7]. We evaluate predictive performance using two common metrics: mean absolute error (MAE) and mean squared error (MSE). 

Implementation Details. The multi-modal embedding dimen-sion is set to 128, the input window length is 480 and forecasting horizons are h ∈ {24 , 96 , 192 , 336 }. We use a batch size of 32, an initial learning rate of 0.0001, the Adam optimizer, and train for 10 epochs with early stopping (patience = 5) and dropout = 0.1. For other deep baselines, we tune training hyperparameters while keeping their model architectures unchanged to ensure a fair comparison. For other LLM-based models, same input prompts were used. The complete parameter settings can be seen in our code.

C. PrismNet: an Effective Few-Shot Learner 

In industrial applications, the increasing number of newly integrated entities often leads to limited historical data for each entity, creating numerous few-shot scenarios. Traditional deep models typically struggle in such few-shot settings, as they are prone to overfitting on scarce data. Therefore, it is crucial to test the model’s few-shot learning capabilities. Following the standard setting [16], [17], [19], only 10% training data are used. Fig. 5 presents the prediction results.

VI. C ONCLUSION 

In this study, we introduce PrismNet, an interpretable multi-modal framework for power load forecasting. By augmenting load time series representations with complementary textual and visual cues, PrismNet improves knowledge coverage beyond the limited unimodal. Moreover, the proposed PID-guided multi-modal contrastive learning enforces domain-specific cross-modal semantic alignment, yielding a princi-pled interpretability perspective on multi-modal interactions. Extensive evaluations on real-world public datasets demon-strate consistent gains over strong deep-learning and multi-modal baselines, with notable improvements under few-shot scenarios, supporting PrismNet as a trustworthy solution for safety-critical electric load scenarios.
