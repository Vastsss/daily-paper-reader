# Emergent Symbolic Structure in Health Foundation Models: Extraction, Alignment, and Cross-Modal Transfer 

Gajendra Katuwal 1 Advait Koparkar 1 Salar Abbaspourazad 1 Anshuman Mishra 1 Sarvesh Kirthivasan 1

# Abstract 

Health foundation models (FMs) learn useful rep-resentations from wearable sensors, but interpret-ing what they encode and transferring that knowl-edge across modalities after training remains dif-ficult. We present a post-training framework that decomposes frozen embeddings into interpretable directions, referred to as symbols , and use these symbols to align the embedding spaces without re-training. We evaluate the framework on three FMs for photoplethysmography (PPG) and accelerom-eter data, independently pretrained on ∼20M min-utes of unlabeled data from ∼172K participants, and analyzed on a held-out cohort of 30K sub-jects. We find that extracted symbols associate selectively with health conditions and physiologi-cal attributes, and these associations are partially shared across modalities and architectures. Cross-modal transfer via symbols retains more than 95% of in-domain performance, is nearly symmetric across domain directions, and saturates with lim-ited paired data, together indicating that align-ment recovers a shared low-dimensional subspace rich in physiological information. Overall, these results suggest that health FM embeddings con-tain an interpretable symbolic organization that is shared across modalities and supports cross-domain transfer without joint training. 

# 1. Introduction 

Health FMs trained on wearable biosignals (Spathis et al., 2020; Abbaspourazad et al., 2023; 2024; Narayanswamy et al., 2024; Pillai et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2025) have demon-strated that health-relevant structure can be learned from large amounts of unlabeled sensor data. However, like other deep learning systems, they lack explicit symbolic structure for transparent, structured reasoning (Kahneman, 2011). Bridging this gap is a central goal of neurosymbolic

AI (Garcez & Lamb, 2020), and is especially pressing in healthcare, where interpretability and structured decision-making are critical. Even partial progress, surfacing symbol-like components from learned representations, would aid interpretability and unlock new capabilities such as cross-modal transfer. A natural question is whether such symbols must be imposed externally, or whether they already emerge within the learned representations and can be surfaced post hoc. Recent work provides theoretical motivation to expect that interpretable, shared structure exists within neural embed-dings. The Linear Representation Hypothesis (LRH) (El-hage et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024), supported by mech-anistic interpretability work (Olah et al., 2020; Elhage et al., 2022a), posits that neural networks encode individ-ual concepts as linear directions in representation space. The Universality Hypothesis (Li et al., 2016; Olah et al., 2020; Chughtai et al., 2023) and the Platonic Representa-tion Hypothesis (Huh et al., 2024) go further, suggesting that networks trained under differing architectures, modal-ities, and objectives converge toward similar latent struc-tures, implying that symbol-like components may be par-tially shared across modalities despite very different sensing mechanisms. Motivated by these hypotheses, we ask: 

Do health FM embeddings admit a symbol-like decomposition that is consistent across modali-ties? If so, do the resulting components carry interpretable health meaning, and can they sup-port cross-modal transfer without joint training? 

Such shared symbolic structure is not directly visible in raw embeddings, where latent factors are entangled across coor-dinates (Hinton et al., 1986; Bengio et al., 2013) and feature superposition compounds the problem (Olah et al., 2020; Elhage et al., 2022b). We address this by viewing frozen embeddings through representational lenses — linear pro-jections that re-express them in coordinate systems whose components are more interpretable and comparable across models (§3). We use symbol as an operational term for a component with selective health associations that can be extracted, aligned, and transferred across modalities. Like 1

# 2. Related Work 

Our framework connects to two lines of research: decom-posing neural representations into interpretable units, and aligning representations across domains. 

Interpretability through decomposition. Sparse autoen-coders recover monosemantic features from polysemantic neurons (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2024), scaling to frontier models (Templeton et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024), extending to causal circuits (Marks et al., 2024) and multi-dimensional features (Engels et al., 2025). (Yam-agiwa et al., 2023) showed that ICA applied to word and image embeddings reveals shared geometric structure across modalities. Concept-based methods explain models through predefined directions (Kim et al., 2018; Koh et al., 2020; Oikarinen et al., 2023) or population-level representation en-gineering (Zou et al., 2023; Marks & Tegmark, 2024). Our work differs in that we study health FM embeddings, require no concept supervision, and discover symbols from frozen embeddings using classical projections evaluated post hoc against clinical attributes. 

Cross-modal alignment and transfer. Most cross-modal alignment methods require joint training (Radford et al., 2021). Post hoc alternatives include model stitching, which learns a linear layer between intermediate represen-tations (Bansal et al., 2021), and relative representations, which re-express embeddings as similarities to shared an-chor points (Moschella et al., 2023). Comparison measures such as Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) (Kornblith et al., 2019) quantify representational similarity but do not pro-duce a mapping for transfer; for a broader taxonomy see (Su-cholutsky et al., 2023). Our approach operates on frozen embeddings through explicit symbol alignment (one-to-one matching or linear map), enabling both transfer and inter-pretability within the same framework. 

# 3. Method 

3.1. Dataset and Model Pretraining 

We use three separately trained FMs, each with its own architecture, modality, and training objective rather than a single multi-modal model. Each is trained on approximately 20 million minutes of unlabeled sensor data collected under informed consent from the same ∼172 thousand participants in the Apple Heart and Movement Study (AHMS) (MacRae, 2021): • Vision Transformer trained on PPG (PPG ViT) • EfficientNet trained on PPG (PPG EfficientNet) • Vision Transformer trained on accelerometer (Ac-cel ViT) See (Abbaspourazad et al., 2023) and (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024) for architecture and pretraining details. Note that the Accel ViT used here is the uni-modal encoder trained solely on accelerometer data, not the knowledge-distilled variant from (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024). Each model produces a d=256 -dimensional embedding per 1 min input window. We aggregate these into subject-level embeddings by averaging across all windows for a given par-ticipant, yielding one vector h ∈ Rd per subject per model. This averaging discards within-subject temporal structure in favor of a stable per-subject representation suitable for population-level analysis. Symbol extraction, alignment, meaning analysis, and transfer experiments all use a held-out cohort of 30 thousand subjects not seen during pretrain-ing. We split this cohort 80/20 into an operator-fit set and an evaluation set. Symbol operators and downstream classifiers are fitted on the operator-fit set. All reported metrics use the evaluation set with frozen operators.

3.3. Evaluation 

We evaluate the symbolic framework against a set of tar-get labels derived from participant metadata in the AHMS, organized into three groups comprising 23 binary targets (Table 1). Health condition labels are constructed from participants’ self-reported survey responses following the protocol in (Abbaspourazad et al., 2023); we aggregate re-lated items into eight condition categories (Supplementary Table 2). Physiological markers (sex, age, BMI) are from enrollment data. Blood type is user-reported via the Apple Health app profile. Health conditions and physiological markers serve as positive controls with expected physiologi-cal signal; blood type serves as a negative control with no expected signal in either modality. 3.3.1. # 4. Results 

4.1. Do Symbols Align Across Modalities? 

If independently trained models on different sensor modal-ities converge on a shared representational structure, their 4Emergent Symbolic Structure in Health Foundation Models 

learned features should align across modalities. Figure 2 shows results for PPG EfficientNet vs Accel ViT, the hard-est pair (different modality and architecture); the other two pairs show similar patterns. 

Cross-modal correspondence is present in embeddings and symbol extraction reshapes it. Comparing raw em-bedding dimensions without alignment yields near-zero cross-modal correlation. Bijective alignment recovers sub-stantial correspondence even on raw embeddings (Fig-ure 2a), and symbol extraction further reshapes how this correspondence is distributed across components. Over-all, cross-modal correspondence is already present in the raw embeddings; symbol extraction reshapes how it is dis-tributed, with ICA concentrating it into a few top symbols and NMF spreading it more broadly (see Supplementary 7.1 for details). 

CCA reveals shared cross-modal linear structure. Dif-ferences between extractors largely disappeared under CCA alignment (Figure 2c). All representations converged to near-identical performance ( r ≈ 0.99 at top ranks). This convergence is expected because CCA (Hotelling, 1936) solves for the linear projections that maximize cross-modal correlation, effectively bypassing whatever basis the extrac-tor produced. These results suggest that PPG and accelerom-eter embeddings share a linearly accessible subspace, and that CCA recovers a maximally correlated coordinate sys-tem for it. Whether this subspace encodes meaningful phys-iology is tested in Section 4.2. 

Sparse CCA preserves alignment with interpretability. 

As shown above, CCA alignment is insensitive to the choice of extractor, but its canonical projections are dense and individual symbols difficult to interpret. We examined whether interpretability could be improved by imposing sparsity on the CCA projection weights without sacrificing alignment (Figure 2d). Low-to-moderate L1 regularization (λ ≤ 2 × 10 −2) maintained > 95% of standard CCA’s alignment capacity while yielding sparser projections. Only at high sparsity ( λ > 4 × 10 −2) did alignment degrade substantially. This suggests that much of the shared linear structure can be captured by a relatively small number of contributing dimensions.

4.3. Can Symbols Support Cross-Domain Transfer? 

We evaluated cross-modal symbolic transfer using all three foundation models and six directed domain pairs using the pipeline described in §3.3.2. 

Symbols enable cross-modal transfer without joint train-ing. Figure 4a summarizes transfer performance; best re-sults are achieved with the CCA family of alignments. Phys-iological markers transfer most readily (AUC 0.88–1.00, 

≈100% retention). Health conditions follow with AUC 0.71–0.83 ( 98 .7% ± 1.6% retention), while blood type re-mains near chance (AUC ≈ 0.56 ) as expected for a negative control (full per-target AUC in Supplementary Tables 4–6). Without symbol extraction and alignment, raw embedding transfer yields near-chance AUC ( ∼0.50). Bijective match-ing on raw embeddings (grey diamonds in Figure 4a) recov-ers partial transfer (health mean AUC = 0 .67 ) but remains well below CCA, because raw dimensions are entangled and a single bijective mapping cannot disentangle them. A natural concern is that cross-modal transfer merely propa-gates demographic information shared between modalities. To rule this out, we include a per-condition confounder baseline that predicts each health target from age, sex, and BMI alone (red ticks in Figure 4a). Every health condition exceeds its confounder baseline, confirming that symbolic transfer carries condition-specific information that demo-graphics alone do not explain. 

Transfer is symmetric across domain directions. Fig-ure 4b shows that forward and reverse transfer AUC are tightly correlated across all targets and domain pairs ( r =0.995 , mean |∆AUC | = 0 .009 ). This near-perfect sym-metry indicates that alignment recovers an approximate isomorphism between symbol spaces. This has a practical implication for data efficiency. An isomorphic mapping has few effective degrees of freedom, so it can be estimated from a small number of paired observations - a prediction we test directly in the efficiency analysis below. 

Symbolic transfer is data-efficient. We evaluate how much paired data is needed for effective cross-modal trans-fer by sweeping the number of paired subjects used for alignment (Figure 4c). All eight health conditions plateau above 95% retention by ∼7.5K paired subjects, with only marginal gains from additional data. We discuss the impli-cations of this rapid saturation in Section 5. 

# 5. Discussion 

A physiologically rich shared subspace. Under CCA, all symbol extractors converge to near-identical cross-modal correlation regardless of modality pairing or extraction basis (Figure 2c). This suggests that CCA recovers a shared sub-space invariant to both modality and extraction basis (Lyu et al., 2022), since each modality is a view of the underly-ing physiology and each extractor is a view of a modality’s embedding space. Beyond geometric alignment, aligned symbols carry consistent health associations across modal-ities, explaining the high transfer retention ( >98%). This consistency holds even for raw embeddings, suggesting extraction sharpens rather than creates the structure. Dif-ferent extractors simply offer complementary lenses suited to different downstream tasks. This is consistent with the Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., 2024): mod-els trained on different modalities converge because they observe the same underlying physiology. 

Symmetric, data-efficient transfer suggests a low-dimensional shared subspace. Cross-modal transfer is nearly symmetric, suggesting an approximate isomorphism between symbol spaces. A similar isomorphism was ob-served in cross-lingual word embeddings, where indepen-dently trained languages shared enough geometric structure that a simple linear rotation sufficed for alignment (Conneau et al., 2018). In our setting, the analogous consequence is that cross-modal information transfer saturates with limited paired data used for alignment (Figure 4c). This observation suggests that the shared structure between modalities has few effective degrees of freedom. Symbols as an interpretable interface: directions and open problems. Our results suggest that health FM em-beddings already contain symbol-like structure that can be surfaced post hoc. These symbols are not only interpretable within a single model but partially shared across modalities, enabling a common vocabulary through which separately trained models can be compared and composed. Connecting symbols to model predictions through causal circuits (Marks et al., 2024) would deepen mechanistic understanding of what drives health predictions. Exploring nonlinear ex-traction methods such as sparse autoencoders may surface structure missed by linear projections. Individual symbol meaning analysis with comprehensive stratified analysis across subpopulations remains an important open direction. The symbolic layer also enables targeted modal unlearning, where specific symbols are suppressed to remove learned biases without retraining the underlying model. Symbolic reasoning over extracted components, composing symbols to derive higher-order health inferences, is a natural next step toward neurosymbolic health AI. Finally, projecting symbols from human-uninterpretable modalities such as accelerometry into more legible spaces such as PPG or ECG offers a path toward interpretability transfer, where the meaning of opaque sensor signals is explained in terms of physiologically familiar ones. 

Limitations. Our extraction uses linear projections, which may miss nonlinear structure (Engels et al., 2025). All three models share the same ∼172K training population, so align-ment may partly reflect shared covariance; extending this analysis to models trained on entirely disjoint populations would further corroborate whether alignment reflects shared physiology rather than shared demographics. Three obser-vations argue against a purely demographic explanation: the blood-type negative control produces no signal despite sharing population statistics; health conditions transfer with varying strength (cardiovascular strong, hepatorenal weak); and every condition exceeds a confounder baseline trained on age, sex, and BMI. 

# 6. Conclusion 

We showed that frozen health FM embeddings can be re-expressed via linear projections into symbol spaces that are selectively grounded in health conditions, partially shared across modalities, and useful for cross-modal transfer with limited paired data.

# 7. Supplementary 

7.1. Symbol Alignment Details 

Applying bijective matching to raw embedding dimensions recovers substantial cross-modal correspondence (Head-10 ≈ 0.56 , area under the correlation curve ≈ 0.32 ), establishing a strong baseline. ICA yields the strongest top-ranked matches but decays rapidly, suggesting it isolates a small set of highly shared modes. In contrast, NMF maintains strong alignment across a much broader range of symbols (area under the correlation curve: 0.39 vs. 0.21 for ICA; Figure 2b), indicating a more distributed shared vocabulary. This behavior is consistent with their inductive biases: ICA favors statistically independent sources (Hyv ¨arinen & Oja, 2000), whereas NMF’s non-negativity promotes parts-based factors that align more consistently across many components (Lee & Seung, 1999). 

7.2. Symbol Meaning Quantification 

Letting z denote a symbol activation and y ∈ { 0, 1} indicate condition presence, we compute the Wasserstein-1 (W1) distance between p(z | y=1) and p(z | y=0) . Embeddings are z-scored to unit variance per symbol before computing W1, so that values are in units of standard deviations. We use two complementary metrics. W1 captures full distributional differences (shifts, spreads, and shape changes), making it sensitive to any systematic difference between groups. However, W1 is influenced by sample size imbalance, producing a nonzero floor for minority groups even under the null. Cohen’s d measures the standardized mean difference and has 

E[d] = 0 under the null regardless of group size, eliminating this floor. However, it only captures mean shifts and misses higher-order distributional differences. Together, they complement each other: W1 provides a complete distributional view while Cohen’s d confirms that observed selectivity reflects genuine effect sizes rather than sample-size artifacts (Figure 6). 

7.3. Selectivity Index Details 

Both the shape (how peaked the profile is) and the magnitude (how strong the associations are) matter for assessing symbol quality: a symbol with profile [0 .002 , 0.001 , 0.001] is peaked but indistinguishable from noise. Standard sparsity measures such as the Hoyer index (Hoyer, 2004) capture only shape, not magnitude. The Selectivity Index (Eq. 4) combines both. L1 is preferred over L2 because L2 upweights large values via squaring, which is redundant with Hoyer’s own sensitivity to concentrated peaks. The product Hoyer × ∥ w∥1 gives a clean factorization: Hoyer captures shape (how concentrated the profile is) and L1 captures magnitude (how large the associations are). 

7.4. Choice of AUC as Transfer Metric 

Because symbol alignment optimizes correlation rather than scale, the predicted distribution may shift relative to the source domain. AUC is invariant to monotonic shifts in the predicted scores. It depends only on the ranking of predictions, not their absolute values. This makes AUC a suitable metric for evaluating whether alignment preserves the discriminative structure of the source classifier, independent of any scale or offset mismatch introduced by the alignment operators.
