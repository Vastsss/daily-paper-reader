# EstemPMM : Polynomial Maximization Method for Non-Gaussian Regression and Time Series in R

Serhii Zabolotnii 

Cherkasy State Business College, Cherkasy 18028, Ukraine State Scientific Research Institute of Armament and Military Equipment Testing and Certification, Cherkasy, Ukraine Uzhhorod National University, Uzhhorod, Ukraine 

Abstract 

We describe the R package EstemPMM , which implements the Polynomial Maximiza-tion Method (PMM) for parameter estimation under non-Gaussian errors. PMM exploits higher-order cumulants of the error distribution — specifically the third standardized moment γ3 and fourth standardized moment γ4 — to construct estimators that outper-form ordinary least squares (OLS) whenever the errors are asymmetric or leptokurtic. The package provides a unified interface for linear regression ( lm_pmm2() , lm_pmm3() ), autore-gressive and moving-average time-series models ( ar_pmm2() , ma_pmm2() , arma_pmm2() ,

arima_pmm2() , and seasonal variants), a data-driven dispatch function ( pmm_dispatch() )that automatically selects OLS, PMM2, or PMM3 based on the sample skewness and ex-cess kurtosis, and Monte Carlo comparison utilities. The implementation uses R’s S4 class system and follows standard generic interfaces ( coef() , fitted() , residuals() ,

predict() , summary() , AIC() , logLik() , vcov() , confint() ). Asymptotic efficiency is characterised by Kunchenko-style coefficients g2, g 3 ∈ [0 , 1] , defined as the ratios of the asymptotic variance of the PMM2 and PMM3 estimators to that of OLS. Monte Carlo experiments confirm the theoretical values and a WTI crude-oil case study illus-trates the dispatcher and parameter-precision benefits of PMM2 on real heavy-tailed data. 

EstemPMM version 0.3.2 is available from CRAN at https://CRAN.R-project.org/ package=EstemPMM under the GPL-3 licence. 

Keywords : polynomial maximization method, non-Gaussian errors, ARIMA, robust estima-tion, parameter estimation, R.

# 1. Introduction 

Statistical models in economics, finance, hydrology, and industrial quality control routinely en-counter non-Gaussian errors. Financial log-returns display pronounced leptokurtosis (Scherer, Rachev, Kim, and Fabozzi 2012; Eom, Kaizoji, and Scalas 2019); industrial measurement er-rors are frequently asymmetric (Zabolotnii, Warsza, and Tkachenko 2018); hydro-meteorological series often exhibit both skewness and heavy tails. Under such departures from normality, ordinary least squares (OLS) and conditional-sum-of-squares (CSS) estimation remain con-sistent but are no longer efficient — the Cramér–Rao bound for non-Gaussian distributions is strictly tighter than the OLS bound (Kunchenko 2002).

2 EstemPMM : PMM for Non-Gaussian Data 

The Polynomial Maximization Method (PMM) addresses this efficiency gap by incorporating higher-order sample cumulants directly into the estimating equations. PMM was introduced by Kunchenko (2002) and further developed in Kunchenko (2006). The key insight is that the score of the true (unknown) log-likelihood can be approximated polynomially in the residuals using only a limited number of central moments, without specifying the full distributional family. The second-order variant (PMM2) uses the skewness γ3 and excess kurtosis γ4 of the resid-uals to augment the OLS normal equations. Following the canonical Kunchenko notation (Kunchenko 2002; Zabolotnii et al. 2018), the efficiency coefficient g2 ∈ [0 , 1] is the ratio of the asymptotic variance of PMM2 to that of OLS (see Section 2 for the explicit formula); 

g2 = 1 indicates no PMM2 gain over OLS, while g2 → 0 in the limit of strongly skewed errors. For skewed distributions commonly encountered in practice — Gamma(2,1) ( g2 = 0 .60 ), Log-normal(0, 0.55) ( g2 ≈ 0.60 ), χ2(3) shifted ( g2 ≈ 0.56 ) — PMM2 reduces asymptotic variance by 40–44% relative to OLS. The third-order variant (PMM3), characterised by an analogous coefficient g3 ∈ [0 , 1] , extends this gain to symmetric non-Gaussian distributions through the sixth standardised cumulant γ6 (Section 2.2). Application of PMM to time series models was developed in three published works. Zabolotnii, Tkachenko, and Warsza (2022) addressed autoregressive models with asymmetric innovations and Zabolotnii, Tkachenko, and Warsza (2023) extended the approach to moving-average models; the unified treatment of the full ARIMA class is given in Zabolotnii (2025) (under review at Japanese Journal of Statistics and Data Science ), which demonstrated that PMM2-ARIMA outperforms CSS and ML estimation for AR, MA, ARMA, and ARIMA models when 

|γ3| ≥ 0.5.Despite a substantial application literature (Zabolotnii, Khotunov, Chepynoha, and Tkachenko 2021; Palahin and Juhár 2016; Warsza and Zabolotnii 2017; Zabolotnii, Warsza, and Tkachenko 2020), no open-source, production-quality R implementation existed prior to EstemPMM . Ex-isting alternatives address different aspects of robustness: rlm() in MASS (Venables and Rip-ley 2002) minimises M-estimator loss functions; lmrob() in robustbase (Maechler, Rousseeuw, Croux, Todorov, Ruckstuhl, Salibian-Barrera, Verbeke, Koller, Conceicao, and di Palma 2023) implements MM-estimators with bounded influence; rq() in quantreg (Koenker 2005) targets conditional quantiles; and Arima() /auto.arima() in forecast (Hyndman and Athanasopou-los 2021) use Gaussian or conditional likelihood. None exploit the higher-order cumulant structure that PMM targets. 

EstemPMM fills this gap. The package provides: 

• Linear regression via lm_pmm2() and lm_pmm3() , returning S4 objects of class ‘ PMM2fit ’/‘ PMM3fit ’compatible with standard R generics. 

• A complete ARIMA family: ar_pmm2() , ma_pmm2() , arma_pmm2() , arima_pmm2() , and seasonal variants ( sar_pmm2() , sma_pmm2() , sarma_pmm2() , sarima_pmm2() ), all re-turning S4 objects of class ‘ TS2fit ’. 

• PMM3 time-series analogues ( ar_pmm3() , ma_pmm3() , arma_pmm3() , arima_pmm3() ). 

• A data-driven dispatch function pmm_dispatch() that selects OLS, PMM2, or PMM3 based on sample cumulants. 

• Bootstrap inference via pmm2_inference() and ts_pmm2_inference() .Serhii Zabolotnii 3

• Monte Carlo comparison utilities ( pmm2_monte_carlo_compare() ). The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the PMM estimat-ing equations. Section 3 describes the package architecture, S4 class hierarchy, and API. Section 4 presents self-contained worked examples. Section 5 reports Monte Carlo efficiency comparisons. Section 6 applies EstemPMM to WTI crude oil prices. Section 9 concludes.

# 3. Implementation and design of EstemPMM 

3.1. Package overview 

EstemPMM is implemented in R (R Core Team 2024) using the S4 object system from the 

methods package (Chambers 2008). Version 0.3.2 comprises approximately 3 600 lines of R

code across 15 source files. The package has no compiled code: all numerical optimisation is performed via R’s optim() function. Dependencies are limited to base R (stats , graphics ,

methods , utils ). 

3.2. S4 class hierarchy 

Figure 1 shows the S4 class hierarchy. ‘BasePMM2 ’ is a virtual base class carrying shared slots (coefficients, residuals, cumulant estimates); all PMM2 fit objects extend it. Two concrete regression classes inherit directly: ‘PMM2fit ’ (from lm_pmm2() ) and ‘ TS2fit ’ (the base for time-series estimation). Eight time-series subclasses extend ‘ TS2fit ’: four non-seasonal (‘ ARPMM2 ’, ‘ MAPMM2 ’, ‘ ARMAPMM2 ’, ‘ ARIMAPMM2 ’) and four seasonal variants (‘ SARPMM2 ’, ‘SMAPMM2 ’, ‘ SARMAPMM2 ’, ‘ SARIMAPMM2 ’). The PMM3 classes (‘ PMM3fit ’, ‘ TS3fit ’, and its four non-seasonal subclasses) are independent of ‘ BasePMM2 ’; they carry analogous slots for the sixth-order cumulants used by PMM3 and are currently restricted to symmetric error distributions ( γ3 = 0 ). Every concrete class implements the full set of standard generics: coef() , fitted() , residuals() ,

predict() , summary() , print() , plot() , AIC() , BIC() , logLik() , nobs() . ‘ PMM2fit ’ and AR-type ‘ TS2fit ’ subclasses additionally support vcov() and confint() , which return the asymptotic covariance matrix and confidence intervals based on Equation (2).

3.4. Numerical algorithm 

Linear regression. lm_pmm2() uses a fixed-point iteration starting from OLS coefficients. Each step updates ˆβ by solving the augmented score equation (1) with cumulant estimates refreshed from the current residuals. Convergence is declared when ∥ ˆβ(k+1) − ˆβ(k)∥∞ < ϵ 

(default ϵ = 10 −6, maximum 200 iterations). 

Time-series models. For AR models, ar_pmm2() constructs the Yule–Walker design ma-trix via create_ar_matrix() and applies the same fixed-point iteration. For MA, ARMA, ARIMA, and seasonal variants, ts_pmm2() minimises objective (6) using optim() with method = "BFGS" , supplying CSS-based starting values from get_classical_estimates() and an-alytic gradients approximated by finite differences. 

Moment estimation. The function compute_moments() returns the sample estimates ˆσ2,

ˆμ3, ˆμ4 using unbiased denominators, and ˆγ3, ˆγ4 as plug-in estimates. These are updated at each iteration for regression and fixed at the CSS-residual values for time-series models. 

3.5. Bootstrap inference 

pmm2_inference() implements a residual-resampling bootstrap. The block argument acti-vates block bootstrap (Carlstein’s non-overlapping blocks) for time-series data. Argument 

B controls the number of replicates (default 500); seed ensures reproducibility. The func-tion returns a data frame of bootstrap estimates and optionally produces a histogram via 

plot_pmm2_bootstrap() .

3.6. Unified dispatch function 

pmm_dispatch() accepts any numeric vector or formula /data pair, computes sample cumu-lants, applies the dispatch rule from Section 2.4, fits the selected model, and returns a named list with components method (character), fit (S4 object), g2 , gamma3 , and gamma4 . For time-series input it delegates to the ARIMA family; for regression input it uses lm_pmm2() /lm_pmm3() .

3.7. Monte Carlo comparison engine 

pmm2_monte_carlo_compare() accepts a list of model specifications, each with fields model 

(character), order (integer), theta (true parameter vector), label (character), and innovations 

(a list with type and distribution parameters). It simulates n_sim data sets for each specifi-cation and fits all requested methods ("ols" , "css" , "pmm2" , "pmm3" , "ml" ), returning a list 8 EstemPMM : PMM for Non-Gaussian Data 

with components results (per-replicate estimates), summary (MSE, bias, variance, coverage), and gain (observed vs. theoretical variance ratio g2 or g3).

4.5. PMM3 for symmetric platykurtic errors 

When residuals are symmetric ( γ3 = 0 ) but distinctly non-Gaussian in their fourth and sixth cumulants, PMM2 offers no improvement over OLS ( g2 ≡ 1) but PMM3 can. We illustrate with Uniform( −1, 1) errors, for which γ4 = −1.2, γ6 = 6 .857 , and the PMM3 efficiency coefficient g3 = 1 − γ24 /(6 + 9 γ4 + γ6) = 0 .30 — a 70% asymptotic-variance reduction.

4.6. Bootstrap inference 

The asymptotic covariance matrix from vcov() relies on Equation (2) and assumes large-sample regularity. For small samples ( n ≲ 100 ), severely skewed residuals, or when infer-ence on non-linear functions of the parameters is required, EstemPMM provides a residual-resampling bootstrap. For regression:
