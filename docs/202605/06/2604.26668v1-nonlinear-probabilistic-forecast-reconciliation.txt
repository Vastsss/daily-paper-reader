Abstract 

Forecast reconciliation adjusts independently generated forecasts so that they satisfy some known constraints. While probabilistic forecast reconciliation is well established for linear constraints, some practical forecasting problems involve nonlinear relationships among variables. In this paper, we address probabilistic forecast reconciliation with nonlinear constraints for the first time. We extend both reconciliation via projection and conditioning to the case of nonlinear constraints. The projection approach reconciles forecast samples by mapping them onto the nonlinear coherent manifold. The conditioning approach adopts a sampling algorithm inspired to the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF). We evaluate both methods on synthetic and real datasets. Empirically, both reconciliation approaches generally improve forecast accuracy. The UKF-based approach achieves the best overall performance while being substantially faster than the projection one. 

Keywords: Forecast, Reconciliation, Probabilistic forecast, Nonlinear constraints 

1. Introduction 

Time series are often related by linear constraints of aggregation. For example, for a certain store, consider forecasting both the sales of each item and the total sales. The forecast of the total sales of the store should be equal to the sum of the forecast of the sales of each item. If forecasts satisfy these constraints they are coherent , while if they fail to satisfy the constraints they are 

incoherent . Base forecasts , which are created independently for each time series, are generally incoherent. Forecast reconciliation adjusts the base forecasts making them coherent. The first methods for forecast reconciliation were based on projection (Athanasopoulos, Ahmed & Hyndman, 2009; Hyndman, Ahmed, Athanasopoulos & Shang, 2011). In particular, Wickrama-

Such methods can be interpreted as pro-jecting the base point forecast onto the linear coherent subspace (Panagiotelis, Athanasopoulos, Gamakumara & Hyndman, 2021). While the above methods refer to point forecast reconciliation, the most advanced approaches perform linear probabilistic reconciliation yielding a probability density on the linear coherent subspace. Earlier studies include Taieb, Taylor & Hyndman (2021), where an empirical copula based approach for reconciliation which uses permutation of the samples was studied. There are two main approaches to probabilistic forecast reconciliation. The first one is the ex-tension of the reconciliation via projection method to the probabilistic case (Panagiotelis, Gamaku-mara, Athanasopoulos & Hyndman, 2023; Wickramasuriya, 2024). The second approach is recon-ciliation via conditioning where the incoherent density of the base forecast is sliced on the coherent subspace. This approach has been extensively studied for continuous, discrete and mixed-type of linearly constrained time series (Zambon, Azzimonti & Corani, 2023; Zambon, Agosto, Giudici & Corani, 2024a; Zambon, Azzimonti, Rubattu & Corani, 2024b). Also the probabilistic approaches of Corani, Azzimonti, Augusto & Zaffalon (2021); Corani, Azzimonti & Rubattu (2024) have been shown to be equivalent to reconciliation via conditioning (Zambon et al., 2024a). Several applications in multiple time series forecasting include nonlinear constraints between the time series, such as socio-demographic ratios like mortality or unemployment rates (Girolimetto, Panagiotelis, Fonzo & Li, 2025). Very recently the projection-based reconciliation approach has been extended to obtain coherent forecasts for time series with nonlinear constraints (Nespoli, Biswas, Rocchetta & Medici, 2026; Girolimetto et al., 2025); however, these works are limited to point forecasts. We address probabilistic forecast reconciliation under nonlinear constraints for the first time. We extend both reconciliation via projection and via conditioning to the case of time series with nonlinear constraints. The projection-based reconciliation projects each sample point from the cloud of the base forecast distribution to points on the manifold. However, it has the shortcoming of requiring a numerical optimization for each sample to be reconciled, leading to heavy computation times. yoriichi300As an alternative, we also propose an algorithm based on reconciliation via conditioning , with a sampling strategy inspired by the Unscented Kalman Filter (Julier & Uhlmann, 22004). In our experiments, it provides slightly more accurate results than projection while also substantially reducing computation time. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the notations and terminology needed for probabilistic nonlinear reconciliation. Section 3 introduces probabilistic reconciliation under nonlinear constraints and presents the extensions of reconciliation via projection and reconciliation via conditioning. Section 4 discusses the experimental setup and evaluates the proposed methods on both simulated synthetic data with different nonlinearities and two real case studies involving socio-demographic ratios of Switzerland and tourism ratio of Australia. Section 5 concludes and highlights the directions for future research along with the limitations of our proposed methods. All the codes related to the experiments can be found in the public Github repository 1 and can be reproduced. 3. Probabilistic Methods for Nonlinear Reconciliation 

Two principled approaches for linear probabilistic reconciliation are projection (Panagiotelis et al., 2023) and conditioning (Zambon et al., 2023; Corani et al., 2021). Here we extend both to the case of nonlinear constraints. 53.1. Reconciliation via Projection 

In the linear case, Panagiotelis et al. (2023) defined probabilistic forecast reconciliation through the push-forward of measures. Given a base forecast distribution ̂ ν on the space Rn, the reconciled measure ˜ν is defined as the push-forward ˜ν = ψ#̂ ν under a reconciliation map ψ : Rn → S , where 

S is the coherent subspace: for any measurable set B ⊆ S , the reconciled probability is given by 

> ˜

ν(B) = ̂ ν(ψ−1(B)) .In the linear literature, ψ is commonly defined as an orthogonal projection onto S with respect to some distance on Rn. Given a positive definite matrix W ∈ Rn×n, the distance is derived from the induced norm ∥x∥W = √x′ W−1 x. Then, ψ maps each point in Rn to its nearest point on 

S: ψ(̂y) = arg min x∈S ∥̂ y − x∥W. When W is an estimate of the covariance matrix of the base forecast errors, this mapping corresponds to the Minimum Trace (MinT) projection. Nespoli et al. (2026) and Girolimetto et al. (2025) reconcile point forecasts satisfying nonlinear constraints by projecting them as the nearest-point mapping onto the manifold M. Since M is not a linear subspace, this mapping is nonlinear. We extend this approach to probabilistic reconciliation by defining the reconciled distribution as the push-forward of the base forecast distribution under the nonlinear nearest-point map.

We consider 3 specifications for the matrix W, each corresponding to a different projection. 1. OLS : we set W as the identity matrix; this choice corresponds to the Euclidean distance in 

Rn. However, the underlying assumption of isotropic error is inappropriate the time series have different scales. 2. WLS : we set W as a diagonal matrix, whose entries are the estimated variances of the base forecast errors. This defines an anisotropic distance that accounts for the influence of the different scales present in the time series. 3. Full : we set W as the full covariance matrix of the base forecast errors, estimated via shrink-age as in (Wickramasuriya et al., 2019). This choice accounts for both heteroscedasticity and the correlation structure between base forecasts. This approach has optimal properties in the linear framework (Wickramasuriya, 2024), but no optimality guarantee currently exists in the nonlinear case.

Unscented Transform. Corani et al. (2021) showed the link, in the linear case, between proba-bilistic forecast reconciliation and the update step of the Kalman filter. We extend this idea to nonlinear reconciliation, proposing an algorithm based on the Unscented Transform used by the Unscented Kalman Filter ( UKF ) (Julier & Uhlmann, 2004; van der Linde & Labbe, 2019). We thus use the UKF as a numerical method for approximating the conditional distribution induced by the nonlinear constraint rather than as a dynamical filtering procedure. The basic idea is to approximate the probability distribution of the base forecast of the free time series by replacing it with a small deterministic set of sigma points whose mean and covariance coincide with those of 8the original distribution. These points are then propagated through the nonlinear map fu(.), and the mean and covariance of the transformed cloud are used to approximate the transformed dis-tribution. The resulting transformed points are then summarized to obtain the quantities needed for a Kalman-style update, which produces the reconciled mean and covariance of the free-level forecasts. We approximate both the incoherent and the reconciled distribution of the free time series with Gaussian distributions. We then treat the base forecasts of the constrained time series as noisy observations of the free time series.

3.3. Limitations 

We acknowledge that the proposed methods are not suitable for all scenarios. In particular, reconciliation via projection is mainly designed for continuous forecast distributions, while discrete and mixed-type time series with nonlinear constraints remain more challenging. As seen in the linear case by Zhang, Panagiotelis & Kang (2024), modifying projection to work with discrete time series requires ad hoc choices and results in computationally expensive algorithms. Conditioning has instead been applied, in the linear case, to reconcile constraints related to intermittent, discrete or mixed time series (Corani et al., 2024; Zambon et al., 2023, 2024b). However, our specific algorithm based on UKF is unsuitable to reconcile intermittent or discrete time series with nonlinear constraints due to its underlying Gaussian assumption. We leave this problem for future research. Both the projection (FULL) approach and the reconciliation via conditioning (UKF) can model the dependence between the free and constrained time series using linear measures of correlation; however this is not fully appropriate and better approaches to account for nonlinear dependencies should be tested in the future.

5. Conclusions 

We studied nonlinear probabilistic forecast reconciliation for the first time, extending (i) rec-onciliation via projection and (ii) via conditioning to the nonlinear setting. Reconciliation in general improves the accuracy compared to both the base forecast and prob-abilistic bottom-up. However, we conclude that these results are still empirical, and we have 20 not explored the connection between the improvement guarantees that were obtained using the projection method for the point forecasts (Nespoli et al., 2026; Girolimetto et al., 2025) and our probabilistic setting. In our experiments, the UKF-based reconciliation via conditioning outperforms, slightly but consistently, any other method in terms of probabilistic scores. It also provides a faster alternative to the projection method, since it does not need to go through the optimization iterations. In particular we observe faster computation in 3-4 orders of magnitudes on a single time step as shown in Tab. 4, Tab. 6, and Tab. 8. These methods, however come with some limitations. As mentioned above, the UKF might fail if the assumption of the free-level time series being Gaussian does not hold true, since that is the core idea of that approach. The projection-based method is dependent on numerical methods for projecting the samples onto the nonlinear manifold, and it might sometime have the issue of divergence or slow convergence which we did not improve upon. Although we mention the Frechèt mean as a probable new point forecast, we do not explore it much in this paper and we keep that as a future research direction. Although we use relative versions of the CRPS and Energy Scores for our experiments both for the simulations and the case studies, sometimes using these Euclidean-distance based scores might lead to misleading results as shown by Nespoli et al. (2026). Developing a new geodesic-distance based proper scoring rule to evaluate the reconciled forecast distribution, which is more meaningful on the nonlinear manifold, is thus important and can be considered as another future research direction that we keep out of this paper.
