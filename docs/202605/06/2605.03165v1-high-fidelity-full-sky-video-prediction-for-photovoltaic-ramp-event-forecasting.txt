Abstract —Accurate ultra -short -term forecasting of photovoltaic 

(PV) ramp events is essential for maintaining grid stability in solar -

integrated power systems, particularly  under rapidly changing 

cloud conditions . This paper presents a generative forecasting 

framework that integrates a future sky -video prediction model 

(PhyDiffNet) with a ramp -aware PV output forecasting model 

(RaPVFormer).  Based on the relatively slow yet chaotic dynamics 

of cloud motion, the system forecasts ramp events up to 16 minutes 

in advance at a 1 -minute resolution by capturing fine -grained 

spatiotemporal cloud patterns and generating high -fidelity full -sky 

video fra mes.  Interpretability is enhanced through attention 

visualization, highlighting cloud -occlusion regions that  significantly 

influence irradiance variability.  Supported by extensive 

quantitative evaluation, the proposed framework demonstrates 

state -of -the -ar t performance in both full -sky video prediction and 

PV output forecasting. It delivers consistent improvements in 

structural, perceptual, and temporal video quality, along with a 

10% increase in Critical Success Index (CSI)  for PV ramp detection. 

These results demonstrate the  capability  of AI -driven multimodal 

sensing for ultra -short -term solar forecasting, supporting more 

reliable renewable integration  and potentially reducing  dependence 

on reserve capacity. 

Index Terms —Full -sky images , multimodal sensing , cloud 

dynamics , photovoltaic ramp event , generative  AI .

I.  INTRODUCTION 

A.  Motivation 

LIMATE  change and the urgent need to decarbonize the 

energy sector are accelerating the global deployment of 

renewable energy technologies. Among these, 

photovoltaic (PV) generation has experienced particularly rapid 

growth, with global installed capacity increasing at an 

unprecedented pace  [1] . However, PV power output is inherently 

weather -dependent, highly sensitive to fluctuations in solar 

irradiance, cloud cover, and other atmospheric conditions. This 

results in significant variability across both temporal and spatial 

scales, posing major c hallenges for reliable grid integration  [2] .

A particularly critical challenge arises under cloudy sky 

conditions,  where rapid cloud motion can suddenly obscure or 

reveal the sun . Based on the  data  provided by  National 

Renewable Energy Laboratory  (NREL)  [3] , cloud occlusion  can 

lead to  irradiance  drop ping  by  up to  70% within  one minute. 

Such  transient event s can trigger abrupt power fluctuations in 

large -scale PV systems  [4] . These  rapid changes impose severe 

stress on power systems, necessitating the use of fast -response 

backup generation to maintain grid stability  [5], [6] . Without 

accurate short -term forecasting,  such fluctuations lead to

increased reliance on costly reserve capacity  and threaten the 

reliability of the grid  [7], [8] . Moreover, precise forecasting can 

help minimize the use of energy storage systems, thereby 

reducing battery degradation and operational costs  [9] . In 

isolated microgrids, where inertia and dispatchable capacity are 

limited, abrupt PV fluctuations can destabilize voltage and 

frequency. Ultra -short -term forecasts help pre -activate reserves 

and manage storage for stable operation  [10], [11] . In high -PV -

penetration grids, accurate minute -scale forecasts enable 

proactive dispatch, frequency regulation, and power control, 

reducing dependence on reserve resources and enhancing overall 

grid stability  [12] . Th ese  critical need s motivate develop ing 

forecasting techniques that can capture the fine -grained, 

spatiotemporal dynamics of cloud behavior and translate them 

into accurate, actionable PV power predictions. 

Recent advancements in  AI techniques  offer promising 

avenues for addressing this challenge. In particular, multimodal 

data sources such as full -sky video imagery provide rich visual 

information about cloud structures and their motion across the 

sky dome  [13] . Simultaneously, generative AI models have 

shown remarkable capability in learning complex spatiotemporal 

patterns from such data, enabling the prediction of future sky 

states with high fidelity. By learning the  dynamics  of cloud 

motion  and their occlusion effects on solar irradiance, generative 

models hold significant promise for minute -level PV forecasting. 

In this paper, we present PhyDiff Net , a novel high -fidelity 

full -sky video prediction framework based on generative AI,  and 

RaPVFormer  designed for ultra -short -term forecasting of  PV 

ramp events. By accurately predicting rapid irradiance 

fluctuations, our method provides grid  and PV plant  operators 

with earlier and more reliable warnings of PV output variability. 

These forecasting improvements support enhanced grid 

resilience, reduced reliance on reserve generation, and more 

reliable large -scale integration of solar energy. 

B.  Literature  Review  and Research Gap 

The forecasting of  PV  generation has been approached using 

a variety of techniques, including numerical weather prediction 

(NWP),  satellite -based remote sensing , local ground -based 

sensing, and hybrid methods that integrate multiple data 

modalities  [14] . However, the  spatial and temporal resolution  as 

well as the  accuracy  of these methods  vary  significantly,  making 

their results highly  complementary . NWP models are capable of 

producing solar irradiance forecasts on an hourly scale  [15] ,

while satellite -based remote sensing is typically used for intra -

day forecasting, offering temporal resolutions of 5 to 15 minutes 

[16] . Both methods are generally focused on forecasting PV

forecasting of photovoltaic ramp events, local ground -based

Early work applied computer vision techniques like image 

segmentation  [19], [20]  and CNNs such as SUNSET  [21]  to 

extract cloud features and predict PV output. To mitigate data 

constraints, classification -based methods  [22]  reduced training 

requirements, while recurrent models like ConvLSTM  [23] 

captured  the  spatiotemporal cloud dynamics. Building on these, 

the ECLIPSE framework  [24]  explicitly modeled cloud -induced 

PV fluctuations, highlighting the impact of occlusion in ramp 

forecasting. To address limitations of purely data -driven models, 

physics -informed networks like PhyDNet  [25], [26]  integrated 

physical constraints to improve generalizability and 

interpretability. More recent efforts leverage generative models 

and transformers: VideoGPT  [27]  applied sequence modeling 

from video generation to PV forecasting, inspiring subsequent 

advances like SkyGPT  [28] , which fuses physics -informed 

imagery with probabilistic deep learning  models  for ultra -short -

term predictions. Transfer learning across diverse datasets  [29] 

has further enhanced model generalization and cross -site 

adaptability. 

Despite recent advancements, accurately forecasting PV 

output under highly variable cloud conditions remains a critical 

challenge. Cloud dynamics are governed by complex, nonlinear, 

and partially chaotic atmospheric processes,  making them 

difficult to  model using traditional physical or statistical methods. 

Unlike general video object  motion  prediction tasks,  cloud 

behavior involves both translational motion and continuous 

non -rigid deformation driven by turbulent airflows, along with 

thermodynamic processe s such as condensation and evaporation 

that lead to cloud formation or dissipation. These characteristics 

make cloud dynamics uniquely challenging to model.  While 

many existing models perform reliably under stable conditions 

like clear skies or uniform cloud cover, their predictive accuracy 

declines significantly during fast -evolving  cloud  dynamics , the 

very scenarios that induce sharp PV ramp events and complicate 

real -time grid operations.  Moreover, most prior work has focused 

on general PV output forecas ting, with limited emphasis on 

forecasting rapid PV ramp events, for which ground -based full -

sky imaging systems are particularly well -suited. 

C.  Contribution  and Paper Organization 

This work presents PhyDiffNet, a high -fidelity full -sky video 

prediction framework built on generative AI, and RaPVFormer, 

a transformer -based model for ultra -short -term forecasting of  PV 

ramp events. The proposed system integrates a physics -informed 

PhyDNet to model cloud dynamics, a  video -conditional diffusion 

module  to enhance the fidelity of predicted sky frames, and a 

transformer -based forecasting model to estimate PV generation 

changes driven by transient cloud cover that intermittently 

obscures or re veals the sun. By effectively capturing the complex 

spatiotemporal patterns of cloud  motion , the framework 

produces realistic future sky states and delivers substantial 

improvements in forecasting accuracy for PV ramp events. 

The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

(1)  We propose a generative AI -based forecasting framework 

that integrates a video prediction module (PhyDiffNet) with a 

ramp -aware PV forecasting module (RaPVFormer)  that enables 

high -fidelity prediction of future full -sky video frames and 

accurate PV -output forecasting under dynamic cloud conditions. 

(2)  The proposed framework incorporates sun -localization 

masks, attention mechanisms, and ramp -sensitive loss functions 

to improve the accuracy of high -frequency PV -output 

predictions. Interpretability is provided through attention 

visualizations that highligh t critical solar -occlusion patterns. 

(3)  Extensive evaluations demonstrate that our framework 

achieves state -of -the -art performance in both full -sky video  clip 

prediction and PV forecasting. The superior spatial texture and 

temporal consistency of the predicted video  clip s significantly 

enhance the  accurate prediction  of PV  ramp events .

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows . Section II 

introduces the proposed PhyDiffNet and RaPVFormer 

frameworks. Section III provides a detailed description of the 

dataset preparation and model training process. Section IV 

explains the evaluation metrics. Section V reports the results for 

both  full -sky image prediction and PV output forecasting. The 

paper concludes with final remarks in Section VI.

C.  RaPVFormer  for  Ramp -Aware  PV  Output Forecasting 

RaPVFormer is a transformer -based architecture designed for 

short -term PV forecasting under rapidly changing cloud 

conditions.  The  architecture  of  RaPVFormer  is shown  in  Fig.  4.

The model encodes both historical and predicted sky  frames 

(15: t tI − and 1: 16 

ˆt tI + + ), along with corresponding  sun -mask s (Sun 15: t tI −

and Sun 1: 16 t tI + + ) , using a four -layer convolutional  image  encoder 5

trained from scratch. Each frame is compressed into a 128 -

dimensional feature vector through  an image encoder , effectively 

capturing cloud structures and irradiance -relevant patterns. 

Historical PV power values are embedded by  a multi -layer 

perceptron (MLP)  embedding  and concatenated with the 

corresponding historical frame features, enabling the model to 

interpret sky appearance in the context of concurrent power 

output. All visual and PV features are then projected into a 256 -

dimensional token space and en riched with sinusoidal positional 

encodings to preserve temporal ordering across the input 

sequences.     

> Fig. 4.Framework of the RaPVFormer for ramp -aware PV output forecasting .

Both historical and future token sequences are processed by 

separate stacks of two transformer layers, each consisting of 

multi -head self -attention (four heads, 256 -dimensional model 

size) and a 512 -dimensional feed -forward network. These layers 

build long -range temporal dependencies,  enabling  the model to 

reason about cloud evolution and its effect on PV variability.  A

central component of RaPVFormer is its cross -attention fusion 

mechanism, implemented as a stack of two cross -attention layers, 

each consist ing of multi -head cross -attention (four heads, 256 -

dimensional model size) and a 1024 -dimensional feed -forward 

network.  This mechanism allows the model to retrieve relevant 

contextual patterns, such as past irradiance responses to similar 

cloud formations, thereby improving its ability to anticipate 

cloud -induced PV ramps.  The fused representation is decoded by 

a PV  forecast  head and a ramp  classification head, providing both 

accurate short -term forecasts and ramp -aware predictions.

III.  DATA SET  PREPARATION  AND  MODEL  TRAINING 

A.  Data set  Preparation 

This study utilizes the publicly available S ky Image and 

Photovoltaic Power Generation  Datase t ( SKIPP’D ) [36], [37] ,

which comprises full -sky videos recorded on the Stanford 

University campus using a 360 -degree fisheye camera between 

2017 and 2019.  The dataset links are provided in the 

supplementary file  [33] . The raw data consists of full -sky  images 

at a resolution of 2048× 2048 pixels, accompanied by PV output 

measurements from a 30.1 kW array. To facilitate model training, 

these images were cropped and downscaled to 128× 128 pixels, 

as suggested in  [21] , while maintaining a temporal resolution of 

one minute.  All frames captured during nighttime periods ,

defined as intervals from dusk to dawn with PV output less than 

20% of the installed capacity , were excluded.  Additionally, 

frames with identical image content or power values due to 

sensor or camera malfunctions were removed. 

Since PV ramp events are more likely to occur under partly 

cloudy conditions than during fully clear or overcast skies, the 

dataset was filtered based on the proportion of cloud coverage 

within the camera’s field of view to improve the model’s ability 

to l earn cloud dynamic patterns.  The cloud coverage ratio  is 

calculated  based on the  cloud segmentation algorithm  [31] . To 

structure the temporal data, we applied a n overlapping  sliding 

window of 32 consecutive minutes to generate video clips. Each 

clip was required to include at least 20 frames with cloudiness 

index values between 0.1 and 0.8 to satisfy the filtering criteria. 

After preprocessing, the final dataset consisted of 24,807 filtered 

full -sky images, organized into 17,333 video clips spanning 138 

days. The dataset was split by day: 10% of the days (14 days) 

were randomly selected for testing, another 10% (14 day s) for 

validation, and the remaining 80% (110 days) were used for 6

training. This split resulted in 1,775, 1,768, and 13,790 video 

clips for the test, validation, and training sets, respectively. To 

enhance generalization, random spatial rotations and flips  of the 

whole video clips  were applied as data augmentation during 

training .

B.  Model  Training 

All models were trained using an NVIDIA H100 GPU with 80 

GB of memory, hosted by Empire AI  [38] . The implementation 

was developed in Python 3.12 using the PyTorch 2.6 framework. 

Each of the three modules was trained independently, following 

tailored strategies to optimize learning.  The  detailed settings of 

the hyperparameters are provided  in the  supplementary file  [33] .

The PhyDNet module was trained  in a sequence -to -sequence 

method  for 300 epochs with a batch size of 32 and an initial 

learning rate of  10 -4. Teacher forcing was employed , where 

ground -truth frames are fed back into the model at a scheduled 

ratio during early training stages. We applied a gradually 

decaying teacher forcing ratio, encouraging the model to rely 

increasingly on its own predictions  in the training process .

The  video -conditional diffusion module  was trained with a 

batch size of  8, a learning rate of  2×10 -4 across 100,000 training 

iterations. 

Finally, the  Ra PV Former  module was trained with a batch size 

of 64, a learning rate of  10 -4, and for  30 epochs.  It was trained 

on a combination of original  training dataset  and  the 

corresponding generated video clips via  PhyDiffNet. This 

strategy improves robustness against potential pattern biases 

introduced by the generative diffusion module.

The sun -mask channel was introduced to enhance spatial 

attention by explicitly encoding the sun's location in each frame. 

Removing this channel led to a consistent decline across all video 

quality metrics, including lower PSNR and SSIM scores, as well 

as i ncreased RMSE in PV forecasts. These findings confirm the 

importance of sun localization in helping the model capture cloud 

occlusion effects that directly influence short -term irradiance. 

A core innovation of the PhyDiffNet architecture is the 

integration of a video -conditional diffusion module , which 

serves to refine initially blurry predictions generated by the 

PhyDNet backbone. When this refinement stage was removed, 

we observed a significant drop in image quality metrics ,

particularly PSNR and VGGCS , indicating that the diffusion 

process is crucial for restoring high -frequency textures ,

preserving temporal coherence  and generating  accurate 

downstream PV forecasting. 

To assess the role of historical PV generation data in ramp 

prediction, we replaced the RaPVFormer module with the 

modified SUNSET baseline proposed in  [28] , which relies solely 

on visual input and lacks access to previous power output 

sequences.  This change had no impact on full -sky frame 

prediction quality, since the video generation module remained 

intact. However, the forecasting performance of PV output 

declined markedly. These findings highlight the value of 

incorporating historical power data , which provides critical 

context beyond what is available from visual inputs alone. 

VI.  CONCLUSIONS 

This paper presents a generative AI -based  framework for high -

fidelity full -sky video prediction and ramp -aware PV output 

forecasting. The proposed system integrates a video prediction 

module  named  PhyDiffNet with a transformer -based PV 

forecasting model  named  RaPVFormer, specifically tailored to 

predict  rapid power fluctuations driven  by rapidly changing 

cloud motion .

By combining physics -based modeling, diffusion -driven 

refinement, and attention -based temporal reasoning, our 

framework effectively captures the complex spatiotemporal 

patterns of cloud  dynamics . Experimental results demonstrate 

that the predicted full -sky frames exhibit superior visual fidelity, 

structural integrity, and temporal consistency, outperforming 

state -of -the -art baselines across both deterministic and 

generative models.  Moreover, the RaPVFormer module achieves 

high ramp detection accuracy while  maintaining low forecasting 

errors across various time horizons. The inclusion of sun 

localization, cross -attention fusion, and ramp -sensitive loss 

functions further enhances the model’s interpretability and 

responsiveness to cloud -induced solar variabilit y.  Ablation 

studies confirm the critical role of the sun -mask channels, 

diffusion -based cloud refinement, and historical PV input in 

achieving optimal performance.  These results underscore the 

effectiveness of combining physical priors with deep generative 

modeling and transformer architectures for high -resolution, data -

driven PV forecasting. The proposed framework leverages 

advanced  AI  and multimodal sensing to enhance grid reliability, 

reduce reserve requirements, and mitigate operational risks in 

renewable -dominated power systems. 10
