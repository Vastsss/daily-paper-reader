## Abstract 

Causal inference in continuous-time sequential decision problems is challenged by hidden confounders. We show that, in latent state-space models with time-varying interventions, observability of the latent dynamics from observed data is necessary for identifying dynamic treatment effects, linking control-theoretic observability to causal identifiability, even when hidden confounders affect both treatments and outcomes. We derive a continuous-time adjustment formula expressing potential outcome dis-tributions under treatment trajectories via the measurement model, latent dynamics, and the filtering distribution over latent states given observed histories. We propose Observable Neural ODEs (ObsNODEs), Neural ODE models in ob-servable normal form for causal forecasting. ObsNODEs learn continuous-time dynamics with states reconstructible from observations, enabling outcome predic-tion under alternative treatment paths. Experiments on synthetic cancer data, semi-synthetic data based on MIMIC-IV, and real-world sepsis data show strong performance over recent sequence models. 

## 1 Introduction 

Sequential decision-making requires predicting how actions affect future outcomes under evolving observations. In medicine, this arises in dynamic treatment regimes (DTRs), where treatments are repeatedly adapted to a patientŌĆÖs longitudinal history (Murphy, 2003; Robins, 2004). A central challenge is the identifiability of causal treatment effects in continuous time. In observational data, time-dependent confounding and treatment-confounder feedback arise when covariates affect both treatment assignment and outcomes while being themselves affected by earlier treatments (Hern├Īn, Robins, 2023). Hidden confounders further complicate this problem, since the relevant disease state is only partially observed. We address this problem from a state-space perspective. We introduce ObsNODEs , a class of Neural ODE models in observable normal form, and show that observability of the latent state is a necessary condition for identifying dynamic treatment effects in latent continuous-time systems with hidden confounding. This establishes a link between the control-theoretic notion of observability and causal identifiability in continuous-time treatment models. Based on this insight, ObsNODEs support causal forecasting under discrete and continuous treatment trajectories.

Patient heterogeneity is introduced by sampling tumor growth and treatment response parameters from predefined distributions (Appendix A.3.1).

Since the IGC-Net cannot handle continuous doses, the treatments were discretized into binary variables (Appendix A.3.1). All models require at least one treatment cycle of data assimilation for accurate tumor volume prediction. For long forecast horizons doseAI achieves the lowest error, followed by ObsNODE and the IGC-Net. For short horizons ( Žä Ōēż 2 cycles) ObsNODE typically yields the most accurate predictions. Detailed RMSE values for the tumor volume at different confounding strengths ╬│ and prediction horizons Žä are reported in Table 1. Our ObsNODE framework is most closely related to CF-ODE (Brouwer et al., 2022), which pa-rameterizes a continuous-time state-space model of the form (3) using a Neural ODE. CF-ODE assumes that observations at treatment time uniquely determine the latent state, corresponding to the observability condition (Definition 3.1). However, this assumption is not structurally enforced and is difficult to verify in practice. In contrast, ObsNODE employs the observable normal form (5) ,which guarantees observability by construction and makes explicit the link between control-theoretic observability and the identifiability condition required for the causal adjustment formula (4) . More-over, CF-ODE conditions only on prior observations and does not explicitly incorporate the treatment history. Together, these limitations motivate a continuous-time state-space formulation that enforces observ-ability and enables causal outcome prediction through an explicit adjustment formula, which we develop in the ObsNODE framework. 

## 7 Conclusion and Limitations 

We introduced Observable Neural ODEs (ObsNODE), a continuous-time state-space framework for causal outcome forecasting under sequential treatments. By combining Neural ODE dynamics with an observable latent-state representation and an adjustment formula, ObsNODE enables identification of dynamic treatment effects under time-dependent confounding while allowing parts of the latent state to represent interpretable disease dynamics. Experiments on synthetic, semi-synthetic, and real-world clinical data demonstrate strong causal forecasting performance compared to recent sequence models. As with all causal models, the approach relies on assumptions about the data-generating process whose validity in real-world multimodal settings requires further study. The adjustment formula also provides a natural basis for uncertainty quantification of causal forecasts, an important direction for future methodological work.

A.2 Details on training the ObsNODE model 

The datasets were split into training, validation, and test sets and feature-wise Z-score normalization was applied using statistics computed on the training set only. The normalized dataset is subsequently used for model training. Each multivariate time series was segmented at a decision time point tc into two disjoint subsequences. The prefix segment consists of all observations prior to tc and is used to condition the recognition model. The model infers an initial state z0 for each individual trajectory using an an LSTM-based recognition network Rubanova et al. (2019). To handle missing observations, the recognition LSTM is augmented with an explicit imputation mechanism. The indicator function 

Itij =

1 : ytij is observed 

0 : ytij is missing .

indicates whether the data for the jŌłÆth component of the iŌłÆth unit at time t is observed or not, The imputation layer is defined as the transformation 

ytij = ytij ┬Ę I tij + bj ┬Ę (1 ŌłÆ I tij ) ,

where bj are learnable parameters Jong de et al. (2019); Wendland et al. (2022). This formulation replaces missing entries with trainable values that are optimized jointly with the LSTM parameters in an end-to-end fashion. The latent initial states z0 inferred by the LSTM, together with the control function at are sub-sequently passed as inputs to the Neural Differential Equation (NODE) model. The observations after the decision time point tc are used for loss computation and subsequent optimization. Model parameters are optimized using the Adam optimizer. The ObsNODE model supports both direct long-horizon forecasting and a recursive scheme, similar in spirit to ODE-RNN Rubanova et al. (2019). In the long-horizon setting, the state estimated by the LSTM recognition model is used to generate a forecast over the entire prediction window. In the recursive setting, forecasts are produced iteratively: at each step, the ODE model generates a short-term prediction, which is appended to the observation and treatment history sequence observed up to tc. Conditioned on this augmented sequence, the LSTM updates the latent state, which then serves as the initial condition for the next ODE rollout. This process repeats until the full horizon is covered, without using any ground-truth observations beyond tc. We find empirically that augmenting the sequence with intermediate predictions can improve stability for longer forecasting horizons in some settings.

Table 6: Overview of the SOFA score subscores (Wendland et al., 2024) Each column corresponds to a subscore (0ŌĆō4) reflecting the severity of dysfunction in the central nervous, cardiovascular, respiratory, coagulation, hepatic, and renal systems. Subscores of 0 indicate normal organ function as defined by the respective criteria. The dataset includes demographic information, laboratory values, vital signs, medications, and diagnoses. For our analysis we focus on sepsis patients identified using the OpenSep pipeline according to the Sepsis-3 criteria (Hofford et al., 2022). Sepsis cases were defined by an increase in the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score of at least two points within 48 hours before or 24 hours after a suspected infection. The SOFA score is used as the clinical outcome variable. It summarizes dysfunction in six organ systems (respiratory, cardiovascular, central nervous system, coagulation, liver, and kidney), each scored from 0 to 4 according to the severity of dysfunction (Vincent et al., 1996). An overview of the subscores is given in Table 6. We consider patients treated with the antibiotics vancomycin, ceftriaxone, and piperacillin-tazobactam. To assess potential treatment side effects we additionally predict laboratory indicators of organ toxicity, including creatinine (kidney injury) as well as total bilirubin and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) (liver injury). Data preprocessing follows the OptAB pipeline (Wendland et al., 2024). The cohort is restricted to ICU patients, and antibiotic treatments are encoded as a vector of three binary variables indicating administration of vancomycin, ceftriaxone, and piperacillin-tazobactam. Time-dependent covariates were selected from all available dynamic variables by excluding variables recorded in fewer than 50% of patients and retaining those with an absolute Spearman correlation of at least 0.3 with the SOFA score during the first 12 hours. The resulting covariates are listed in Table 7 . To capture information about measurement patterns, cumulative missingness indicators were added as additional time-dependent covariates. Static covariates include sex, age, height, and weight at admission. The final dataset contains 3,556 patients. We use 80% of the data for training and 20% for testing, with the training set further split into 80% training and 20% validation data. 

Data availability: The public MIMIC-IV dataset (Johnson et al., 2023; Goldberger et al., 2000) is available under https://physionet.org/content/mimiciv/2.2/ and is shared by the data owners after reasonable request.
